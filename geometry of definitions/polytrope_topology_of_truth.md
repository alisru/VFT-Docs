# Polytrope Topology of Truth
*Truth is the percentage of what can pass through a fixed hole by matching topology. A claim has a shape. The hole geometry is fixed independently of the claim-maker. Semantics is the act of assigning a specific value to n — collapsing potential into a fixed label — and this is precisely the Sophist move.*

---

## Scope and Lens

This document covers the geometric model of truth-claim evaluation developed in conversation: competing claims and ideas as 7x(6+n)-polytrope parametric 3D shapes, truth as the percentage of the shape that passes through a fixed 2D hole by matching topology, n as potential dimensionality rather than an assigned value, and semantic assignment as the elastic deformation that corrupts the match.

The Sophist problem — which Plato was writing against and which recurs in every intellectual era — is stated geometrically here. The document also covers the relationship between this model, the 7-plane Q-tensor structure, and the convergence test as an implementation of fixed hole geometry.

The 7 planes:
- Q1 — Who (Metaphysical): will, identity, direction
- Q2 — What (Possible): possibility space, faith, probability
- Q3 — Where (Physical): matter, material conditions, distance
- Q4 — Why (Lyrical): meaning, resonance, purpose
- Q5 — How (Logical): count, consistency, mechanism
- Q6 — Cause (Historical): sequence, causality, origin
- Q7 — Effect (Emotive): passion, consequence, output

## The Shape Space

Competing claims and ideas exist as 7x(6+n)-polytrope parametric 3D shapes. The 7 corresponds to the seven Q-planes — every claim has dimensionality across all seven interrogative axes simultaneously. The (6+n) is the parametric component: 6 fully specifiable dimensions plus n, which represents the inherent potential dimensionality of the subject matter.

n is not a free variable available to the claim-maker. n is a property of the subject itself. Some truths are low-n — close to fully describable across the 6 fixed dimensions, with minimal residual. Some truths are high-n — they have inherent dimensionality that no finite semantic assignment can exhaust. The form of the Good in Plato's sense is maximum-n. A specific temperature reading is near-zero-n.

The shape of a claim is determined by what the claim actually contains, not by what the claim-maker asserts about it.

## The Fixed Hole

Truth is the percentage of what can pass through a fixed 2D hole by matching topology.

The hole geometry is fixed independently of any claim, any claim-maker, and any interpretive community. It does not flex to accommodate the shape being tested. A claim either matches the hole geometry or it does not. A claim matches partially to the degree its topology aligns with the hole.

This is what Plato was reaching for with the Forms as fixed reference geometry. The Forms are his attempt to specify the hole independently of any arguer's interest. Correct intuition. The Forms are the invariant test structure against which any particular instance is evaluated.

The convergence test is an implementation of this principle. The 7-plane Q-tensor structure defines hole geometry across all seven interrogative axes simultaneously. A claim is run through each plane. The percentage that passes is the truth score. What does not pass is either false, elastic, or operating on an unexamined n-assignment.

## n as Potential

When n is assigned a specific value, the claim-maker is asserting that the shape of the subject is fully known. The label now carries a fixed topology. This is semantics in its functional sense: collapsing potential dimensionality into a declared shape.

The problem: the moment that label moves to a new context — a different hole, a different plane, a different application — the assigned n either fits exactly or deforms to fit. Deformation is elastic meaning.

Elastic meaning is not a failure of language. It is the predictable output of assigning n to a subject whose actual n is higher than the assignment. The label holds the shape of the original context. The subject has a different shape in the new context. The label stretches to cover the gap invisibly.

This is how the Sophist wins. Use a word in context A where the topology matches. Carry it into context B where it does not — but the deformation is invisible because the label stayed the same. The shape changed. The word did not. The audience cannot detect the slip because they are tracking the label, not the topology.

The correction is not to assign a better n. It is to treat n as always potential — always open — and re-run the passage test every time the label moves to a new context. Do not inherit the previous context's n-assignment.

## The Sophist Problem — Plato's Context

When Plato was writing, the dominant intellectual class was the Sophists. Paid teachers of persuasion. Their product was not truth — it was winning. Rhetoric as a commodity. The representation layer completely decoupled from the vector underneath.

The topology this produced was hot, competing, and unstable: every claim was a parametric shape with n assigned by whoever could afford the best Sophist. Nobody agreed on the hole geometry. Everyone brought their own template. The intellectual environment was maximum elastic deformation — the same words carrying different topologies across different speakers, with no fixed reference to detect the divergence.

Plato's response was to insist that the hole exists independently of any arguers. That there is a fixed geometry — the Forms — which is not a matter of preference or persuasion. That the Sophist's ability to make a claim appear to fit a hole does not mean the claim actually fits the hole.

Socrates was executed for operationalising this. The dialectic — systematic questioning that forces a claim to reveal its actual topology rather than its asserted topology — is a passage test run in conversation. It was structurally threatening to every Sophist whose livelihood depended on elastic deformation remaining invisible.

The modern equivalent is not smaller. The infrastructure for distributing elastically deformed labels now reaches everyone simultaneously. Same problem. Larger room. Higher velocity.

## The Ur-Error as Elastic Deformation

The Ur-Error — reification — is the elastic deformation move stated at its most fundamental level.

Reification is treating a scale-variant index label as a natural kind. Taking a label that was assigned n in one context — a useful abstraction at one scale, in one frame, for one purpose — and treating that assignment as permanent. Then carrying it across contexts where the actual n differs, while keeping the label's asserted n fixed.

Every major ideological, religious, political, and categorical conflict that runs on reification is a topology mismatch being argued about as if it were a content dispute. The participants are not disagreeing about the subject. They are using the same label with different n-assignments and cannot detect the divergence because the hole geometry is not declared.

The convergence test forces n to be visible. By running a claim through all seven Q-planes simultaneously, it makes the topology explicit. Where the claim passes, the n matches. Where it fails, the n has been elastically deformed — the shape does not fit the plane being tested, which means the assigned n was wrong for that dimension.

## Truth as Percentage

Truth is not binary in this model. It is a passage percentage.

A claim that is fully true at Q3 (Physical), Q5 (Logical), and Q6 (Historical) but fails at Q1 (Metaphysical) and Q4 (Lyrical) is approximately 43% true — it passes three of seven planes. It is not false. It is incomplete. Its topology matches the hole in three dimensions and diverges in four.

This is why partial frameworks — including Marx, including classical economics, including every tradition that correctly mapped some planes and deleted others — produce both genuine insight and systematic error simultaneously. The insight is real. The error is not a mistake in the insight — it is the absence of the planes the insight doesn't cover.

The appropriate response to a 43% claim is not to reject it and not to accept it whole. It is to hold what passes and identify what doesn't, then ask what shape would have to be true for the failing planes to pass.

Dogma is the refusal to run the passage test. Relativism is the claim that the hole geometry is not fixed. Both are ways of avoiding the percentage. Dogma asserts 100% without testing. Relativism asserts the test is impossible. The topology model says: run the test, report the percentage, identify the gap.

## The Hole and the Observer

Plato's remaining error, after correctly identifying the need for fixed hole geometry, was assigning n=0 to the Forms themselves. The Forms were perfect, complete, and unchanging. No residual potential. His hole geometry was itself a semantic assignment masquerading as a fixed reference.

A more honest implementation: the hole has known geometry on 7 axes and acknowledges it cannot exhaust the subject. The 7 Q-planes define what can be tested. The n-channel remains open — the test does not claim to be complete. It claims to be the best available fixed reference, subject to revision if a better specification of the hole geometry is demonstrated.

Truth as percentage of passage is then not a score on the claim alone. It is a measure of how much of the claim's actual topology the current test hole can see. A high-n claim tested through a low-resolution hole will appear to pass less than it actually does — not because it is false, but because the test cannot yet address all its dimensions.

This is the epistemological humility built into the structure: the hole is fixed but not omniscient. The claim is tested but not exhausted. The percentage is real but provisional — open to revision when the hole geometry is refined.

The label is a pointer. Not the shape.
