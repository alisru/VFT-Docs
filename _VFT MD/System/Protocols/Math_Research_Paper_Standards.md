# Mathematics Research Paper Standards

Based on standard academic requirements for publishing formal mathematical proofs and theorem propositions, the following stylistic and structural criteria must be met:

## 1. Formal Structure
A mathematically rigorous paper must be organized logically to lead the reader from fundamental axioms up to complex applications.
- **Abstract**: A brief, concise summary of the paper's results (no storytelling).
- **Introduction**: Context, historical motivation, clear statement of results, and methodology outline.
- **Preliminaries & Notation**: Dedicated formal definitions for symbols and conventions. Must be strictly consistent.
- **Main Results (Theorems / Lemmas)**: Core propositions stated formally and precisely.
- **Rigorous Proofs**: Detailed, step-by-step logical deductions for every assertion. Separated clearly from the main text with formal start and end points (e.g., *Proof.* ... $\blacksquare$).
- **Examples / Applications**: Concrete numerical or physical applications demonstrating the sharpness and practicality of the theorems.
- **Conclusion**: Implications and summary of the formal argument.

## 2. Verbosity and Stylistic Constraints
- **Concise but Thorough**: Mathematical writing strikes a balance. It must efficiently explain logic without artificial bloating or "overthinking."
- **No Colloquialisms or Slang**: Maintain high academic rigor. Expressions like "WLOG" should be written out as "Without loss of generality."
- **Full Sentences**: Equations must be grammatically fused into complete sentences. Avoid bullet-point dumps of math without connective prose.
- **Structural Signposting**: Use Lemmas (smaller preliminary proofs) to build up to Theorems (major results). 
- **Numbered Elements**: Consistency in numbering (e.g., Theorem 3.1, Definition 3.2) is critical for cross-referencing within the paper.
