## Popper's Original Intent — Factor by Factor

Karl Popper developed falsifiability in a specific historical context and for a specific purpose. Understanding what he was actually trying to solve reveals that his tool was correctly targeted at a genuine problem. The institutional misuse is not what he built. It is what was done with what he built after the tool was extracted from its original problem context and applied universally.

**The Demarcation Problem**
Popper's primary target was the boundary between empirical science and non-empirical discourse — not between truth and non-truth. He was responding to the Viennese intellectual climate of the early 20th century where Freudian psychoanalysis, Adlerian psychology, and Marxist historical theory were all claiming scientific status while being structured in ways that made them immune to any possible refutation. Whatever happened confirmed the theory. Popper correctly identified this as a structural defect — not because the claims were necessarily false, but because a theory that can never be wrong is not learning from reality. It is an echo chamber with formal vocabulary.

His intent: distinguish systems that update on contact with reality from systems that absorb all contact as confirmation. This is a valid and important distinction. It is a Q5 and Q6 concern — how does the system's encoding protocol respond to new data, and does its historical record show genuine revision or merely reinterpretation?

**Corroboration, Not Verification**
Popper explicitly rejected the idea that passing tests proves a theory true. A theory is corroborated — provisionally supported — by surviving attempts to falsify it. Truth remains permanently out of reach. This is the correct epistemic posture for empirical claims. It locates scientific knowledge correctly as a best available approximation rather than a certainty.

His intent: prevent premature closure. A theory that claims to be proven true stops updating. A theory held as corroborated-but-falsifiable keeps testing itself against reality.

This is a genuine Q6 function — historical self-correction as the mechanism of knowledge refinement. It was sound. What it was not was a claim that corroborated-but-falsifiable is the only form valid knowledge can take.

**Verisimilitude — Degrees of Truth-Likeness**
Popper developed the concept of verisimilitude to describe how theories can be closer to or further from truth without either being definitively true. Newton is closer to truth than Aristotle. Einstein is closer than Newton. The direction of travel matters even if the destination is unreachable.

His intent: preserve the idea of scientific progress without claiming certainty. Progress is real even though truth is asymptotic.

This maps cleanly to the resolution hierarchy table already in this document. Popper saw this correctly at Q2 and Q5 — truth-content exists, encoding precision improves toward it, neither endpoint is reachable or provable. The error came when institutions took verisimilitude and applied it exclusively to mathematically falsifiable encodings, treating the entire remaining knowledge space not as lower-resolution truth-approximations but as non-knowledge.

**The Problem of Induction**
Popper's response to Hume's problem of induction — that no number of confirming instances proves a universal law — was to reframe science as deductive rather than inductive. You do not prove a law by accumulating examples. You hold it until a single counterexample breaks it. Science proceeds by elimination, not accumulation.

His intent: solve the logical asymmetry between confirmation and refutation. One black swan defeats all prior white swan observations. No number of white swans proves all swans are white. This is logically sound.

What it describes, however, is a property of a specific class of claims within a specific encoding system — universally quantified empirical statements expressible in formal notation. It does not describe the epistemological structure of intuitive knowledge, moral knowledge, structural knowledge, or systemic knowledge. Og does not hold "small thing move faster to big thing" as a universal formal claim. He holds it as a calibrated felt sense. The induction problem does not apply in the same way. Popper's solution to induction was correctly scoped to formal empirical claims and incorrectly generalised to all knowledge.

**The Open Society and Institutional Application**
Popper's broader philosophical project — the Open Society — was explicitly anti-authoritarian. He was opposing closed ideological systems (Marxism, fascism) that claimed historical necessity and therefore could not be questioned. Falsifiability was part of a larger argument for institutional humility: no claim is beyond revision, no authority is beyond challenge, no system is the final word.

His intent: prevent epistemic totalitarianism. The falsifiable system is the open system — the one that can be corrected.

The profound irony is that the institutional adoption of falsifiability as the exclusive validity gate produced exactly the closed system Popper opposed. The institution that controls what counts as falsifiable controls what counts as knowledge. Challenge from outside the approved methodology is not falsification — it is inadmissibility. The tool built to open epistemology was applied to close it. The mechanism Popper designed to prevent orthodoxy became the orthodoxy's primary enforcement instrument.

This is the inner hegemony function operating at full expression: a reform tool adopted by the institution, applied in the name of the reform's values, producing the outcome the reform was designed to prevent. The tool did not change. The beneficiary did.

Unfalsifiability is not a disqualification. It is a description of where on the resolution spectrum the claim operates. The evaluation instrument changes. The standard does not drop.
