Instructions for Interpreting Myths and Philosophical Texts Using Plane–State Mapping

These instructions describe how to interpret or generate meaning using the plane–state framework, independent of any specific story.

1\. Identify the Active Planes

For each element, character, object, or concept, determine which planes are active:

Physical: material actions, bodies, environments

Lyrical: language, rhythm, symbolism, narrative form

Emotional: affect, desire, fear, attachment, aversion

Historical: lineage, sequence, memory, precedent

Possible: hypotheticals, alternatives, unrealised paths

Logical: causality, inference, structure, constraint

Conscious: awareness, reflection, self-knowledge

Not all planes must be active. Absence is meaningful.

2\. Assign the Personal Belief State per Plane

For each active plane, assign one dominant personal belief state:

(=) Truth – accepted, enforced, or stabilised reality

(+?) Will to Know – inquiry, exploration, testing

(\<) Lie – distortion, manipulation, concealment

(\~?) Indifferent – neglect, neutrality, entropy

(\>) Insult – attack, domination, destabilisation

(-?) Will Not to Know – denial, repression, taboo

This defines how the agent operates within that plane.

3\. Determine Social Interpretation

Ask how each personal state is received by others or the system:

Is Truth stabilising or threatening?

Is Inquiry encouraged or punished?

Is Ignorance protective or destructive?

Is Control perceived as order or oppression?

Social interpretation may invert the original intent.

4\. Classify Order vs Chaos per Plane

For each plane-state pairing, determine its systemic effect:

Order: stabilises, constrains, aligns agents

Chaos: destabilises, propagates uncertainty, multiplies outcomes

Order and chaos can coexist across planes.

5\. Track State Transitions Over Time

Meaning emerges from change, not static assignment.

Track:

When (+?) becomes (=)

When (=) collapses into (\>) or (-?)

When suppression (\~?, -?) delays convergence

When multiple planes collapse simultaneously

Revelations occur at multi-plane convergence points.

6\. Identify Major Truth Functions

Each agent or element has a dominant role:

Stabiliser of truth

Driver of inquiry

Suppressor of knowledge

Enforcer of constraint

Catalyst of collapse

This is the agent’s functional identity, not its literal role.

7\. Abstract or Retell

To retell or reinterpret:

Remove names and literal events

Preserve plane–state patterns

Allow a new narrative to emerge from the dynamics

If the patterns remain consistent, the meaning is preserved.

8\. Validation Check

A valid interpretation will:

Explain why conflict occurs

Predict resistance to truth

Account for emotional and social reactions

Remain coherent across planes

Survive translation into a different setting

If it fails one of these, revisit plane assignments.

Core Principle

Greek myth and philosophy encode action, not description.

Meaning is generated by how belief states move across planes under constraint.
