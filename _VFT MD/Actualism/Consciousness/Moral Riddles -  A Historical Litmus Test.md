## Moral Riddles: A Historical Litmus Test

Moral riddles are more than just puzzles; they are diagnostic tools for the human soul. They function as "litmus tests" or even "troll tests," designed to reveal a person's underlying character, moral framework, and capacity for lateral thinking, often more than their raw intelligence. In the tradition of Joseph Campbell's "threshold guardians," these riddles test if the hero is worthy of continuing their journey. The person who defaults to basic manners, empathy, or social intelligence often solves the "real" puzzle, while the person focused only on the superficial logic reveals their own rigidity.

This concept is a fundamental pattern in storytelling, appearing in myths, legends, and modern fiction. Below are examples tracing this theme through history.

### Ancient World: The Test of Self-Awareness

#### **The Riddle of the Sphinx**

The Riddle:

Outside the city of Thebes, the Sphinx devours all travelers who cannot answer her riddle: "What goes on four feet in the morning, two feet at noon, and three feet in the evening?"

The "Litmus Test":

This is the archetypal test of Perspective and Humility. The answer, "Man," is simple, but it requires the solver to see the human condition in its entirety—from crawling baby to walking adult to the elder with a cane.

- **The "Troll Test":** The riddle is a trap for the arrogant. Those who fail are consumed because they lack the basic self-awareness to see themselves in the answer. They cannot solve a puzzle about "Man" because they don't truly understand their own nature. It tests whether you approach a problem with abstract logic alone or with an understanding of your place within the world.

### Arthurian & Medieval Legend: The Test of Integrity

#### **Sir Gawain and the Green Knight**

The Riddle/Trial:

A mysterious Green Knight issues a challenge: a knight may strike him with an axe, on the condition that the Green Knight may return the blow in a year and a day. Sir Gawain accepts and beheads the knight, who then picks up his head and tells Gawain to seek him out.

The "Litmus Test":

This is a profound test of Integrity, Honor, and Honesty. The true test is not the final axe blow, but everything that leads up to it, specifically the temptations at Lord Bertilak's castle. Gawain's small failure—accepting and hiding a protective girdle out of fear—is what is ultimately judged. The quest for the Holy Grail functions similarly, as it is entirely a test of spiritual purity, not martial prowess.

- **The Verdict:** The Green Knight only nicks Gawain's neck, symbolizing his minor failure of integrity. It's a litmus test for whether your code of ethics is absolute or situational.

### Folklore & Foundational Logic: The Test of Deception

#### **The Two Guards, Two Doors Riddle**

The Riddle:

One door leads to heaven, the other to hell. One guard always tells the truth, the other always lies. You may ask one question to one guard to find the door to heaven.

The "Litmus Test":

This is a test of Social Intelligence vs. Abstract Logic. The classic logical solutions assume the liar is a simple robot who must invert the truth. A more insightful approach recognizes that a true liar's goal is to deceive, not just be logically false.

- **The "Manners" Solution:** The "smarter" approach bypasses logic entirely. You introduce yourself by name and ask the guards to greet you in return. The truth-teller is bound to use your correct name; the liar, in their nature to deceive, will likely use a false one. You then ask the identified truth-teller the direct question.

- **The "Troll Test":** The riddle traps those who believe complex logic can solve human problems. It rewards the person who uses basic social etiquette, revealing that the true path is often found by testing character, not by trying to outsmart a liar on their own terms.

### Modern Philosophy & Science Fiction: The Test of Frameworks

#### **Psychological & Philosophical Tests**

- **The Heinz Dilemma:** A man must decide whether to steal an overpriced drug to save his dying wife. This tests an individual's stage of **Moral Reasoning**. The justification ("stealing is illegal" vs. "a life is worth more") reveals whether their framework is rule-based, outcome-based, or systems-based (seeking to change the situation itself).

- **The Trolley Problem:** A runaway trolley will kill five people. You can pull a lever to divert it, killing one person instead. This is a raw test of **Utilitarianism (consequences) vs. Deontology (actions)**. The variant where you must physically push someone to stop the trolley reveals the gap between intellectual ethics and a visceral aversion to direct violence.

#### **Game Theory**

- **The Prisoner's Dilemma:** Two criminals must decide whether to betray each other or stay silent, with varying prison sentences. This is a foundational test of **Trust, Cooperation, and Risk Assessment**. Betraying is the safest individual choice, but mutual silence is the best overall outcome. The choice reveals a mindset of cynical self-preservation versus one capable of trust for a collective good. Other models like the "Stag Hunt" further explore this dynamic between individual risk and group reward.

#### **Science Fiction Scenarios**

- **The Voight-Kampff Test (*Blade Runner*):** A series of questions designed to provoke an empathetic emotional response, used to distinguish humans from replicants. It is the ultimate litmus test for the presence of a soul, suggesting that the core of humanity lies in our capacity for empathy.

- **The Kobayashi Maru (*Star Trek*):** A no-win training scenario for Starfleet cadets. The test isn't about winning, but about revealing how an individual confronts certain failure and death. Captain Kirk's solution—to cheat by reprogramming the test—is itself a character reveal, showing a mindset that refuses to accept the terms of a losing game.
