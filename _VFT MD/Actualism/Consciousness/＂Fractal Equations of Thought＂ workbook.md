### Introduction

This workbook provides practical examples for using the analytical formulas laid out in the "Fractal Equations of Thought" document. The goal is to demonstrate how to use these coherence checks as a real-world application of Occam's Razor to test the validity of a claim or the nature of a situation.

We will deconstruct four scenarios, covering each of the primary analytical variants.

### Example 1: Testing an Intent (The Why_test)

This example demonstrates how to use the analytical toolkit to deconstruct a "Why," testing a politician's stated intent.

**Scenario:** A politician proposes a new bill called the "Economic Patriotism Act." The stated purpose is to provide significant tax cuts to large domestic corporations to encourage them to create jobs within the country.

- **The Proposed Why (Why_test):** To create jobs and strengthen the nation for everyone.

**Step 1: Identify the Known Variables**

- **Where_known (Context):** The political system is one where corporations have significant lobbying power and are legally obligated to maximize shareholder profit.

- **What_known (Substance):** The core of the bill is a direct financial benefit (tax cut) given to these corporations.

- **How_known (Process):** We apply Occam's Razor and assume the simplest, most direct process: corporations will use the extra money to maximize profit (e.g., stock buybacks, executive bonuses).

**Step 2: Choose and Apply the Analytical Check**

We use a coherence check from the "Deconstructing a 'Why' (Intent)" section: Where_known == Why_test / (What_known \* How_known)

**Step 3: Run the Logic Gate**

The formula asks: "Is the known context of reality consistent with this proposed intent?"

1.  **Calculate the "Required Where":** For the intent ("create jobs") to be the direct result of giving corporations more money which they will use to maximize profit, we would need to be in a **Where** where maximizing shareholder profit *is the same thing* as maximizing domestic job creation.

2.  **Compare:** This "Required Where" is not our Where_known (the real world).

3.  **Result:** The equation does not balance.

**Conclusion (Step 3): Logic Gate Returns FALSE**

The Proposed Why is inconsistent with the known reality. Occam's Razor suggests the intent is a deception.

**Step 4: Analyze the Evidentiary Requirement**

Since the logic gate returned FALSE, we analyze the evidence required to make it TRUE. For the Proposed Why to be true, it would require a hidden, more complex How (e.g., intricate, legally binding requirements forcing job creation).

- **The Evidence:** The proponent of the bill must provide extraordinary evidence for this hidden How.

- **The Contradiction:** The claim is that the How is simple (a tax cut), but for the Why to be true, the How must be complex. The claim is **contradictory**, and therefore cannot be backed by evidence.

### Example 2: Testing a Context (The Where_test)

This example tests how a situation is being framed.

**Scenario:** A friend with a history of poor planning asks for "emergency" help on a project.

- **The Proposed Where (Where_test):** A collaborative partnership facing a shared emergency.

**Step 1: Identify the Known Variables**

- **What_known (Substance):** Your skilled labor; their benefit.

- **How_known (Process):** You will do the work, solving their problem.

- **Why_known (Intent):** To get their project completed.

**Step 2: Choose and Apply the Analytical Check**

From the "Deconstructing a 'Where' (Manifestation)" section: What_known == Where_test / (How_known \* Why_known)

**Step 3: Run the Logic Gate**

This asks: "Is the substance of this interaction consistent with how it's being framed?"

1.  **Calculate the "Required What":** In a true partnership (Where_test), the substance would be **shared labor and shared benefit.**

2.  **Compare:** This "Required What" is not our What_known (your labor, their benefit).

3.  **Result:** The equation does not balance.

**Conclusion (Step 3): Logic Gate Returns FALSE**

The framing is deceptive. The situation is not a partnership.

**Step 4: Analyze the Evidentiary Requirement**

For the situation to be a true partnership (Where_test), the What would need to change.

- **The Evidence:** The friend would need to provide evidence of shared benefit (e.g., offering immediate, concrete compensation or a reciprocal exchange of valuable labor).

- **The Contradiction:** The claim is one of partnership, but the substance is one of a one-sided transaction. The claim is **contradictory** and lacks evidence.

### Example 3: Testing a Substance (The What_test)

This example tests a company's claim about its product.

**Scenario:** A company markets a new, affordable "eco-friendly" water bottle as being made from a revolutionary, 100% plant-based, biodegradable plastic.

- **The Proposed What (What_test):** A revolutionary, easily biodegradable material.

**Step 1: Identify the Known Variables**

- **Where_known (Context):** A market where genuine, highly biodegradable bioplastics are known to be technologically complex and expensive.

- **How_known (Process):** The company is using standard, low-cost manufacturing techniques.

- **Why_known (Intent):** To capture the eco-conscious market and increase profit.

**Step 2: Choose and Apply the Analytical Check**

From the "Deconstructing a 'What' (Substance)" section: Where_known == What_test / (How_known \* Why_known)

**Step 3: Run the Logic Gate**

This asks: "Is our world consistent with the existence of this claimed substance?"

1.  **Calculate the "Required Where":** For a *truly* revolutionary material (What_test) to be produced with cheap, standard methods (How_known), you would need a **Where** where a scientific and economic miracle has occurred.

2.  **Compare:** This "Required Where" is not our Where_known.

3.  **Result:** The equation does not balance.

**Conclusion (Step 3): Logic Gate Returns FALSE**

The claim about the product's substance is inconsistent with the known context.

**Step 4: Analyze the Evidentiary Requirement**

This is where the distinction between "contradictory" and "visionary" becomes crucial.

- **The Evidence:** For the claim to be true, the company must provide **visionary evidence**. They must present the new scientific papers, the patents for the new manufacturing process, and the data that proves a fundamental breakthrough has occurred.

- **The Possibility:** Unlike the previous examples, this claim is not inherently contradictory. It is **visionary**. It is *possible* that such a breakthrough has been made. The logic gate returns FALSE based on *current* knowns, but the proponent has a clear path to prove it TRUE by providing evidence that changes the Where_known. If they cannot provide this extraordinary evidence, the claim is a deception (greenwashing).

### Example 4: Testing a Process (The How_test)

This example tests a claim about a method.

**Scenario:** A guru sells a course promising to make you a millionaire in 30 days with a "secret, guaranteed method."

- **The Proposed How (How_test):** A secret, guaranteed, rapid method of wealth generation.

**Step 1: Identify the Known Variables**

- **Where_known (Context):** The real-world economy, where no such guaranteed get-rich-quick schemes exist.

- **What_known (Substance):** Standard online course materials.

- **Why_known (Intent):** To generate revenue for the guru.

**Step 2: Choose and Apply the Analytical Check**

From the "Deconstructing a 'How' (Process)" section: Where_known == How_test / (What_known \* Why_known)

**Step 3: Run the Logic Gate**

This asks: "Is our world consistent with a reality where this process works?"

1.  **Calculate the "Required Where":** For a secret, guaranteed method (How_test) to work for everyone, you would need a **Where** where the fundamental laws of economics are suspended.

2.  **Compare:** This "Required Where" is not our Where_known.

3.  **Result:** The equation does not balance.

**Conclusion (Step 3): Logic Gate Returns FALSE**

The claimed process is inconsistent with known reality.

**Step 4: Analyze the Evidentiary Requirement**

- **The Evidence:** For the guru's claim to be true, they would need to provide evidence that they can alter the fundamental laws of economics.

- **The Contradiction:** The claim is that a simple process (How) can produce an extraordinary result. This is a **contradictory** claim. The process is guaranteed to work for the guru (by selling courses), but its guarantee for the customer is based on a reality that does not exist. The claim cannot be backed by evidence.
