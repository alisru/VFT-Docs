# The Collision Event of Propositions, Knowledge, and Observations in an Infinite Relative Frame

In an infinite relative frame, absolute coordinates do not exist. A singular point suspended in a void possesses no intrinsic location, trajectory, or scale. Its existence as quantifiable data is entirely dependent on the arbitrary interaction frame we construct around it. To determine the absolute truth of an event - defined here as a collision between Propositions, Knowledge, and Observations - we must redefine the geometry of the interaction space itself.

## 1. The Non-Standard Relative Circle Definition

Standard geometry defines a circle or sphere by anchoring a center point and radiating outward to an arbitrary edge. In this relativistic informational model, the bounding geometry is defined strictly by the relationship between two specific points (Point A and Point B).

The interaction space does not exist prior to the relationship between these two points. It is manifested by their relative distance.

Let the vector distance between Point A and Point B be defined as D.

Rather than placing a static boundary around Point A, we define the bounds of the interaction space strictly by the relative frame between A and B. To encapsulate the full directionality for both particles - accounting for motion toward each other, away from each other, and the contextual space behind them - we double the bounds.

If the midpoint between A and B is M, the standard radius to touch both particles is D/2. By doubling the bounds, the radius of the interaction space becomes exactly D.

This non-standard relative circle (or sphere) places Point A and Point B perfectly between the center and the absolute edge of the interaction volume. Both particles possess an equal volume of \"forward\" space (the distance between them) and \"rearward\" space (the expanded boundary behind them). This constructs an arbitrary, perfectly symmetrical bounding arena specifically fit for calculation purposes.

## 2. Bounding Polytropes: The Shape of Knowledge

Once the interaction arena is defined by the doubled bounds, we must define the particles themselves. A particle in this system is a Proposition (a stated claim or vector of intent).

However, a Proposition cannot interact directly; it must be processed through Knowledge (the context). We represent this by touching the particle to the extents of an arbitrary bounding shape - a square, a circle, or a complex polytrope.

This bounding shape is the knowledge.context\[\] array. It defines the limits of what the particle can observe and how it interacts with the relative frame.

- A restrictive bounding shape (a tight square) represents a rigid, low-resolution context.

- A perfectly circular bounding shape represents a frictionless, symmetrical context.

To determine if two Propositions will interact, we extend their bounding shapes temporally. We extrude the 2D or 3D polytrope along its velocity vector over time, transforming the shape into a swept volume (a Minkowski Worldtube).

This is where the collision check occurs:

1.  **The Cull Phase (\'Throwing it out\'):** If the temporally extended volumes of Point A and Point B do not intersect within the doubled bounds of the interaction space, the calculation is immediately thrown out. There is no shared Truth, and therefore no observation required.

2.  **The Collision Event:** If the extended volumes intersect, we have located the coordinate of potential.

The collision itself is the Observation. When the extended Proposition A intersects with the extended Proposition B, the abstract vectors collapse into a physical, invariant reality.

This fulfills the absolute truth condition: \_\_\_ == knowledge.context\[\] == observations. The raw reality (\_\_\_), processed through the bounding shapes (knowledge.context\[\]), results in a shared intersection coordinate (observations) that both particles must mathematically agree upon, regardless of their individual velocities.
