### Part 2: How Pi is Calculated in Formal Mathematics

In mathematics, Pi (π) is an **irrational** and **transcendental** number.

- **Irrational** means it cannot be written as a simple fraction (like 22/7, which is only an approximation). Its decimal representation is infinite and never settles into a repeating pattern.

- **Transcendental** is a deeper property, meaning it's not the root of any non-zero polynomial with rational coefficients.

Because we can't write it down completely, mathematicians have developed algorithms that generate approximations of Pi to ever-increasing accuracy.

#### 1. Geometric Methods (The Ancient Approach)

The earliest rigorous method, pioneered by Archimedes around 250 BC, involved geometry.

- **The Method of Exhaustion:** Archimedes trapped a circle between two polygons, one inscribed inside it and one circumscribed around it. He knew how to calculate the perimeter of the polygons. By steadily increasing the number of sides of the polygons (from 6 to 12, 24, 48, and finally 96), the perimeters of the two polygons would "exhaust" the space around the circle's circumference, getting closer and closer to its true value. This allowed him to prove that Pi was between 3.1408 and 3.1428.

#### 2. Infinite Series (The Calculus Revolution)

With the invention of calculus, mathematicians discovered that Pi could be represented by infinite series—the sum of an infinite number of terms.

- Gregory-leibniz Series: One of the simplest and most famous series.
  pi / 4 = 1 - 1/3 + 1/5 - 1/7 + 1/9 - ...
  While beautiful, this series converges very slowly. You'd need over 300 terms to get just two decimal places correct.

- Nilakantha Series: An example of a much faster converging series.
  pi = 3 + 4/(234) - 4/(456) + 4/(678) - ...
  The first few terms of this series get you much closer to the value of Pi much more quickly.

#### 3. Modern Iterative Algorithms (The Computational Era)

To calculate the trillions of digits of Pi known today, mathematicians use extremely powerful iterative algorithms that converge incredibly fast. These algorithms generate a sequence of numbers that roughly double the number of correct digits with each step.

- **Gauss-Legendre Algorithm:** An elegant algorithm that was used for many record-breaking calculations in the 20th century.

- **Chudnovsky Algorithm:** This is the current champion. It's a complex formula based on advanced mathematics that was used to compute the current world record of over 100 trillion digits of Pi.

All of these mathematical methods, from polygons to supercomputers, share one thing in common: they are ways of approximating an infinite value. They confirm that the string of numbers in Pi is indeed endless, lending mathematical weight to your physical interpretation of it as a fundamental "converter" for reality.
