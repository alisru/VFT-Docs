**Phonosomatic Limerick Translations**

*Maintaining Rhyme Kingdom Fidelity Across Languages*

This document explores the challenge of translating poetry not merely at the semantic level, but at the somatic-geometric level. Each limerick presents an original English poem with its phonetic kingdom map, followed by translations into multiple languages. The goal of each translation is to preserve --- or document the loss of --- the specific phonetic kingdoms (Liquid, Nasal, Plosive, Fricative) that carry the poem\'s physical meaning in the body of the speaker.

Phonetic Kingdoms: LIQUID (L · R · W · Y) --- continuous unobstructed flow \| NASAL (M · N · NG) --- internal resonance, closed container \| PLOSIVE (K · G · T · D · P · B) --- sudden stop and release \| FRICATIVE (H · F · S · SH · V) --- continuous audible friction

**Limerick 1 --- The Cat**

*Theme: gravity, boundaries, disappearance*

**English Original**

> *There once was a cat on a wall,*
>
> *Who suffered a terrible fall.*
>
> *He slipped on the stone,*
>
> *And fell with a groan,*
>
> *And landed on nothing at all.*

Rhyme Map --- A (**wall / fall / all**): **\[LIQUID (L·R·W·Y)\]** B (**stone / groan**): **\[NASAL (M·N·NG)\]**

A-rhymes terminate in Liquid-L: smooth, unobstructed landing in the vocal tract. B-rhymes terminate in Nasal-N: internal resonance, the closed container of the ribcage.

*Theme: the void, the threshold, the final darkness*

> *A man at the edge of the dark*

> *Could not find a flame or a spark.*
>
> *He breathed in the night,*
>
> *And swallowed the light,*

**Conclusion: The Ecology of Rhyme**

A translator preserving only semantic content produces a dead poem. A translator preserving phonosomatic kingdom fidelity produces a living one --- even if the words themselves are not the most natural choices. The translations above demonstrate a spectrum from perfect kingdom preservation (German\'s wall/fall/Schall; Afrikaans\'s straat/traktaat) to near-total ecological migration (Japanese, where the syllabic open-vowel structure prevents any terminal consonant kingdom whatsoever).

The four phonetic kingdoms behave exactly as biological species: they thrive in some linguistic environments and cannot survive in others. Arabic adds a fifth kingdom (pharyngeal Fricative) unavailable in English. Hungarian adds vowel harmony as a lateral dimension. Latin uses inflectional morphology as a rhyme engine. Tibetan rhymes through the verb suffix alone.

No language is phonosomatically impoverished --- each has evolved the exact acoustic architecture necessary to encode its speakers\' physical encounter with their environment. Translation is ecological migration. The poem survives only if its phonosomatic skeleton can generate lift in the new atmosphere it has entered.
