## 2. The Historical Anatomy of the Strategy of Tension

To understand the present "preservation" of radical assets, one must first dissect the historical architecture that established this precedent. The "Strategy of Tension" (*strategia della tensione*) is not a conspiracy theory; it is a documented methodology of statecraft, most visibly deployed during the "Years of Lead" (*Anni di Piombo*) in Italy (late 1960s to early 1980s). This period serves as the "ur-text" for understanding how Western security services utilize right-wing extremism as a prophylactic against left-wing political ascendancy.

### 2.1 Operation Gladio and the "Stay-Behind" Logic

The foundational structure for this strategy was "Operation Gladio," a clandestine network organized by the CIA, MI6, and NATO intelligence services across Western Europe. Originally conceived as a "stay-behind" force to lead guerrilla resistance in the event of a Soviet invasion, Gladio’s infrastructure—arms caches, safe houses, and communication networks—was rapidly repurposed for domestic political control.^1^

The recruitment profile for Gladio operatives favored virulent anti-communists, which inevitably meant drawing from the ranks of former fascists and neo-Nazi groups. In Italy, this included members of *Ordine Nuovo* and *Avanguardia Nazionale*. The logic was cold but pragmatic: in the fight against a potential communist takeover (whether by Soviet tanks or the ballot box), the fascist was the most reliable foot soldier. However, the "stay-behind" armies did not stay behind; they became active participants in the internal politics of the nation.^1^

The "Strategy of Tension" relied on the deliberate manufacturing of violence to create a climate of fear. This fear was designed to "sow panic, blame the left, and provoke an authoritarian coup" or, at the very least, consolidate public support for the centrist Christian Democracy party against the rising Italian Communist Party (PCI).^2^ The Piazza Fontana bombing of December 1969, which killed 17 people, was initially blamed on anarchists. Subsequent investigations, however, pointed to neo-fascist groups linked to the state security services (SID).^2^

### 2.2 The Mechanism of the "False Flag"

The utility of the neo-fascist asset in this context relies on "plausible deniability." The state cannot be seen to bomb its own citizens. Therefore, it requires a "cut-out"—an intermediary who performs the act, ostensibly for their own ideological reasons, while the state facilitates the logistics or ensures the investigation is botched.

Vincenzo Vinciguerra, a neo-fascist terrorist convicted for the 1972 Peteano bombing, provided testimony that strips away the veneer of "rogue actors." He confessed that the attacks were part of an anti-communist strategy originating "not with organizations deviant from the institutions of power, but from the state itself, and specifically from within the ambit of the state's relations within the Atlantic Alliance".^3^ Vinciguerra’s testimony is crucial because it elucidates the "pact" between the state and the extremist. The neo-fascists were "left alone" by the security apparatus—allowed to organize, arm, and plot—because they were serving the state's strategic interest in destabilizing the political left.

The explosive used in the Peteano attack was identified as C-4, originating from a NATO arms cache.^3^ This physical evidence corroborates the theoretical model: the state provides the *means* (explosives, immunity), and the radical asset provides the *motive* (ideological hatred) and the *execution*. The resulting terror serves the *strategic end* (authoritarian stabilization).

### 2.3 The Evolution of the "Deep State" Network

The involvement of the *Propaganda Due* (P2) Masonic lodge further illustrates the depth of this "managed chaos." ### 3.1 The National Socialist Underground (NSU)

For over a decade (1998–2011), the NSU operated as a clandestine terrorist cell, murdering ten people (mostly of Turkish origin), bombing migrant communities in Cologne, and robbing banks to fund their operations.^4^ Throughout this period, the group remained "undetected" by the German authorities. The police consistently attributed the murders to organized crime within the Turkish community (the so-called "Döner Murders"), ignoring obvious signs of a far-right motive.^5^

However, the eventual "discovery" of the NSU revealed that the German domestic intelligence agency, the *Bundesamt für Verfassungsschutz* (BfV), had heavily infiltrated the neo-Nazi scene surrounding the trio.

The "V-Mann" System:

The BfV relies on "Confidential Informants" (Vertrauensleute or V-Männer) within extremist groups. In the case of the Thuringian Home Guard (the neo-Nazi organization from which the NSU emerged), the density of informants was staggering.

- **Funding the Enemy:** The state paid substantial sums to these informants. Tino Brandt, a high-ranking neo-Nazi and BfV informant, received over 200,000 Deutschmarks, which he admitted funneling directly into building the neo-Nazi organization.^4^ In effect, the German taxpayer was subsidizing the recruitment and arming of the very terrorists the state claimed to be fighting.

- **Proximity to Violence:** One BfV official, Andreas Temme, was present at the internet café in Kassel where Halit Yozgat was murdered by the NSU in 2006. Temme claimed not to have seen or heard anything, a claim widely regarded with skepticism given the small size of the venue.^4^

The Shredding of Files:

Perhaps the most damning evidence of "asset preservation" occurred on November 11, 2011, days after the NSU was publicly exposed. A BfV official (codenamed "Lothar L.") ordered the destruction of files related to the operation "Rennsteig," which covered the recruitment of informants in the Thuringian neo-Nazi scene.4 The destruction of these files suggests a deliberate attempt to conceal the extent to which the NSU was "managed" or "monitored" by the state. The files likely contained evidence that the state had actionable intelligence on the group's location and activities but chose not to intervene.
