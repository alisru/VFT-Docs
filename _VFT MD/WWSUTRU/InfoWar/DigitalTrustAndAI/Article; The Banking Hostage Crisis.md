# ANALYSIS: The Big Four's Big Bet: Why Your Rent Pays for Their Risk

**By Alethekanon** \| *Finance & Strategy*

**SYDNEY** — In the theatre of Australian politics, the housing crisis is a play with many actors. We have the greedy landlord, the desperate renter, the helpless politician, and the unwanted immigrant.

But if you look behind the curtain, you will see the director of the play sitting quietly in the dark. He isn't saying a word, yet every line is written for his benefit.

That director is the **Banking Sector**.

To understand why the Australian government refuses to let house prices fall—even at the cost of social cohesion—you must first understand that Australian banks are not really "banks" in the traditional sense. They are, effectively, giant hedge funds that have bet the entire national economy on a single asset class: **Residential Real Estate.**

### The "Hedge Fund" Reality

In a healthy economy, banks lend to businesses to buy machines, hire workers, and create productivity. In Australia, that model is dead.

Today, roughly **60% to 70%** of the loan books of the "Big Four" (CBA, Westpac, NAB, ANZ) are tied up in residential mortgages. They have ceased to be engines of business growth and have become engines of asset inflation.

This concentration of risk is terrifying. If you ran a hedge fund and told your investors, *"I have put 70% of our capital into one specific type of asset in one specific country,"* you would be fired for recklessness. Yet, this is the business model of the Australian banking system.

### The Insolvency Trigger

This is where the "Hostage Situation" begins. Banks operate on leverage. For every \$1 of their own money they hold in equity, they lend out roughly \$15 to \$20.

This leverage works beautifully on the way up. But on the way down, it is lethal.

If the value of the collateral (your house) drops by just **20%**, it doesn't just hurt the bank's profits. Because of their extreme leverage, a 20% drop in asset values can technically wipe out their equity.

In plain English: **If house prices return to "affordable" levels, the Big Four banks become insolvent.**

### The Government as the "Insurance Policy"

This creates a perverse dynamic. The Australian Federal Government knows that if the Big Four fail, the country fails. Credit freezes, ATMs stop working, and the currency collapses.

Therefore, the primary, unwritten directive of Australian economic policy is: **House prices must never fall.**

Everything else—your rent, your commute, your wage growth, your social stability—is secondary to this directive.

This explains the "mysterious" policy decisions that seem to defy logic:

- **Why allow record immigration during a housing shortage?** Because 500,000 new people creates a floor under housing demand, ensuring prices (the bank's collateral) stay high.

- **Why protect Negative Gearing?** Because it keeps investors buying, keeping the collateral liquid.

- **Why give grants to first home buyers?** To feed fresh capital into the bottom of the pyramid to prop up the top.

The government acts as the "Insurance Policy" for the banks. But the premiums for this insurance are not paid by the banks. **They are paid by you.**

### Privatizing Gains, Socializing Losses

We are witnessing the ultimate form of "Moral Hazard."

When housing booms, the banks keep the profits (billions in interest). But when housing threatens to bust, the *pain* is socialized.

The pain is distributed to the renter sleeping in a tent. It is distributed to the commuter stuck in traffic. It is distributed to the young family delayed in having children.

These are the **"Social Bailouts"** we pay every day to keep the banks solvent. The government effectively says: *"We are willing to break the social contract of the nation to save the balance sheet of the Commonwealth Bank."*

### The Feudal Exit Strategy

The banks are not stupid. They know this Ponzi scheme has a limit. You cannot squeeze the renter forever.

This is why we are seeing the shift toward **"Build-to-Rent"** and corporate ownership. The banks would much rather lend to a single, stable corporation (like Blackrock or a Super Fund) that owns 500 apartments than to 500 struggling individuals who might lose their jobs.

The "Feudal" future is the bank's preferred de-risking strategy. It moves the risk from the volatile public to the stable corporate sector, cementing a permanent rental class that provides a steady, unbreakable yield for the lender.

### The Verdict

We do not have a housing policy; we have a **Bank Solvency Policy**.

Until we force the banking sector to de-couple from residential real estate—perhaps by imposing higher risk-weighting on mortgages or breaking up the Big Four—we will never solve the crisis.

We are not fighting for a home. We are functioning as the collateral damage in a frantic attempt to keep the ledger balanced. The question is: How much more social destruction are we willing to absorb to keep the Big Four in the black?
