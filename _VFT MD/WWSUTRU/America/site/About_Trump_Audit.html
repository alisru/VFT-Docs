About the American Kanon

The Mythological Substrate: Understanding the rulebook of American national identity.

"America is the nation that keeps trying to become itself. These are the ideals we all truly hold in the nation's heart of hearts, that drive the nation regardless of—and despite—the pessimistic and selfish people trying to slow the nation down. No matter how far we err or stray, these are the ideals that we keep returning to: what we define to be Truly Right and Good."

Literal Level The Nation

The national character keeps correcting back toward these values despite corruption, greed, and historical errors. The scandals (Slavery, Jim Crow, The Gilded Age) prove the ideal existed—because you can only betray a promise.

Meta Level The Kanon Project

The methodology itself is an attempt to identify and map those gravitational wells—the "Truly Right and Good" that the national rhetoric keeps invoking. The 343 vectors are the mathematical attempt to locate the attractor in the national vector space.

1. The Core Insight: The Rulebook of the Mythology

The Kanon is not a description of America as it is (Sociology), nor strictly as it should be (Utopia). It is the Mythological Substrate—the aspirational ghost that politicians, poets, and soldiers have conjured for 240+ years when they say "this is what it means to be an American."

It captures the values that have been performed, weaponized, and sentimentalized in American rhetoric, regardless of whether they were truly enacted. This is not cynicism; it is the recognition that ideals have power precisely because they can shame failure.

3. The Methodology: The 343-Vector Protocol

The American Kanon is structured using the 7×7×7 Protocol, a recursive framework that yields 343 unique value-vectors. This is not arbitrary; it maps the 7 fundamental planes of existence onto national identity.

The 7 Planes

  • 1. Identity (Who): The self-image of the nation.
  • 2. Definition (What): The constitutional structure.
  • 3. Land (Where): The relationship to place.
  • 4. Drive (Why): The motivations and aspirations.
  • 5. Method (How): The operational style.
  • 6. Foundation (Cause): The historical origins.
  • 7. Result (Effect): The outcomes and impacts.

The Recursive Structure

Each Plane is analyzed through the lens of all 7 Planes, creating 49 "Senses" per Plane. Each Sense is then supported by 7 quotes, yielding:

7 × 7 × 7 = 343

Unique Value-Vectors

Each vector is sourced from historical quotes, constitutional clauses, or cultural artifacts, creating a verifiable "Audit Trail" for the national identity.

A Note on Representation: The Historical Tally

"America is a promise that has yet to be fully kept."

The Kanon aims to record the nation as its conceptual founders and influential figures collectively desired it to be. It is a map of aspirations. But for better and worse, the recent history has been dominated by paradox.

We do not dismiss the virtues of the founding project because of its sins, nor do we dismiss its sins because of its virtues. The ideals of liberty were penned by men who owned slaves; the wealth of the nation was built on stolen land.

We need a relative metric—a Tally of Reality—that acknowledges both the good and the bad. This creates a solid floor from which we can all collectively build. The hypocrisy you hear in the early planes is part of that tally; it measures the work required to bridge the gap between rhetoric and reality.

If you find this hypocrisy confronting, good. Use it to build a better future.

Use this matrix to demand that the nation live up to its own creed.

4. What It Feels Like To Live Here

The Absolute Frontier: A Continent of Scale

America is defined by sheer, overwhelming scale. From the dense urban metropolis of New York to the infinite expanse of the Great Plains and the towering Rockies, it is a geography that demands ambition.

This geography shaped the culture. You cannot be complacent in a land built entirely on movement, conquest, and extraction. You are expected to reinvent yourself, to move west, to find a new market.

"The frontier is the line of most rapid and effective Americanization. The wilderness masters the colonist." — Frederick Jackson Turner, 1893

The Insight: The American myth relies on the idea of endless space. There is always a place to start over, always a new frontier to exploit. This breeds restless ambition.
