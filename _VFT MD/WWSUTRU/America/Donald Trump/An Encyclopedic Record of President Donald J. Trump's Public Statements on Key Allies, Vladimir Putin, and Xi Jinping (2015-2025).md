# An Encyclopedic Record of President Donald J. Trump's Public Statements on Key Allies, Vladimir Putin, and Xi Jinping (2015-2025)

## Introduction

This report provides a comprehensive, chronologically organized record of public statements made by Donald J. Trump concerning the United States' key international relationships. The scope of this document extends from his first presidential campaign (circa 2015-2016) through his second term in office (ending circa 2025), as documented in the available source material. The statements compiled herein are categorized by the relevant nation, alliance, or individual leader, including the North Atlantic Treaty Organization (NATO), Germany, Japan, South Korea, Canada, Russian President Vladimir Putin, and Chinese President Xi Jinping. The objective is to present an unembellished, factual repository of President Trump's declared foreign policy positions and personal assessments, intended to serve as a primary source reference for research, policy analysis, and historical documentation. Each entry is dated and sourced to ensure fidelity and verifiability.

## Part I: Statements Concerning U.S. Alliances and Allies

President Trump's approach to U.S. alliances was consistently framed by his "America First" foreign policy, a doctrine that prioritized bilateral relations over multinational agreements and re-evaluated long-standing commitments through a transactional lens.^1^ This perspective frequently led to public criticism of allies for what he characterized as insufficient financial contributions to collective defense and unfair trade practices. Throughout his tenure, security guarantees were often presented not as sacred commitments but as services for which the United States should be compensated, a recurring theme that reshaped diplomatic discourse with both European and Asian partners.^2^

### The North Atlantic Treaty Organization (NATO)

President Trump's statements on NATO reveal a multi-year effort to fundamentally alter the perceived nature of the alliance. His rhetoric consistently moved away from the traditional conception of a mutual defense pact toward that of a fee-for-service security arrangement, where U.S. protection was conditional upon members meeting specific financial targets.

- **March 21, 2016:** During his first presidential campaign, Trump established his foundational critique of the alliance, describing NATO as "obsolete" and "costing us a fortune." He argued that the financial burden on the United States was disproportionate and that the "distribution of costs has to be changed".^5^

- **February 6, 2017:** Shortly after taking office, President Trump moderated his tone, stating, "We strongly support NATO." However, this support was immediately qualified with the demand that "all NATO members make their full and proper financial contribution to the NATO alliance, which many of them have not been doing".^5^

- **March 18, 2017:** Trump specifically targeted Germany, tweeting that it "owes vast sums of money to NATO & the United States must be paid more for the powerful, and very expensive, defense it provides".^6^ This statement explicitly linked the U.S. military presence in Europe to direct financial compensation.

- **July 13, 2018:** At a NATO summit, Trump falsely claimed the U.S. was paying "probably 90 percent of the costs of NATO" and took credit for securing new spending commitments from allies. This narrative was disputed by leaders such as German Chancellor Angela Merkel, who noted that the decision to increase defense spending had been made in 2014 and that the "turning point has long been initiated".^7^

- **December 4, 2019:** The dynamic of Trump pressing for funds and NATO leaders crediting him for the results was evident during a meeting with Secretary General Jens Stoltenberg, who affirmed that Trump's leadership was having a "real impact" on allied spending.^2^

- **February 10, 2024:** During a campaign rally, Trump recounted a conversation with the leader of an unnamed NATO country, illustrating the ultimate expression of his transactional view of the alliance. He claimed to have told the leader, "'You didn't pay? You're delinquent?... No I would not protect you. In fact, I would encourage them to do whatever the hell they want. You gotta pay!'".^2^ This statement, which made the collective defense commitment of Article 5 conditional on meeting spending targets, was characterized by the Biden White House as "appalling and unhinged".^2^

- **June 26, 2025:** The White House announced what it termed a "monumental victory" at a NATO summit, where allies agreed to increase their annual defense investment target from 2% of GDP to 5% of GDP by 2035. This was presented as a "diplomatic masterstroke" and the culmination of Trump's sustained pressure on the alliance to ensure "no more free rides" for Europe.^9^

- **October 22, 2025:** In a meeting with NATO Secretary General Mark Rutte, Trump recounted his first-term efforts, claiming he raised "hundreds of billions of dollars" by threatening allies. He described his approach as direct: "I just said, 'We're not going to be involved with you if you're not going to pay.'" He further stated, "No, I won’t protect if you’re not paying. If you’re delinquent or if the money isn’t paid, why would we do that?".^11^ He estimated the total influx of funds during this period was "probably close to \$600 billion".^11^

This consistent use of commercial language—"pay their bills," "delinquent," "reimbursed," "owed"—was a deliberate rhetorical strategy. It reframed the 2% spending guideline not as an internal investment goal for each member's own military, but as a "bill" owed to the United States for services rendered.^8^ This framing was a necessary precondition for his threat to withhold protection, transforming a potential treaty violation into what could be portrayed as a justifiable business consequence for non-payment. While this approach proved effective in securing increased financial commitments, it also eroded foundational trust within the alliance, forcing allies to question the reliability of the U.S. security guarantee and prompting fears of American abandonment.^13^

### Germany

Throughout both his campaigns and presidencies, Trump consistently singled out Germany as the principal example of a wealthy U.S. ally failing to meet its obligations. His criticisms were twofold, focusing on Germany's low defense spending and its economic policies, particularly its reliance on Russian energy. This targeting of the European Union's largest economy served to personify his broader critique of the entire transatlantic relationship, creating a powerful symbol of "European free-loading" to rally domestic support and pressure other allies.^13^

- **March 18, 2017:** Following his first meeting with Chancellor Angela Merkel, Trump tweeted, "Germany owes vast sums of money to NATO & the United States must be paid more for the powerful, and very expensive, defense it provides to Germany!".^6^ This directly established his view of the U.S.-Germany security relationship as a financial transaction in which Germany was in arrears.

- **2018:** At the U.N. General Assembly, Trump publicly rebuked Germany for its energy policy, stating, "Germany will become totally dependent on Russian energy if it does not immediately change course." He warned that Russia was using energy as a weapon.^13^ This comment, which drew a visible reaction of skepticism from the German delegation at the time, was later cited by his supporters as a prescient warning following Russia's 2022 full-scale invasion of Ukraine.^13^

- **June 2025:** Following the June 2025 NATO summit where a new 5% spending target was established, Trump continued to press Germany on defense, arguing that because the U.S. contributes significant resources to protect the country, Germany should increase its defense spending to 5% of GDP.^13^ This sustained pressure, combined with the geopolitical reality of the war in Ukraine, was credited as the "Trump effect" that compelled Berlin to finally meet the 2% NATO spending goal in 2024 for the first time since the end of the Cold War in 1991.^13^

The narrative constructed around Germany was that it was not only failing to pay for its own defense but was also actively funding a U.S. adversary (Russia) through gas purchases while simultaneously benefiting from a trade surplus with the United States. This made Germany a uniquely effective target in the "America First" framework and was used to justify a more demanding posture toward the entire continent.^6^

### Japan and South Korea

President Trump's approach to the key U.S. alliances in Asia mirrored his policy toward NATO, characterized by a transactional focus on the cost of stationing U.S. troops and a direct linkage between security commitments and economic concessions. This led to contentious renegotiations of both defense cost-sharing agreements and bilateral trade deals.

- **March 30, 2016:** As a candidate, Trump voiced his foundational complaint, stating, "We spend a fortune on defending South Korea," and questioning why Seoul was not reimbursing the U.S. for the cost. He issued a stark warning about the potential for U.S. withdrawal: "If we have to walk, we have to walk".^15^

- **April 2017:** As president, Trump described the U.S.-Korea Free Trade Agreement (KORUS) as a "Hillary Clinton disaster, a deal that should've never been made," and initiated a process to "either terminate or negotiate" the pact.^16^

- **September 24, 2018:** Trump and South Korean President Moon Jae-in signed a renegotiated KORUS agreement. The amended deal included key concessions from South Korea, such as doubling the quota for U.S. auto exports that could enter its market under U.S. safety standards and extending U.S. tariffs on Korean-made trucks from 2021 to 2041.^18^

- **2019:** During his first term, Trump demanded a five-fold increase in South Korea's annual payments for the upkeep of U.S. Forces Korea (USFK), seeking to raise the contribution from approximately \$1 billion to \$5 billion. The negotiations stalled and caused significant friction in the alliance.^15^

- **February 7, 2025:** In his second term, Trump praised Japan for its commitment to "double its defense spending by 2027" and underscored the U.S.'s "iron-clad commitment to Japan's security".^23^

- **June 23, 2025:** Reports emerged that the Trump administration was pressing Japan to further increase its defense spending to 5% of GDP, aligning with the new NATO target. Trump was quoted describing the U.S.-Japan Security Treaty as unequal, stating, "We have to protect them \[Japan\], but they don't have to protect us".^24^

- **July 30, 2025:** Trump announced a "Full and Complete Trade Deal" with South Korea. The deal imposed a 15% tariff on South Korean goods—down from a threatened 25%—while securing a pledge from Seoul for "\$350 Billion Dollars for Investments owned and controlled by the United States, and selected by myself".^26^

- **September 26, 2025:** Trump announced that South Korea's \$350 billion pledge would follow a recent commitment from Japan to provide "\$550 billion" in "upfront" investments as part of a new trade deal. He celebrated these large foreign investments as direct evidence that his tariff-driven trade policy was working to bring funds into the U.S. economy.^28^

This evolution from demanding higher host-nation support payments to securing massive, direct capital investments into the U.S. economy represents the ultimate transactional alliance model. The "cost" of the U.S. security umbrella was no longer just about offsetting the expense of an overseas military presence; it became about making direct contributions to Trump's domestic economic agenda. This "pay-to-play" model placed significant strain on allied economies and risked undermining trilateral security cooperation in favor of bilateral deal-making.^28^

### Canada

President Trump's public statements regarding Canada were overwhelmingly focused on bilateral trade, which he characterized as inherently competitive and unfair to the United States. His primary objective was the renegotiation of the North American Free Trade Agreement (NAFTA), which was replaced by the United States–Mexico–Canada Agreement (USMCA) during his first term.^1^

- **October 7, 2025:** In a meeting with Canadian Prime Minister Mark Carney, Trump described the U.S.-Canada economic relationship as one of "natural conflict." He explained this by citing competition in key industries, stating, "The problem is that they want a car company, and I want a car company... They want steel, and we want steel".^31^

- **October 7, 2025:** He asserted that American interests were harmed by automotive companies establishing operations across the border, a practice that he said placed the two neighbors "in competition".^31^

- **October 7, 2025:** Despite imposing a 35% levy on many Canadian imports, with sector-specific tariffs of 50% on metals and 25% on automobiles, Trump expressed a positive outlook on negotiations. He stated that Canada would ultimately be "very happy" with a new deal and professed a personal affection for the country, saying, "I love Canada and the people of Canada".^31^

### Other Allies

President Trump's transactional approach was also evident in his dealings with other allies, where relationships were often defined by specific deals or disputes.

- **Australia (October 2025):** Trump and Australian Prime Minister Anthony Albanese signed an \$8.5 billion critical-minerals deal. Trump boasted about the agreement, which was aimed at countering China's dominance in rare earth materials, stating, "In about a year from now we'll have so much critical mineral and rare earth that you won't know what to do with them".^33^

- **Colombia (October 19, 2025):** Trump's relationship with Colombia became strained over counternarcotics policy. After Colombian President Gustavo Petro criticized Trump's approach, Trump called Petro an "illegal drug dealer" and vowed to end aid payments. The Colombian Foreign Ministry described Trump's rhetoric as a "direct threat to national sovereignty by proposing an illegal intervention in Colombian territory".^34^

- **Argentina (October 2025):** The Trump administration authorized a \$20 billion currency swap to stabilize the Argentine peso under the leadership of his ideological ally, President Javier Milei. Trump acknowledged the bailout was contingent on a favorable outcome for Milei's faction in upcoming elections, telling reporters, "We don't have to do it. It's not going to make a big difference for our country... Our approvals are somewhat subject to who wins the election".^35^

## Part II: Statements Concerning President Vladimir Putin and the Russian Federation

### Early Statements (Pre-Presidency)

Even before his first presidential campaign, Donald Trump's public comments about Vladimir Putin were characterized by a degree of admiration for his leadership style.

- **November 2013:** In connection with the Miss Universe pageant in Moscow, Trump tweeted, "Do you think Putin will be going to The Miss Universe Pageant in November in Moscow - if so, will he become my new best friend?" Later that month, he claimed to have a "relationship" with Putin.

- **February 2014:** On *Fox and Friends*, Trump praised Putin, stating, "When I went to Russia with the Miss Universe pageant, \[Putin\] contacted me and was so nice." He added that Russian leaders were "outsmarting us at many turns".

- **March 2014:** In an interview with NBC, Trump taunted then-President Obama, saying, "Putin has eaten Obama's lunch, therefore our lunch, for a long period of time".

- **April 2014:** Following Russia's annexation of Crimea, Trump remarked that Putin was "absolutely having a great time" and that "Russia is like, I mean they're really hot stuff".

- **July 2015:** Trump speculated that if he were president, Putin would hand over NSA whistleblower Edward Snowden because Putin "hates Obama. He doesn't respect Obama".

- **November 2015:** Defending Putin from accusations that he authorized the assassination of journalists, Trump said, "He's running his country and at least he's a leader, unlike what we have in this country".

### Statements During First and Second Presidencies

President Trump's statements regarding Russian President Vladimir Putin were marked by a consistent personal admiration for his leadership style, which often stood in contrast to the official U.S. policy toward Moscow. This dynamic became particularly pronounced during his second term as he attempted to personally broker an end to the Russia-Ukraine war. His approach revealed a fundamental tension between his belief in the efficacy of personal, high-stakes diplomacy and the intractable geopolitical realities of the conflict.

- **July 16, 2018:** At a summit in Helsinki, Trump appeared to accept Putin's denial of Russian interference in the 2016 U.S. election over the findings of his own intelligence agencies, stating, "I have great confidence in my intelligence people, but I will tell you that President Putin was extremely strong and powerful in his denial today".

- **February 2022:** Shortly after Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine, Trump praised Putin's strategy. He told an audience, "reporters asked me if I thought President Putin was smart. I said 'Of course he's smart.'" He contrasted Putin's perceived strength with the leadership of NATO and the West, stating, "the real problem is that our leaders are dumb".^45^

- **February 16, 2024:** During a campaign rally, Trump touted a recent comment from Putin, who had stated he would prefer Joe Biden as U.S. president because he was "more experienced and more predictable".^2^

- **May 19, 2025:** After a two-hour phone call with Putin, Trump issued a highly optimistic statement, announcing that Russia and Ukraine would "immediately start negotiations toward a Ceasefire and, more importantly, an END to the War." He described the "tone and spirit of the conversation" as "excellent" and noted that Russia "wants to do largescale TRADE with the United States when this catastrophic 'bloodbath' is over".^36^

- **August 15, 2025:** Trump held his first second-term summit with Putin at Joint Base Elmendorf-Richardson in Alaska. The meeting was intended as a "feel out" session to gauge Putin's ideas for ending the war.^37^ Following the meeting, Trump accepted Putin's position that negotiations should proceed directly to a final peace agreement rather than beginning with a ceasefire, a decision that allowed Russia to intensify its military attacks.^38^

- **October 16, 2025:** A surprise phone call from Putin appeared to shift Trump's policy calculus once again. Just before a scheduled meeting with Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelenskyy, where the provision of long-range Tomahawk missiles was to be discussed, Putin warned Trump that supplying such weapons would cause "substantial damage" to U.S.-Russia relations. Following the call, Trump backed away from the missile discussion.^46^

- **October 17, 2025:** During a tense White House meeting, Trump reportedly pressured President Zelenskyy to make significant territorial concessions, including ceding the eastern Donbas region, to secure a peace deal. He warned that Putin would "destroy" Ukraine if a deal was not struck.^38^ Publicly, Trump called for a ceasefire along the existing front lines, a proposal Zelenskyy accepted as a starting point for talks.^41^

- **October 19, 2025:** In a subsequent interview with Fox News, Trump made his clearest statement yet on the necessity of Ukrainian concessions. He said he expected Russia would "take something" in a final deal, adding, "I mean, he's won certain property".^42^ This marked a significant reversal from his position just weeks earlier, when he had suggested Ukraine could regain all its lost territory.^42^

- **October 21-22, 2025:** After Russia publicly rejected his proposal for a ceasefire on the current battle lines, Trump abruptly canceled a planned follow-up summit with Putin in Budapest. He explained his decision by stating, "I don't want to have a wasted meeting. I don't want to have a waste of time, so I'll see what happens".^38^

The rapid oscillation in Trump's proposed peace terms demonstrates a reactive diplomatic style, heavily influenced by his most recent conversation. The cancellation of the Budapest summit marked a crucial moment where his belief in his personal deal-making ability collided with Putin's unwillingness to grant him a quick diplomatic victory on terms that deviated from Moscow's core objectives. The expressed frustration over a "wasted meeting" was a tacit admission that his preferred method of high-stakes, personality-driven negotiation had failed to break the geopolitical impasse.^38^

## Part III: Statements Concerning President Xi Jinping and the People's Republic of China

### Early Statements (Pre-Presidency)

Donald Trump's public statements on China long before his presidency were consistently critical, focusing on trade imbalances and what he viewed as unfair economic practices.

- **2011:** Trump began using strong language to describe the U.S.-China trade relationship, claiming "China is raping this country." He also stated that China's currency manipulation made it "almost impossible for our companies to compete with Chinese companies".^4^

- **November 2012:** In a tweet, Trump claimed, "The concept of global warming was created by and for the Chinese in order to make U.S. manufacturing non-competitive".^4^

- **2015:** Criticizing the practice of hosting formal state dinners for Chinese leaders, Trump said, "Just take them to McDonald's and go back to the negotiating table".^4^

### Statements During First and Second Presidencies

President Trump's policy and rhetoric toward the People's Republic of China were defined by a strategic duality. He cultivated an image as the most aggressive U.S. president on China's economic practices, launching a multi-year trade war, while simultaneously emphasizing his strong personal relationship with President Xi Jinping as the essential channel for resolving disputes. This approach was further complicated by the COVID-19 pandemic, which Trump used to intensify political pressure on Beijing.

#### Trade and Economic Policy

The centerpiece of Trump's China policy was the trade war initiated in 2018, aimed at correcting what he described as decades of unfair trade practices, intellectual property theft, and a massive trade deficit.

- **March 22, 2018:** The Trump administration officially launched the trade war, announcing plans to impose tariffs on up to \$60 billion worth of Chinese imports as a countermeasure to China's "unfair trade practices".^50^

- **July 6, 2018:** The first major round of tariffs took effect, with the U.S. imposing a 25% tax on \$34 billion of Chinese goods. China immediately retaliated with its own tariffs on U.S. products, including soybeans.^50^

- **May 10, 2019:** After trade talks stalled, Trump intensified the conflict by raising tariffs from 10% to 25% on \$200 billion worth of Chinese goods.^50^

- **January 15, 2020:** The U.S. and China signed the "Phase One" trade agreement. The deal involved a partial reduction of U.S. tariffs in exchange for a commitment from China to purchase an additional \$200 billion in American goods and services over two years and to strengthen intellectual property protections.^50^

- **May-September 2020:** In a series of speeches, Trump articulated his administration's formal position, stating that while "The United States wants an open and constructive relationship with China," achieving it "requires us to vigorously defend our national interests" against the conduct of the Chinese Communist Party.^52^

- **September 19, 2025:** Following a phone call with Xi Jinping, Trump expressed a conciliatory tone, stating that the "U.S.-China relationship is the most important bilateral relationship" and that the U.S. "would like to have a long-term, big and great relationship with China".^53^

- **October 2025:** The trade war dramatically re-escalated. Trump threatened to impose tariffs of up to 155% on Chinese goods by November 1 if a comprehensive trade deal was not reached.^54^ He cited China's new export controls on rare earth minerals—a move he called "an extraordinarily aggressive position on trade"—as the primary justification for the threatened tariff hike.^55^

- **October 2025:** Despite the extreme tariff threats, Trump simultaneously expressed optimism about an upcoming meeting with Xi in South Korea. He maintained the duality of his approach, stating, "I get along great with him I think we're going to be fine with China, but we have to have a fair deal".^59^ He also suggested that tariffs could be reduced if Beijing agreed to specific concessions, including resuming purchases of U.S. soybeans and cracking down on the export of fentanyl precursors.^60^

This dual strategy—employing the "stick" of massive tariffs to create economic pressure while offering the "carrot" of a personal deal with Xi—created enormous uncertainty for global markets and businesses.^3^ It positioned Trump as the sole indispensable figure capable of managing the volatile relationship, a tactic that forced China to develop its own retaliatory "toolkit" of economic countermeasures, thereby institutionalizing the conflict for the future.^58^

#### The COVID-19 Pandemic

The outbreak of the COVID-19 pandemic introduced a new and highly contentious dimension to U.S.-China relations. President Trump's rhetoric shifted from initial praise for Beijing's response to assigning direct blame for the global crisis, using terminology that was widely criticized as racist and stigmatizing.

- **January 24, 2020:** In the early days of the outbreak, Trump praised Beijing's handling of the virus, tweeting, "China has been working very hard to contain the Coronavirus. The United States greatly appreciates their efforts and transparency. It will all work out well. In particular, on behalf of the American People, I want to thank President Xi!".^63^

- **March 16, 2020:** Trump's rhetoric shifted sharply when he first used the term "Chinese Virus" in a tweet. Research later linked this tweet to a significant increase in the use of anti-Asian hashtags on the social media platform.^64^

- **May 30, 2020:** In official remarks, Trump formally held the Chinese Communist Party accountable for the pandemic, stating, "China's cover-up of the Wuhan virus allowed the disease to spread all over the world, instigating a global pandemic that has cost more than 100,000 American lives".^52^

- **June 20, 2020:** At a campaign rally in Tulsa, Oklahoma, Trump used the term "kung flu" to describe the coronavirus. He defended his use of such terms, saying, "It has more names than any disease in history. I can name kung flu. I can name 19 different versions of names".^66^ Despite criticism that the language was racist and endangered Asian Americans, he continued to use it.^68^

The political framing of the pandemic as a direct result of the Chinese state's failure or malfeasance served to bolster the "tough negotiator" persona. It provided another justification for punitive economic measures and linked the global public health crisis directly to the ongoing economic and geopolitical conflict with Beijing.

## Part IV: Statements Concerning Other Individuals

President Trump's foreign policy is often characterized by his personal relationships with world leaders. His public statements frequently reflect a preference for strong, authoritarian figures and a transactional view of diplomacy, where personal rapport can, in his view, override traditional alliances and geopolitical norms.

### UK Leaders

Trump's interactions with successive UK leaders have been marked by public commentary on their leadership, Brexit, and domestic policies.

- **Theresa May:** Trump's relationship with former Prime Minister Theresa May was frequently strained. He publicly undermined her, bullied and humiliated her, and interfered in the UK's domestic politics.^69^ In a July 2018 interview, he stated that May had ignored his advice on how to negotiate Brexit and that her proposed deal would likely "kill" any future free trade agreement with the United States.^69^ He also offered her a "brutal" suggestion for dealing with the EU, which he acknowledged she might find "a little bit tough".^70^

- **Boris Johnson:** In contrast to his relationship with May, Trump heaped praise on Boris Johnson, whom he considered a friend.^71^ He referred to Johnson as "Britain Trump," a nickname he suggested was a "good thing" because it showed that the British people wanted a leader like him.^72^ Upon Johnson becoming Prime Minister, Trump called him a "good man," who is "tough and he's smart," and predicted he would "get it done".^73^

- **Keir Starmer:** During his second state visit to the UK, Trump advised Prime Minister Keir Starmer to use the military to tackle the small boats crisis, warning that illegal migration "destroys countries from within".^74^ Despite policy disagreements on issues like the recognition of a Palestinian state, the tone of their meetings was described as warm and cordial, with Starmer referring to Trump as "my friend, our friend".^74^

### Middle Eastern Leaders

Trump has cultivated close ties with several leaders in the Middle East, particularly in the Gulf states and Israel, often based on shared strategic interests regarding Iran and economic partnerships.

- **Saudi Leadership (King Salman and Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman):** Trump has consistently offered effusive praise for the Saudi leadership. He has referred to King Salman as a "great man" and Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman (MBS) as an "incredible man" and a friend whom he has known for a long time.^79^ He has also called MBS "wise beyond his years".^81^ Trump has highlighted the strength of the U.S.-Saudi relationship, contrasting it with the "very, very strained" relations under the Obama administration, and has pointed to Saudi Arabia's large investments in the U.S. and purchases of military equipment as evidence of the strong partnership.^80^

- **Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu:** Trump has shown strong support for Benjamin Netanyahu. During an address to the Israeli Knesset, Trump made an extraordinary call for President Isaac Herzog to pardon Netanyahu, who was on trial for corruption charges.^82^ Trump dismissed the allegations, which included accepting luxury gifts, asking, "Cigars and champagne — who the hell cares about that?".^82^ He has also referred to Netanyahu as a "very popular man" because "you know how to win".^86^ Amid the Gaza ceasefire, Trump warned that Israeli forces could resume fighting "as soon as I say the word" if Hamas did not honor the deal.^87^

- **Turkish President Recep Tayyip Erdoğan:** Trump has frequently praised Turkish President Recep Tayyip Erdoğan, describing him as a "friend," a "tough cookie," and "one of the most powerful in the world".^88^ He has noted that Erdoğan is "highly respected" and that despite being "difficult," he has "never fail\[ed\]" him when asked for help.^89^ Trump has also suggested that Erdoğan, being respected by both Russia and Ukraine, could serve as a mediator to help end the war.^91^

### North Korean Leader Kim Jong Un

Trump's relationship with North Korean leader Kim Jong Un underwent a dramatic transformation, from public insults to a self-described "bromance."

- **Initial Hostility (2017):** At the outset, Trump used inflammatory language, referring to Kim as a "madman with nuclear weapons" and "little rocket man" on a "suicide mission".^92^ He threatened North Korea with "fire and fury like the world has never seen".^93^ Kim responded in kind, calling Trump a "mentally deranged US dotard" and a "sick puppy".^93^ The two also publicly sparred over the size of their respective "nuclear buttons".^93^

- **Shift to Diplomacy and "Love Letters" (2018-2019):** Following a series of historic summits in Singapore, Vietnam, and the Korean Demilitarized Zone (DMZ), Trump's rhetoric changed completely.^92^ He began praising Kim, calling him "far beyond smart" and "terrific".^96^ Most famously, Trump claimed that the two leaders "fell in love" after Kim sent him "beautiful letters".^96^ Woodward's book *Rage* revealed details from 27 of these letters, where Kim used florid language, addressing Trump as "Your Excellency" and reminiscing about their meetings as scenes from a "fantasy film".^100^ Trump, in turn, wrote to Kim that they shared a "special friendship" and that only they could resolve the issues between their countries.^100^

### Other European and North American Leaders

Trump's relationships with leaders of traditional Western allies have often been more contentious, marked by public criticism of their policies on trade, immigration, and defense spending.

- **German Chancellor Angela Merkel:** Trump's criticism of Angela Merkel was particularly harsh, especially regarding her 2015 decision on refugees. He called the policy "insane" and claimed she was "ruining Germany," predicting that "the German people are going to end up overthrowing this woman".^101^ Despite these attacks, he would occasionally offer qualified praise, calling her a "really great world leader" before immediately criticizing her "tragic mistake" on immigration.^101^ Their first meeting was marked by an awkward moment when Trump appeared to ignore her suggestion of a handshake.^102^

- **French President Emmanuel Macron:** While Trump has described his relationship with Emmanuel Macron as friendly at times, particularly after hosting him for the first state visit of his presidency and attending the Bastille Day parade in Paris, he has also used negative terms.^103^ During his first term, Trump reportedly called Macron "very, very nasty".^105^

- **Canadian Prime Minister Justin Trudeau:** Trump expressed open disdain for Justin Trudeau, calling him a "loser" and "radical left".^106^ He claimed Trudeau had "destroyed" Canada and was "killing" the country with his policies.^106^ Trump's rhetoric often included jokes about Canada becoming the "51st state" and referring to its prime minister as a "governor".^107^
