# ANALYSIS: The Big Four's Big Bet: Why Your Rent Pays for Their Risk

**By Alethekanon** \| *Finance & Strategy*

**SYDNEY** — In the theatre of Australian politics, the housing crisis is a play with many actors. We have the greedy landlord, the desperate renter, the helpless politician, and the unwanted immigrant.

But if you look behind the curtain, you will see the director of the play sitting quietly in the dark. He isn't saying a word, yet every line is written for his benefit.

That director is the **Banking Sector**.

To understand why the Australian government refuses to let house prices fall—even at the cost of social cohesion—you must first understand that Australian banks are not really "banks" in the traditional diversified sense. **Functionally, they act as giant hedge funds that have bet the entire national economy on a single asset class: Residential Real Estate.**

### The "Hedge Fund" Reality

In a healthy economy, banks lend to businesses to buy machines, hire workers, and create productivity. In Australia, that model is dead.

According to the latest **Westpac 2024 Financial Results**, the bank's exposure to housing is even higher than commonly cited. Of its \$725.6 billion loan book, **\$496.5 billion (68.4%)** is tied up in residential mortgages.

**This is not a diversified bank; it is a monoline mortgage lender with a side hustle in business banking.**

This represents a fundamental shift in the nation's DNA. We have replaced an economy based on **production** with a model best described as a *"Population Ponzi"*—a system based on consumption and debt. Because the banks no longer fund business innovation, the economy cannot grow through productivity. Instead, it must rely on importing people to fuel the mortgage engine.

The [[International Monetary Fund (IMF)]{.underline}](https://www.imf.org/en/Countries/AUS) explicitly warns in its 2024 Article IV Consultation that this "banking concentration" and "housing affordability" remain the key systemic risks to the nation.

**If you ran a hedge fund and told your investors, *"I have put nearly 70% of our capital into one specific type of asset in one specific country,"* you would be fired for recklessness. Yet, this is the business model of the Australian banking system.**

### The Insolvency Trigger

This is where the "Hostage Situation" begins. Banks operate on leverage. While they boast about "strong capital positions," the [[Westpac Pillar 3 Report (Dec 2024)]{.underline}](https://www.westpac.com.au/about-westpac/investor-centre/financial-information/regulatory-disclosures/) reveals the actual **Leverage Ratio** is just **5.11%**.

Similarly, [[NAB's 2024 Full Year Results]{.underline}](https://www.nab.com.au/about-us/shareholder-centre/financial-disclosures-and-reporting/financial-results) confirm a leverage ratio of **5.1%**.

This means for every \$5 of their own money they hold in equity, they lend out roughly **\$100**.

Banks use what is called **"amortised cost"** accounting to manage this risk. In plain English, this means they do not have to record a loss just because house prices fall. As long as the borrower keeps making payments, the bank can treat the loan as "healthy" and the asset as full value—even if the house itself is now worth less than the debt. This is the strategy of **"Extend and Pretend."**

But if a recession hits and unemployment rises, the payments stop. Suddenly, the bank is forced to admit the losses it was previously ignoring. And because the bank has only \$5 of its own money for every \$100 it has lent, it only takes a small wave of these bad loans to wipe out its entire buffer.

In plain English: **The banks are walking a tightrope with a safety net only 5% wide. The real danger is not just falling house prices, but the combination of falling prices and rising unemployment. Together, they trigger a systemic solvency crisis.**

### The Great Betrayal: Solvency Over Sovereignty

This creates a perverse dynamic. The Australian Federal Government knows that if the Big Four fail, the country fails.

Therefore, the **revealed preference** of Australian economic policy—visible in every stimulus package since 2008—is a single, unwritten directive: **House prices must never fall.**

This is not a conspiracy; it is a structural reality acknowledged by the [[Reserve Bank of Australia (RBA)]{.underline}](https://www.rba.gov.au/publications/bulletin/) in its analysis of bank funding costs. The market knows the banks have an "Implicit Guarantee"—a taxpayer-funded insurance policy that allows them to borrow cheaply because the government will never let them fail.

The tragedy is that the government has prioritized the **Solvency of the Banks** over the **Sovereignty of the Citizen.**

In the hierarchy of financial stability, **all other objectives are subordinated to this directive.** Your rent, your commute, your wage growth, your social stability—everything is secondary to the solvency of the collateral.

This explains the "mysterious" policy decisions that seem to defy logic:

- **Why allow record immigration during a housing shortage?** Because new people create a floor under housing demand, ensuring prices (the bank's collateral) stay high. The migrant is not being imported to be your neighbor; they are being imported as a **financial unit**—a battery hen designed to lay golden eggs for the landlord and the bank.

- **Why protect Negative Gearing?** Because it keeps investors buying, keeping the collateral liquid.

- **Why give grants to first home buyers?** To feed fresh capital into the bottom of the pyramid to prop up the top.

### The Feudal Exit Strategy (Build-to-Rent)

The banks are not stupid. They know this Ponzi scheme has a limit. You cannot squeeze the individual renter forever.

This is why we are seeing the shift toward **"Build-to-Rent" (BTR)**. Financiers like [[Merricks Capital]{.underline}](https://merrickscapital.com/) and [[Macquarie Bank]{.underline}](https://www.macquarie.com/au/en/perspectives.html) are explicitly framing BTR as a "de-risking" strategy.

They would much rather lend to a single, stable corporation that owns 500 apartments (providing a "higher quality" yield) than to 500 struggling individuals. It moves the risk from the volatile public to the stable corporate sector.

### The Verdict

We do not have a housing policy; we have a **Bank Solvency Policy**.

Until we force the banking sector to de-couple from residential real estate—perhaps by imposing higher risk-weighting on mortgages or breaking up the Big Four—we will never solve the crisis.

We are not fighting for a home. We are functioning as the collateral damage in a frantic attempt to keep the ledger balanced. The question is: How much more social destruction are we willing to absorb to keep the Big Four in the black?
