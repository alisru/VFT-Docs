# A Comprehensive Directory of the Australian News Media Landscape: Corporate, Regional, and Community Contacts

## Executive Summary

This report provides a comprehensive and meticulously researched directory of contact information for the Australian news media. It is structured to guide professionals in navigating a complex landscape, from national corporations to hyperlocal outlets. The Australian media environment is defined by a significant concentration of ownership, with two major commercial players, **Nine Entertainment Co.** and **News Corp Australia**, dominating the metropolitan and national print and digital markets.^1^ This duopoly is counterbalanced by two robust, government-funded public broadcasters, the

**Australian Broadcasting Corporation (ABC)** and the **Special Broadcasting Service (SBS)**, which have a mandate to provide universal and multicultural services, respectively.^2^ A third, vital pillar is the highly fragmented but deeply embedded sector of independent country press and community broadcasters, which serve as the primary source of local news for millions of Australians.^4^

Sections 2 and 3 provide extensive state-by-state directories of metropolitan and local press. Section 4 covers the community broadcasting sector, and Section 5 lists key industry associations. ## Section 1: Major National News Corporations

The Australian media landscape is characterized by a high degree of corporate concentration, where a small number of parent companies own and operate a vast portfolio of seemingly distinct news brands.^1^ Understanding these corporate structures is critical for effective strategic communication. Well-known mastheads such as

*The Sydney Morning Herald* and youth-focused digital publisher *Pedestrian* are both assets within the Nine Entertainment portfolio, while the national broadsheet *The Australian* is a flagship publication of News Corp Australia.^2^ This interconnectedness means that editorial direction, resource allocation, and overarching business strategies of individual outlets are influenced by their corporate parent. Consequently, engagement with any single brand should be considered within the context of its broader corporate family, as a story pitched to one outlet may have implications for, or be of interest to, other assets within the same network. This section provides detailed profiles of these major organizations, differentiating between corporate, media relations, and news-specific contact points.

## Section 3: Comprehensive Directory of Local and Country Press

The fabric of local news in Australia is woven by hundreds of smaller, independent, and locally-focused newspapers. This sector operates within a complex ecosystem designed for survival and influence. At its core is Country Press Australia (CPA), a national association representing over 200 regional news producers.^4^ The CPA functions not merely as a membership body but as a powerful lobbyist and a centralized point of contact for government and commercial entities, providing a "one-stop shop" for advertising and engagement.^4^ This collective structure, replicated at the state level by bodies like Country Press SA and the Queensland Country Press Association, is essential.^39^ While an individual paper may have limited clout, their unified voice allows them to lobby on critical industry issues, such as Freedom of Information laws, and to secure large-scale advertising buys that would otherwise be inaccessible.^4^ This demonstrates a crucial survival mechanism that amplifies the influence of local news. For professionals, this presents a dual strategy: for highly localized outreach, direct contact with the individual newspaper is most effective; for broader regional campaigns or policy engagement, contacting the CPA or its state affiliates can be a more efficient and impactful approach.

## Section 4: The Community and Independent Broadcasting Sector

Beyond the commercial and public service giants lies the "third sector" of Australian media: community broadcasting. This is Australia's largest independent media sector, comprising over 450 non-commercial, community-owned and operated radio and television stations.^5^ Its influence is not derived from commercial power but from deep community trust and relevance. The sector is powered by a workforce of over 22,200 volunteers and reaches more than 5 million listeners weekly.^5^ The primary motivation for this large audience is the availability of local information and news that they cannot obtain from other media sources.^5^ This hyperlocal focus is particularly vital for communities not adequately served by mainstream media, including First Nations, ethnic, regional, and other minority groups.^126^ The volunteer-driven model ensures these stations are run by the community, for the community. This dynamic necessitates a tailored engagement strategy; a generic press release is less likely to gain traction than a story pitch that directly addresses a local issue or a specific community of interest.

### 4.1 Community Broadcasting Association of Australia (CBAA)

**Overview:** The Community Broadcasting Association of Australia (CBAA) serves as the peak body and national representative organization for the more than 450 community radio and television stations across the country. It provides essential support, resources, legal advice, and advocacy to ensure a healthy and thriving environment for the sector.^5^

**Primary Contact:**

- **Phone:** (02) 9310 2999.^126^

- **Website:** www.cbaa.org.au.^127^

**Services:** The CBAA offers a wide range of capability-building services. This includes the creation and distribution of news, talk, and music programs to over 160 independent stations via the Community Radio Network, reaching 1.7 million listeners weekly. It also promotes new Australian music to community radio nationwide through the Australian Music Radio Airplay Project (Amrap) and provides stations with critical advice on matters such as broadcast law, copyright, and station development.^128^

### 4.2 Directory of Community Radio Stations

**Note on Availability:** A comprehensive, static directory of all 450+ CBAA member stations is not publicly available in a single document. Instead, the CBAA website features a "Find a Station" tool, which allows users to locate specific stations by region, name, or frequency.^129^ This tool is the most reliable method for identifying and contacting individual community radio stations.

**Recommendation:** Users seeking to contact specific community radio stations should utilize the official "Find a Station" directory on the CBAA website (cbaa.org.au) or contact the CBAA directly for guidance on reaching stations that serve particular communities of interest.

### 4.3 Key First Nations Media Outlets

**Overview:** A critical component of the community broadcasting sector is First Nations media. This network includes 28 urban and regional radio services and 8 remote media organizations, which collectively reach nearly half of the Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander population each week.^5^ These organizations are the primary providers of news and current affairs to their communities, often in First Nations languages, and play a vital role in cultural maintenance and social cohesion.^5^

**Key Organizations (as mentioned in research):**

- **Koori Mail:** A national Indigenous newspaper.

- **IndigenousX:** An online news and opinion platform.

- **ICTV (Indigenous Community Television):** A remote television service reaching 240,000 households.^5^

- **NITV (National Indigenous Television):** A national free-to-air television service, now part of the SBS network. Contact for NITV should be directed through SBS.^5^

## Conclusion

The Australian news media landscape is a study in contrasts, defined by three distinct and powerful tiers. At the apex, a highly concentrated market is dominated by two commercial behemoths, Nine Entertainment Co. and News Corp Australia, whose vast portfolios of television, print, and digital assets shape the national news agenda. This commercial duopoly is balanced by the extensive reach and public service mandates of the two government-funded broadcasters, the ABC and SBS, which provide a crucial alternative voice and serve diverse communities across the continent.

Beneath these giants lies the third and most granular tier: a resilient and deeply influential network of local and community media. The hundreds of independent newspapers that form Country Press Australia, along with the volunteer-powered community radio stations represented by the CBAA, function as the primary source of hyperlocal information for millions of Australians. Their strength lies not in corporate scale but in community trust and relevance.

For professionals engaging with this landscape, a nuanced understanding of these tiers is paramount. Effective communication requires a multi-faceted strategy: engaging with corporate headquarters for national announcements, targeting specific metropolitan news desks and editorial pages to influence state-level discourse, and building relationships with local editors and broadcasters to connect with communities on the ground. This directory provides the foundational contact information necessary to execute such a strategy, serving as an essential tool for navigating the complexities of Australian media.
