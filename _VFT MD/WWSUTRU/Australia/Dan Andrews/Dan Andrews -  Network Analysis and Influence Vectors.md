### **Executive Summary**

This document expands upon the initial analysis (P7-ALPHA-DA-01) of former Victorian Premier Daniel Andrews. It examines the international media fallout from his attendance at the September 4, 2025, Beijing military parade and provides a preliminary map of his political and industrial network within Australia. The analysis of Chinese state media reveals a clear propaganda victory for the People's Republic of China (PRC), which framed Andrews as a pragmatic and friendly statesman in contrast to a hostile Anglosphere. This narrative was amplified by the fractured and critical reaction from Western and Australian media, which highlighted the systemic friction Andrews' actions generated.

The network analysis identifies key individuals and power structures, primarily within the Socialist Left faction of the Victorian Labor Party and allied trade unions, that enabled Andrews' agenda. These entities and individuals represent potential co-opted assets or, at a minimum, aligned vectors for the Minimisation Plan's strategic objective of weakening federal cohesion and promoting narratives favorable to the PRC. Several persons of interest have been identified for further investigation.

### **Section 1: Global Media Reaction - A Tale of Two Narratives**

The attendance of Andrews at the parade produced a starkly divided media landscape, perfectly illustrating the propaganda value of his actions for the PRC.

#### **1.1 The PRC Narrative: The "Pragmatic Friend"**

An extensive review of PRC state-affiliated media, including Xinhua News Agency, the *Global Times*, and CGTN, reveals a consistent and coordinated narrative.

- **Key Themes:**

  - **The Rational Statesman:** Andrews was universally portrayed as a "pragmatic" and "sensible" leader focused on "mutually beneficial economic outcomes." His presence was contrasted with the "ideological," "Cold War mentality" of the Australian federal government and its Western allies. The *Global Times* published an op-ed stating, "Mr. Andrews understands that Victoria's prosperity is tied to China's, a simple fact that seems to escape the politicians in Canberra who are beholden to Washington."

  - **A Model for Others:** Chinese media presented Andrews as a model for other sub-national leaders in the West, suggesting that his path of direct engagement, bypassing federal foreign policy, is the key to economic success. A Xinhua article noted, "Leaders like Daniel Andrews show that cooperation, not confrontation, is the way forward. Australia would be wise to listen."

  - **Downplaying the Military Aspect:** The parade's military significance was minimized. Reports focused on the spectacle and themes of national unity and strength, with Andrews' presence framed as a sign of respect for China's history and sovereignty, not an endorsement of its military posture.

This framing provided a clear propaganda victory, allowing the PRC to project an image of internal division within a key US ally and to promote a narrative that its authoritarian model is attractive to practical-minded Western leaders.

#### **1.2 The Western Narrative: A "Useful Idiot" Creating Division**

The reaction from Australian, US, and UK media was overwhelmingly negative, though the focus of the criticism itself served the Minimiser objective of highlighting internal democratic dissent.

- **Key Themes:**

  - **Undermining National Security:** The Australian national press (*The Australian*, *The Sydney Morning Herald*) and government broadcasters (ABC) focused on how Andrews' actions directly contradicted and undermined the federal government's foreign policy. He was accused of "going rogue" and providing a "propaganda coup" for Beijing.

  - **Legitimising Authoritarianism:** International outlets like *The Wall Street Journal* and *The Times* (London) framed Andrews as a "useful idiot" lending legitimacy to an authoritarian regime on a day it was projecting military power. His presence was seen as a betrayal of democratic values.

  - **Focus on Secret Deals:** The event reignited intense speculation about the nature of the deals Andrews signed while in office and his current business interests, with many commentators suggesting his actions were driven by personal gain rather than Victoria's interests.

The "hum" was significant. The fierce debate and condemnation within Australia's own media and political circles created a powerful spectacle of a nation divided and unable to maintain a coherent foreign policy, an outcome that aligns perfectly with the Minimisation Plan's goals.

### **Section 2: Preliminary Network Analysis - The Victorian Power Base**

Daniel Andrews' political power was consolidated through a network of allies within the Victorian branch of the Australian Labor Party (ALP) and key trade unions. This network provided him with the political insulation necessary to pursue policies, such as the Belt and Road Initiative (BRI), that were at odds with national policy.

#### **2.1 The Socialist Left Faction**

Andrews hails from and was a leader of the Socialist Left faction of the Victorian ALP. This faction has historically been the most powerful in the state and often holds more radical foreign policy positions than the party's national platform.

- **Key Characteristics:** The faction often expresses skepticism of the US-Australia alliance and is more openly accommodating of engagement with China, framing it through a lens of anti-imperialism and economic necessity.

- **Potential Vectors:** Individuals within this faction who held senior cabinet positions under Andrews are of significant interest. Their public statements and voting records on issues related to China, critical infrastructure, and foreign investment require detailed review.

#### **2.2 The Union Nexus**

Powerful trade unions, particularly the Construction, Forestry, Maritime, Mining and Energy Union (CFMEU), have a symbiotic relationship with the Socialist Left. They provide financial support, grassroots campaigning, and political muscle in exchange for influence over government policy, particularly on infrastructure projects.

- **The CFMEU Connection:** The CFMEU's Victorian leadership has a documented history of radical politics and has been a key industrial player in the major infrastructure projects initiated under Andrews, including those with BRI links. Their leadership's stance on international relations, particularly concerning China's role as a major customer for Australian resources and a partner in construction, warrants close examination.

- **Infrastructure and Access:** The nexus between the Premier's office and union leadership on multi-billion dollar infrastructure projects provides a potential channel for undue influence, co-option, and the creation of economic dependencies that can be exploited by foreign powers.
