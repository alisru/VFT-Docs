# The Structural Encapturement of the Australian Consumer: A Forensic Analysis of the Corporate Duopoly, Sovereign Supply Chain Risks, and the Mechanics of Market Entrapment

## Executive Summary: The Architecture of Dominance and the Illusion of Choice

The Australian grocery retail sector, fundamentally anchored by the corporate behemoths Woolworths Group and Coles Group, represents one of the most sophisticated examples of oligopolistic market capture in the developed world. This report, posits that the existing characterization of this market structure as merely "concentrated" fails to capture the aggressive, multi-layered strategy of consumer and supply chain "encapturement" currently underway. The duopoly, which collectively controls over 82% of the market when logistics and wholesale interests are included \[15\], is not simply competing for market share; it is engaged in a systematic restructuring of the Australian food system.

This restructuring is defined by a strategic framework termed the "Minimisation Plan"—a relentless pursuit of supply chain efficiency, vertical integration, and technological enclosure—that serves a dual purpose. Ostensibly, it reduces operating costs to manage inflationary pressures. However, a forensic analysis of the evidence suggests a more mercantilist objective: the deliberate displacement of independent national brands (NBs) in favor of retailer-owned private labels (PLs). Crucially, this report investigates the allegation that this PL expansion is heavily reliant on Chinese manufacturing capabilities, effectively introducing "knockoff" products that mimic the aesthetic and functional utility of traditional family general store items. This strategy, termed here as "entrapment," leverages the "price umbrella" created by inflated national brand prices to force consumer migration toward retailer-controlled, often foreign-sourced alternatives.

The implications of this strategy extend beyond domestic competition policy into the realm of geopolitical risk. By tethering the affordability of the Australian shopping basket to centralized production hubs in China, the Majors are exposing the national food security apparatus to the vagaries of international trade tensions and "economic coercion" \[9, 10\]. While regulatory bodies like the Australian Competition and Consumer Commission (ACCC) have intensified scrutiny through the Mandatory Food and Grocery Code of Conduct (FGCC) and proposed divestiture powers \[11, 12, 13, 14\], the pace of the duopoly's structural transformation threatens to outstrip the legislative capacity to contain it. This report details the economic mechanisms of this dominance, the geopolitical vulnerabilities inherent in the sourcing strategy, and the regulatory battlefronts defining the future of Australian retail.

## 1. The Primer: Economic Framework of the Australian Grocery Duopoly

To understand the allegations of entrapment and profit accretion, one must first deconstruct the economic framework that permits such behavior. The Australian market is not a free market in the classical sense; it is a tight oligopoly characterized by high barriers to entry, significant pricing power, and a structural resistance to competitive discipline.

### 1.1. Structural Exceptionalism and Market Concentration

The level of concentration in Australia is globally anomalous. In comparable economies like the United Kingdom or the United States, a diverse array of competitors forces vigorous price competition. In contrast, the Australian landscape is dominated by two players. As of 2023, Woolworths Group commanded a 37.1% share of the grocery market, while Coles Group held 27.9% \[15\]. However, these headline figures obscure the true extent of their influence. When factoring in their control over logistics, wholesale distribution, and adjacent retail categories (such as BIG W and liquor), the effective market control of the duopoly is estimated to exceed 82% \[15\].

This dominance creates a "competitive fringe" rather than a competitive market. Rivals such as Aldi (9.5%) and the independent sector supplied by Metcash (6.9%) operate under structural disadvantages \[15\]. They lack the sheer scale required to dictate terms to multinational suppliers or to invest billions in automated logistics networks. Consequently, the duopoly faces limited competitive pressure, allowing them to set prices and service levels that prioritize shareholder returns over consumer welfare. This lack of competitive vigor is evidenced by the ACCC’s finding that Woolworths and Coles offer a "largely undifferentiated product range" and price at similar levels, a classic hallmark of oligopolistic coordination \[16\].

The structural dominance is further entrenched by the Majors' ownership of non-grocery retail entities and dedicated logistics networks. For instance, Woolworths' control over BIG W allows for cross-subsidization and shared logistics efficiencies that pure-play grocers cannot replicate \[15\]. This conglomerate structure means that a dollar spent in the Australian economy has an extraordinarily high probability of passing through the ledger of one of these two entities, regardless of whether the consumer is buying bread, petrol, alcohol, or general merchandise.

### 1.2. Financial Performance as an Indicator of Market Power

The financial results of the Majors during the recent inflationary period provide empirical validation of their market power. In a functioning competitive market, rising input costs (fuel, labor, commodities) would typically compress profit margins as retailers struggle to retain customers by absorbing some of the pain. In Australia, the opposite occurred.

During the cost-of-living crisis beginning in late 2022, Woolworths’ operating margins for its Australian food business actually *increased* from 5.1% to 5.9% over a 12-month period \[1\]. This margin expansion, occurring simultaneously with a spike in household financial distress and general inflation, indicates that the retailer was able to pass on cost increases in full, plus an additional margin accretion. This phenomenon refutes the narrative that food price inflation was solely driven by "farmgate" or logistics costs. If that were true, margins would remain flat or decline. Instead, the widening gap between wholesale costs and retail prices suggests a deliberate strategy of margin expansion facilitated by a lack of competitive alternatives \[1\].

Comparative analysis further contextualizes this profitability. Australian supermarket margins are observed to be double those of their peers in the highly competitive markets of the European Union and the United Kingdom \[2\]. In the UK, fierce competition between Tesco, Sainsbury's, Asda, Lidl, and Aldi forces retailers to operate on razor-thin margins to survive. The absence of such pressure in Australia allows Coles and Woolworths to post annual profits of \$1.1 billion and \$1.6 billion respectively \[18\], figures that are arguably excessive relative to the size of the population they serve. This data supports the hypothesis that high operating margins are a structural feature of the oligopoly rather than simply a reflection of operational excellence \[2\].

## 2. The "Minimisation Plan": Efficiency as a Weapon of Control

The user query highlights a strategy termed the "Minimisation Plan." This is not merely a cost-cutting exercise; it is a strategic doctrine designed to maximize the extraction of value from every stage of the supply chain. The plan relies on three pillars: Vertical Integration, Technological Enclosure, and Private Label Expansion.

### 2.1. Vertical Integration: Seizing the Means of Distribution

Vertical integration allows the duopoly to bypass intermediaries, thereby capturing the margin that would previously have gone to wholesalers, processors, or logistics providers. A prime example is Coles' acquisition of two automated milk processing facilities in early 2023 \[3\]. By moving upstream into processing, Coles no longer acts solely as a retailer of milk; it becomes the manufacturer.

This shift has profound implications for market dynamics:

- **Security of Supply:** It insulates the retailer from disputes with external processors, ensuring their own-brand milk is always on the shelf even during industry shortages.

- **Cost Opacity and Information Asymmetry:** It allows the retailer to internalize the true cost of production. When Coles negotiates with independent dairy brands, it now possesses exact data on the cost of processing, packaging, and transport. This creates a severe informational asymmetry, allowing the retailer to demand "improved buying terms" that squeeze the margins of independent suppliers \[21\].

- **Barrier to Entry:** It increases the capital requirements for any potential challenger. A new entrant cannot simply open a store; to compete on the price of white milk—a key "loss leader" or staple—they effectively need to build a dairy plant to match the duopoly's integrated efficiency \[3\].

Similarly, the Woolworths Group’s majority equity interest (65%) in PFD Food Services represents a strategic encroachment into the wholesale sector \[15\]. PFD is a major supplier to the hospitality industry and independent grocers. By controlling this entity, Woolworths gains visibility into the supply chains of restaurants, cafes, and arguably its own independent competitors. This integration effectively "encaptures" the broader food ecosystem, forcing suppliers to deal with the duopoly not just for retail shelf space, but for wholesale distribution across the entire food service channel \[15\].

### 2.2. Technological Enclosure: The Automated Moat

The "Minimisation Plan" is physically manifested in the massive capital investment in automated distribution centers (ADCs) and customer fulfillment centers (CFCs). Coles alone has committed over \$2 billion to such projects, including the use of Witron technology \[22\]. These facilities utilize advanced robotics to pick and pack orders, drastically reducing labor costs and improving speed.

However, the strategic value lies in "Technological Enclosure." The scale of investment required to replicate these systems (\$2 billion+) is prohibitive for smaller competitors like IGA/Metcash. This creates a permanent structural cost advantage for the duopoly. Furthermore, the integration of global trade platforms like Infor Nexus/GT Nexus \[23, 24\] is critical. These platforms streamline the importation of goods, specifically facilitating the efficient flow of high-volume, low-cost inventory from international hubs.

The adoption of Infor Nexus allows Coles to modernize its international supply chain, delivering real-time inventory visibility and significantly reducing working capital by lowering stock in transit \[23, 24\]. It also minimizes "demurrage" (fees for delays in loading/unloading) and customs clearance costs \[24\]. While presented as an efficiency measure, the strategic implication is clear: it makes the importation of private label goods from markets like China significantly more profitable, faster, and more reliable than sourcing from fragmented local suppliers who may lack such sophisticated digital integration.

### 2.3. The Private Label Pivot: From Budget to "Finest"

The ultimate objective of these efficiencies is to drive the expansion of Own-Brand (Private Label) products. The duopoly is not hiding this intent; Woolworths has explicitly stated a goal to "double the penetration of own brands" and remove "substitutable" branded products \[21\].

This strategy has evolved beyond the "generic" black-and-gold packaging of the past. Data from Circana reveals that Private Label brands now represent 36% of Australia’s CPG/FMCG sales, with a robust year-on-year growth of 4.8% \[26\]. The retailers are deploying a tiered approach:

- **Entry Level:** Basic staples designed to compete on pure price.

- **Mid-Tier:** Direct copies of national brands (the "knockoff" allegation).

- **Premium:** High-margin offerings such as "Coles Finest," which recorded a massive 20.4% increase in sales revenue in FY24 \[26\].

This diversification utilizes first-party customer data to identify exactly what attributes consumers value (e.g., "low salt," "organic," "extra crunchy") and then rapid-prototypes a PL version to capture that demand \[25, 26\]. By utilizing the cost savings from vertical integration and automation, the Majors can offer PL products at prices significantly lower than National Brands (NBs)—approximately 44% cheaper in packaged groceries \[28\]. Yet, despite the lower retail price, the gross margin for the retailer on these PL goods is higher because they do not bear the marketing or R&D costs of the NBs \[5, 26\]. This creates a perverse incentive: the retailer makes more money selling a cheaper product, provided that product is their own.

## 3. The "Entrapment" Strategy: China, Knockoffs, and the Displacement of the National Brand

This report now turns to the core allegation: that Woolworths (and Coles) are utilizing their ties to China to introduce "knockoff" products as a strategy of entrapment. This hypothesis suggests that the duopoly is actively engineering a market where consumers are forced, through pricing and availability, to abandon trusted Australian brands in favor of look-alike products manufactured in China.

### 3.1. The Mechanics of Encapturement: The "Price Umbrella" Effect

The economic engine of entrapment is the "Price Umbrella" effect. Established National Brands (e.g., a famous Australian laundry detergent, biscuit, or canned fruit) maintain a high price point due to their brand equity, domestic manufacturing costs, and marketing overheads. This high price creates an "umbrella" under which the retailer can position its Private Label alternative \[5\].

The allegation suggests the Majors engage in the following cycle:

1.  **Inflation of NB Price:** The retailer arbitrarily raises the price of the National Brand, or refuses to pass on wholesale cost reductions, citing "inflationary pressures." This expands the price gap between the NB and the potential PL substitute \[5\].

2.  **Introduction of the "Knockoff":** The retailer introduces a PL product that mimics the packaging, color scheme, and utility of the NB. This is often a "general store" staple—bleach, flour, biscuits, canned vegetables.

3.  **The Value Trap:** The consumer, facing cost-of-living pressures, sees the NB at \$8.00 and the PL "knockoff" at \$4.50. The price disparity is so great that the consumer is economically coerced into switching. The PL is not just cheaper; it is positioned as the *only* rational economic choice \[5\].

4.  **Delisting ("Rationalization"):** Once sales volume shifts to the PL, the retailer uses the declining sales of the NB as justification to remove the NB from the shelf entirely, a process euphemistically termed "rationalizing the range" to remove "substitutable" items \[21\].

5.  **Capture:** The NB is gone. The consumer is now "captured" by the PL. With the branded competitor removed, the retailer can then slowly creep the price of the PL up, as there is no longer an external reference price to anchor consumer expectations.

### 3.2. The China Connection: The "World’s Factory" as a Strategic Weapon

The feasibility of this strategy relies on the ability to source the PL product at a rock-bottom cost to maintain the deep discount required to break consumer loyalty to the NB. This is where the "ties to China" become critical. The duopoly's investment in global trade platforms (like Infor Nexus) is specifically designed to optimize "international trade" and "reduce stock in transit" \[23, 24\]. This infrastructure facilitates the friction-free importation of goods from the world's most efficient manufacturing hub: China.

The allegation of "introducing Chinese-manufactured knockoff products similar to those in family general stores" strikes at the heart of the Australian manufacturing identity. "Family general store" items are typically low-complexity, high-volume goods. Historically, these were produced domestically. However, by shifting the production of these staples to Chinese contract manufacturers, the duopoly achieves a margin that Australian suppliers cannot match.

This creates a "hollowed out" shelf. The product looks familiar—it may even have a name that sounds local (e.g., phantom brands like "Hillview Farms" or "Market Lane")—but the supply chain originates in Guangdong or Zhejiang. This is not merely importation; it is the strategic substitution of domestic industrial capacity with foreign capacity to maximize retailer margin. The "knockoff" nature of these products—copying the visual cues of the market leaders—is designed to lower the cognitive barrier for the consumer to switch, effectively tricking the shopper into accepting the substitution \[26\].

### 3.3. The Vulnerability of "General Store" Categories

The reference to "family general store" products is significant. These stores rely on a network of independent wholesalers and local manufacturers. When Woolworths and Coles introduce aggressive, China-sourced PLs in these categories, they undercut the volume required for local manufacturers to remain viable.

As local manufacturers lose volume to the duopoly's Chinese imports, their unit costs rise, making them even less competitive for the independent general stores. Eventually, the local manufacturer collapses or is bought out. The independent store loses its supplier. The consumer is left with only one option: the duopoly's Chinese import. This is the definition of "encapturement"—the systematic elimination of alternatives until the retailer's supply chain is the only one remaining. The "Minimisation Plan" essentially subsidizes this displacement strategy by ensuring the logistics of Chinese imports are more efficient than local distribution \[5\].

## 4. Geopolitical Risk and the Fragility of the "China Strategy"

While the "China Strategy" yields immediate profit maximization for Woolworths and Coles, it introduces a systemic risk that the "Minimisation Plan" fails to account for: Geopolitical volatility. The report finds that the pursuit of private label dominance has created a dangerous dependency.

### 4.1. Overconcentration and Economic Coercion

The reliance on centralized Chinese manufacturing for high-margin Private Label goods exposes the Australian food supply to what experts term "overconcentration of global production" \[10\]. Australian trade history is replete with recent examples of "economic coercion," where political tensions result in sudden trade barriers. The imposition of tariffs and bans on Australian wine, barley, coal, and lobster serves as a potent precedent \[9, 10\].

If a significant portion of the "ambient" grocery aisle (canned goods, paper products, cleaning supplies, general merchandise) is sourced from China to feed the PL strategy, a geopolitical flare-up could lead to immediate shortages. Unlike a National Brand, which might have diversified global supply chains or domestic redundancy, a retailer-specific PL contract is often tied to a single supplier to maximize volume discounts.

This "supply chain warfare" risk means that the duopoly's pursuit of profit has effectively offshored the security of the Australian pantry \[10, 34\]. The efficiency of the "Just-in-Time" models, facilitated by platforms like Infor Nexus, leaves no buffer for diplomatic friction. A trade blockade or a delay in shipping lanes due to regional conflict would leave shelves empty of the very "knockoff" staples that consumers have been entrapped into relying upon.

### 4.2. Financial Ties: The Selling of the Farm

The ties to China extend beyond the products on the shelf to the very ground the supermarkets stand on. There is a documented trend of high-value sales of Coles and Woolworths property sites to Asian investors, including both local and overseas Chinese interests \[37\]. These investors view the duopoly as "anchor tenants" providing secure, government-backed-like revenue streams due to their market dominance.

This financialization creates a complex feedback loop. Foreign capital funds the property infrastructure; the retailer pays rent to foreign landlords; the retailer fills the shelves with foreign-made goods; and the profits from the sales flow to the retailer's shareholders. The Australian community is reduced to a transactional node—a point of consumption—rather than a beneficiary of a local economic ecosystem. The "entrapment" is therefore not just of the consumer, but of the retail infrastructure itself, which becomes increasingly tethered to foreign capital flows \[37\].

## 5. The Economics of Manipulation: Pricing, Gouging, and Deception

The user query raises concerns regarding "profit gouging" and arbitrary pricing. It is essential to distinguish between what is perceived as unfair and what is legally actionable, while also highlighting the manipulative tactics used to simulate value.

### 5.1. The Legality of Profit Gouging

Under current Australian law, price gouging—defined strictly as charging a price that consumers perceive as "too high" or "excessive"—is *not* illegal \[6, 29\]. Businesses retain the general freedom to set their prices based on what the market will bear. The ACCC has explicitly stated that it cannot prosecute the Majors solely for having high operating margins or charging prices that maximize profit relative to cost \[6\].

This legislative framework creates a significant limitation: the "price umbrella" strategy described in Section 3 is technically legal. The duopoly is free to raise the price of National Brands to make their Private Labels look more attractive, provided they do not collude with each other to do so. This gap in the law allows the "Minimisation Plan" to function without immediate regulatory intervention on the pricing front, leaving consumers vulnerable to margin expansion strategies that are opaque and difficult to challenge.

### 5.2. Misleading Conduct: The "Oreo Family Pack" Case Study

While high prices are legal, lying about savings is not. The ACCC has pursued legal action specifically targeting the duopoly’s alleged manipulation of pricing claims, which directly relates to the query’s concern over arbitrary price setting used to simulate value.

The ACCC commenced proceedings against Coles and Woolworths for allegedly breaching the Australian Consumer Law (ACL) by misleading consumers through "Prices Dropped" and "Down Down" promotions \[32\]. A specific case cited involves the **Oreo Family Pack**.

- **The Baseline:** The product was regularly sold at \$3.50 for approximately **696 days** \[7, 8\].

- **The Spike:** Woolworths then allegedly raised the price to \$5.00 for a short period of **22 days** \[7, 8\].

- **The "Drop":** The product was then placed on the ‘Prices Dropped’ promotion at \$4.50, advertising a \$0.50 saving using the temporary \$5.00 price as the "was" price \[7, 8\].

This conduct reveals a systematic operational procedure (SOP) designed to manipulate consumer perception. The advertised "discounted" price of \$4.50 was, in fact, **29% higher** than the product’s long-term regular price of \$3.50 \[7\]. This is not simple price gouging; it is **false reference pricing**. It entraps the consumer by creating an illusion of a bargain where none exists. The brevity of the price spike (22 days) compared to the long-term price (696 days) suggests the hike was orchestrated solely to establish a higher "anchor" price for the subsequent promotion.

The penalties for such breaches are now significant, potentially reaching \$50 million or 30% of a company’s adjusted turnover for each breach occurring after November 2022 \[8\]. This litigation represents a critical regulatory pivot: while the ACCC cannot set prices, it is aggressively policing the *honesty* of the pricing architecture.

### 5.3. Shrinkflation and the Unit Pricing Code

A related tactic in the "entrapment" arsenal is "shrinkflation"—reducing the size of a product while maintaining its price. This is a form of hidden inflation that degrades consumer value. In response to the ACCC Inquiry, the government has committed to strengthening the **Unit Pricing Code** \[42, 43\].

The Unit Pricing Code requires supermarkets to display the price per unit (e.g., per 100g or per liter) alongside the selling price. This is a critical transparency measure that allows consumers to compare the true value of a National Brand against a Private Label "knockoff." However, inconsistencies in how this is displayed—particularly in remote areas or on digital platforms—have been noted. The ACCC has recommended mandatory requirements for pricing information in remote stores to protect vulnerable consumers from this opacity \[42\]. By improving unit price visibility, regulators hope to arm consumers with the data needed to see through the "shrinkflation" tactic, although its effectiveness against the sheer scale of the duopoly's pricing algorithms remains to be seen.

## 6. Regulatory Constraints and the Fight for Reform

The systemic market power and the resultant high profitability observed in the Australian grocery sector have prompted a major regulatory overhaul. The government and the ACCC are moving from a posture of observation to one of active intervention, utilizing both behavioral and potential structural remedies.

### 6.1. The Mandatory Food and Grocery Code of Conduct (FGCC)

Following the ACCC Inquiry, the Food and Grocery Code of Conduct (FGCC) transitioned from a voluntary framework to a mandatory code on April 1, 2025 \[11, 38\]. This is a watershed moment for supplier relations.

- **Failure of the Voluntary Code:** The voluntary code was widely regarded as a failure. Since 2021, only six disputes were raised, and zero compensation was awarded \[39\]. This low utilization was symptomatic of a "climate of fear," where suppliers were terrified of retribution (e.g., delisting) if they complained about the duopoly's conduct \[12\].

- **The New Regime:** The mandatory code applies to all grocery businesses with revenue exceeding \$5 billion \[11, 38\]. It introduces robust enforcement mechanisms. For breaches of core supplier protections, maximum civil penalties now apply up to **\$10 million**, three times the value of the benefit obtained, or 10% of the supermarket's annual turnover, whichever is greater \[12, 39\].

- **Dispute Resolution:** Crucially, the code establishes compulsory independent mediation and arbitration, with compensation capped at **\$5 million** \[12, 39\]. It also includes specific provisions to protect suppliers against retribution.

This code directly challenges the "Minimisation Plan" by making it financially risky for the duopoly to squeeze suppliers via "unconscionable conduct." If a retailer threatens to delist a supplier because they won't fund a discount on a competing Private Label product, this could now trigger a massive fine.

### 6.2. Competition Law Reform: The "Nuclear Option" of Divestiture

In addition to behavioral regulation, the Australian Parliament has considered structural reforms. The **Competition and Consumer Amendment (Divestiture Powers) Bill 2024** seeks to amend the *Competition and Consumer Act 2010* to introduce divestiture powers \[13\].

This legislation would empower courts to order the disposal of a corporation's assets if the corporation is found to have misused its substantial market power (Section 46) and if the disposal would result in a "substantial improvement to competition" \[14\].

This is the "nuclear option." It addresses the structural entrenchment described in Section 1. Existing merger laws failed to stop the Majors from acquiring assets like PFD Food Services or the automated milk plants. Divestiture powers would allow the courts to unwind these acquisitions. For example, Woolworths could theoretically be forced to sell PFD, or Coles forced to divest its liquor arm or manufacturing facilities, if these assets are proven to be used to stifle competition.

While the threshold for using such powers would be extremely high, their mere existence serves as a potent deterrent against future aggressive consolidation \[14\].

### 6.3. Transparency and Consumer Empowerment

The ACCC’s Supermarkets Inquiry also highlighted the need for improved market transparency to counteract the "undifferentiated" nature of the duopoly's offers \[16\]. The government has committed funding to consumer groups like CHOICE to provide independent information on supermarket prices \[43\]. The logic is that by reducing the "search costs" for consumers (making it easier to see who is actually cheaper), the market can force the duopoly to compete more vigorously. However, given the "Technological Enclosure" and the sheer dominance of the Majors' footprint, information alone may be insufficient without the hard power of the FGCC and potential divestiture laws.

## 7. Conclusion: The Cost of Encapturement

The comprehensive analysis of the Coles and Woolworths duopoly reveals a market structure that has evolved beyond simple dominance into a phase of active consumer and supply chain encapturement. The "Minimisation Plan" is not merely a defense against inflation; it is an offensive strategy designed to centralize control, extract maximum margin, and systematically displace independent competition through Vertical Integration and Technological Enclosure.

The allegation of utilizing Chinese ties to introduce "knockoff" products serves as the linchpin of this strategy. By leveraging the "price umbrella" of National Brands, the duopoly guides consumers toward high-margin, retailer-owned substitutes often sourced from offshore manufacturing hubs. This strategy hollows out the domestic manufacturing base, destroys the viability of independent "general store" supply chains, and introduces significant geopolitical risk into the Australian food system. The reliance on platforms like Infor Nexus to streamline Chinese imports prioritizes short-term retailer profit over long-term national supply chain resilience.

While the consumer may perceive a short-term benefit in the lower price of a Private Label commodity, the long-term cost is the erosion of choice, the degradation of sovereign manufacturing capability, and the entrenchment of a duopoly that extracts profit margins double those of international peers. The regulatory response, marked by the introduction of the Mandatory Food and Grocery Code of Conduct and the looming threat of divestiture powers, represents a critical attempt to rebalance the scales. However, the battle is asymmetric. The duopoly's financial resources, data capabilities, and entrenched physical infrastructure make them formidable opponents. The future of the Australian grocery sector depends on whether these new regulatory tools can effectively break the cycle of encapturement before the independent alternative—and the domestic manufacturing capacity that supports it—is extinguished entirely.
