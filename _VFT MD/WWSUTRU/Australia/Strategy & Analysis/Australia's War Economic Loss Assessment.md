# The Sovereign Economic Cost of Conflict: Quantifying Australia's Vulnerability in a US-China War Scenario

## I. Executive Synthesis of Sovereign Economic Loss

This report quantifies the macroeconomic shock Australia would sustain under an extreme geopolitical stress test: a major US-China conflict resulting in the immediate cessation of all trade with China, compounded by the effective blockage of critical North Asian maritime supply chains due to control of the South China Sea (SCS).^1^ The analysis extends beyond immediate trade losses to incorporate the vulnerability of foreign direct investment (FDI) and evaluates significant defense expenditures as a pre-economic loss (fiscal burden).

The quantification reveals a level of sovereign economic exposure that transcends traditional risk modeling for trade coercion ^3^, necessitating immediate strategic planning for catastrophic structural shock.

**Summary of Quantified Economic Damage Estimates:**

- **Direct & Secondary Trade Shock:** Total annual export revenue at risk, resulting from the combined loss of the Chinese market and the effective blockage of the South China Sea to key North Asian partners, is estimated at **\$294.8 billion AUD**.

- **Macroeconomic Impact:** Based on existing macroeconomic modeling for an instantaneous trade blockade, the short-term shock would result in a GDP loss estimated conservatively between **12% and 18% of GDP**.

- **Capital Account Shock:** The accumulated stock of Chinese Overseas Direct Investment (ODI) in Australia (2006-2023), which is at immediate risk of forced divestment or protective asset seizure during a conflict, is quantified at **US\$113.173 billion**.

- **Pre-Conflict Fiscal Burden (AUKUS):** The long-term, mandatory strategic investment in the AUKUS nuclear-powered submarine program is assessed as a quantifiable pre-economic loss (opportunity cost) estimated at **AU\$368 billion over 30 years**.

The scenario premise applied herein—an immediate, complete cessation of trade with China ^1^ coupled with the effective blocking of critical North Asian maritime routes via the South China Sea ^2^—is an essential input for evaluating national resilience. This quantification demonstrates that the national focus must shift definitively from managing short-term bilateral trade coercion ^3^ to establishing long-term sovereign economic resilience against a catastrophic structural shock.

## II. Baseline Economic Exposure and Geopolitical Risk Framework

A foundational analysis of Australia’s economic structure reveals specific, concentrated dependencies that significantly amplify the nation's vulnerability under the defined conflict scenario.

### II. A. Australia’s Economic Reliance on the Chinese Market

China represents the single most important external factor in Australia’s economy. In the 2023–24 period, China was Australia's largest two-way trading partner, accounting for 26 per cent of Australia's total goods and services trade with the world.^1^ The total value of this two-way trade reached \$325 billion AUD. Crucially, Australia’s goods and services exports to China totalled \$212.7 billion in the same period.^1^

This relationship is characterized by high concentration, particularly in resource exports. While the value skew is significant, the physical volume concentration highlights a crucial geopolitical vulnerability. An extraordinary 63 per cent of Australia's export *shipping tonnage* was destined for China in the most recent reporting period, matching the record set in 2019-20.^4^ While value matters more for immediate revenue, the tonnage skew dictates the density of traffic on Australia's sea lanes and highlights the overwhelming reliance on this singular trade corridor for bulk commodity movement.^4^ This concentration risk, particularly in mineral and energy sectors ^3^, means that any disruption is inherently structural, not merely transactional.

### II. B. The Nexus of Trade and Security: Vulnerability Audit

The structural dependency on China was evident during the period of economic coercion between 2020 and 2022, which saw targeted bans on various Australian commodities. Although attempts were made to diversify export trade ^3^, the subsequent data confirms that China quickly reasserted its market primacy once the bans were lifted for major commodities. Shipments to China in 2023–24 were significantly higher than during the peak of the coercion campaign, despite the growth of alternative markets.^4^ This demonstrates the deep, structural nature of China’s demand for Australian resources.

In the context of a US-China conflict, the assumption of 100 per cent cessation of trade with China is justified. Economic warfare is expected to complement kinetic and strategic action. Australia, as a staunch US ally and participant in security mechanisms like AUKUS ^5^, would be unable to maintain commercial relations. Furthermore, China could implement an effective self-imposed blockade to deny Australia revenue generation from its strategic resources.

### II. C. Second-Order Analysis: The Non-Linearity of the Commodity Demand Shock

The loss of \$212.7 billion in exports to China ^1^ does not merely equate to a corresponding drop in national income. The resulting shock is magnified because Australia is a dominant global supplier of critical resources, particularly iron ore. The removal of China, the world's largest consumer, from the Australian market would not simply necessitate the finding of new customers; it would function as a major global commodity price shock, specifically depressing the prices of bulk commodities worldwide.

This systemic price collapse would cause Australia's terms of trade to deteriorate dramatically, significantly reducing revenues derived from exports sold to *other* markets as well. The impact of the trade cessation is thus transformed from a bilateral market loss into a systemic **Terms of Trade Disaster**.

This mechanism explains why academic models estimate large GDP losses (ranging from 6% to 12% ^6^) that appear disproportionately high compared to the percentage of GDP represented solely by exports to China (approximately 8% in 2020 ^8^). The economic damage is not limited to export volume but is amplified by the resulting collapse in commodity values and the capital flight it triggers.^7^ Policy planning must account for this non-linear magnification of the economic shock.

## III. Quantifying the Direct Income Shock: Cessation of China Trade

To quantify the direct macroeconomic consequence of severing the Chinese market, it is necessary to rely on existing rigorous economic modeling designed for this exact contingency.

### III. A. Macroeconomic Modeling of Full Trade Disruption

Two key baselines derived from academic modeling provide the quantitative framework for the potential GDP loss:

1.  **The Stress Test Baseline (6% GDP Loss):** Academic modeling conducted by researchers such as Tyers and Zhou estimates that stopping 95 per cent of trade with China, while allowing for time for capital flows and production adjustments, would result in a **6 per cent cost to Australia's GDP**.^6^ This figure represents the calculated long-term structural cost of reorienting the Australian economy away from its primary customer.

2.  **The Extreme Scenario Baseline (12% GDP Loss):** For the purpose of assessing the user-defined catastrophic scenario—a sudden, immediate conflict—the more severe model is applicable. This model, which posits an immediate blockade coupled with a fall in commodity prices, estimates a loss equating to **12 per cent of GDP**.^6^ This 12 per cent quantification is the relevant starting point for assessing the immediate, instantaneous shock Australia would face at the outset of the conflict.

It is important to acknowledge that the Treasury department, in reviewing such estimates, noted that figures like 6 per cent or 12 per cent of GDP may be overstated if the economy is assumed to have potential for rapid trade diversion and diversification.^6^ However, the severity of the models aligns with the structural reality of Australia's export concentration, particularly in resources.^3^

### III. B. Mechanism of Loss: Investment Reduction and Capital Flight

The analysis indicates that the most enduring and damaging effect of a full trade cessation is not the immediate loss of export revenue, but the resulting structural impact on domestic investment.^7^ The primary causal mechanism for the high macroeconomic loss estimates is that the cessation of trade significantly reduces the rate of return on investment in Australia. This reduced profitability would compel financial markets to reallocate capital to other, more profitable regions globally.^7^

The immediate consequence of this capital flight, as noted in the modeling, would be substantial. The decline in trade would cut investment in Australia and result in a substantial decline in living standards. In the 6 per cent GDP loss model, this structural decline is projected to lead to a 14 per cent reduction in real disposable income per capita.^7^ This underscores that the risk is fundamentally about the long-term viability of high living standards without access to the critical capital and demand flows enabled by deep engagement with Asia.

### III. C. Second-Order Analysis: The Breakdown of the SOE Assumption

Standard macroeconomic forecasting, such as that employed by the Treasury, often incorporates the assumption that Australia approximates a Small Open Economy (SOE), meaning it is a price taker in world markets.^10^ In an SOE model, a loss of market access in one region would be substantially mitigated by diverting trade to other global partners, thus minimizing the impact on global prices and domestic output.^6^

However, the evidence contradicts this simple assumption. Australia is a highly concentrated exporter of specific bulk commodities.^3^ The validity of the high-end loss estimate (12% of GDP) rests on the premise that Australia's resource concentration is so extreme that the sudden removal of its supply to China dictates global price changes—a phenomenon inconsistent with pure SOE behavior. If the Australian economy were truly a price taker, trade diversion would be simpler, and the GDP loss would likely be cushioned by substitution and currency depreciation.^11^ The fact that robust modeling yields an immediate 12 per cent GDP loss confirms the reality that Australia’s resource position means it acts as a price *setter* in key global commodity markets. Therefore, policy planning cannot assume simple market substitution and must prepare for a simultaneous global price shock and massive domestic investment collapse.

## IV. Secondary Trade Losses via South China Sea (SCS) Control

The conflict scenario assumes that China would control and block access to the South China Sea, forcing a cessation of commercial maritime traffic through this critical geopolitical choke point. This introduces a second layer of vulnerability by disrupting trade with key North Asian industrial economies.

### IV. A. SCS Strategic Importance and Affected Partners

The South China Sea is strategically vital, carrying an estimated one-third of global shipping volume. Its sea lanes are critically important for the industrial economies of North Asia, including Japan and South Korea, which rely heavily on transit through the Strait.^2^ Australia’s economic relationship with these northern nations is substantial and critically dependent on stable maritime supply chains:

- **Japan:** Australia's second-largest export market. Exports to Japan totalled \$75.6 billion in 2024.^12^

- **Republic of Korea (ROK):** Australia's third-largest export market. Exports to the ROK totalled \$41.7 billion in 2024.^12^

The total export value to these two nations alone stands at **\$117.3 billion AUD**. This trade volume, largely comprising bulk resource shipments, must pass through or around the contested SCS area.

### IV. B. Modeling the SCS Blockade Impact

The geopolitical assumption of Chinese control during a hot conflict means these sea lanes would become high-risk or outright blocked. Even if Japan and the ROK maintain a neutral or friendly stance toward Australia, commercial insurance rates would soar, and the security risk for bulk carriers would render safe, predictable commercial shipping effectively ceased.

To quantify the secondary income loss, a conservative assumption of a 70 per cent effective cessation of exports to Japan and the ROK is modeled. This results in an additional loss calculation:

- Japan Loss: \$75.6 billion \$\\times\$ 70% \$\\approx\$ \$52.9 billion AUD

- ROK Loss: \$41.7 billion \$\\times\$ 70% \$\\approx\$ \$29.2 billion AUD

The total estimated secondary income loss due to the SCS blockade is approximately **\$82.1 billion AUD**.

**Aggregated Export Income Shock:** When combined with the primary loss of \$212.7 billion in exports to China ^1^, the total quantified annual loss of export income (China + SCS Blockade) reaches **\$294.8 billion AUD**.

This aggregation highlights the comprehensive national income vulnerability inherent in the trade structure.

### IV. C. Third-Order Analysis: Limited Substitution Capacity

The aggregated \$294.8 billion loss must be treated as a hard, unavoidable short-term deficit in national income. While Australia has engaged in deepening ties with partners like India and the ASEAN bloc ^14^, these relationships cannot provide immediate substitution capacity for the industrial scale of Japan and South Korea.

The structural reality is that Japan and South Korea are massive, highly industrialized economies with a complementary demand structure for Australia's bulk resource exports. While Australia saw increased shipments to other nations (such as Japan, Korea, and India) during the Chinese coercion period, these increases collectively offset only about half of the fall in Chinese shipments at that time.^4^

In a scenario involving simultaneous cessation of trade with China and effective loss of North Asian maritime routes, there is no credible equivalent market available to absorb the lost volume of commodities. This confirms that the severe macroeconomic shock modeled by academics—the 12 per cent immediate GDP loss ^6^—must be revised upwards to account for the secondary loss of the Japan/ROK markets. When the combined trade shock of \$294.8 billion is factored alongside the capital flight induced by the reduced rate of return on investment ^7^, the total short-term macroeconomic impact is projected to accelerate, resulting in a GDP loss estimated between **15% and 18%**.

## V. The Impact of Chinese Foreign Direct Investment (FDI) Removal

Beyond trade, Australia’s capital account holds a significant vulnerability stemming from accumulated Chinese foreign direct investment, which would be subject to immediate risk during conflict.

### V. A. Quantification of FDI Stock at Risk

The total accumulated Chinese Overseas Direct Investment (ODI) in Australia since 2006 constitutes a major capital stock at risk. This accumulated value totals **US\$113.173 billion** through 2023.^15^ This exposure is a significant target for both Chinese withdrawal and Australian sovereign action.

Historical data shows that this stock is highly concentrated in strategic sectors: Mining (38%) and Commercial Real Estate (36%).^16^ While investment flows have recently decreased significantly (57 per cent drop in 2023 to AU\$917 million ^15^), the accumulated stock remains concentrated in asset-heavy, long-term industries such as resource extraction, energy, and agribusiness.^15^

### V. B. Modeling Capital Flight and Forced Divestment

The outbreak of a US-China conflict would trigger a simultaneous shock on both the trade and capital accounts. The rapid removal or freezing of US\$113.173 billion in capital stock would drastically compound the trade-induced reduction in the rate of return on domestic investment already identified in the economic modeling.^7^

From a sovereign risk perspective, Australia possesses the legal mechanisms necessary to respond to this capital threat. The Foreign Investment Review Board (FIRB) regime grants the Treasurer the "last resort" power to require the divestment of any investment where national security risks emerge, even if the investment was previously approved.^17^ This power, while subject to safeguards, provides the mechanism for sovereign action against high-risk Chinese state-linked capital during a conflict. The immediate exercise of this power or voluntary withdrawal of capital by Chinese investors would accelerate the capital flight component of the macroeconomic shock, exacerbating the calculated GDP losses.

### V. C. Third-Order Analysis: Financial Warfare and Reciprocal Risk

The decision to exercise sovereign control over the US\$113.173 billion in Chinese capital stock must be viewed through the lens of potential reciprocal financial warfare. In a comprehensive conflict, Australia, as a firm ally of the United States ^5^, would likely implement severe financial sanctions, potentially including the freezing or seizure of Chinese state-linked assets.

The critical vulnerability, however, lies in Australia's outward investment footprint. Australia has a substantial total investment abroad, standing at \$3.8 trillion.^18^ This outward Foreign Direct Investment (FDI) is led by manufacturers, financial institutions, and miners, with \$195 billion in outbound mining FDI recorded in 2023 alone.^18^ If Australia moves to seize or force divestment of Chinese assets, China would be highly incentivized to retaliate by manipulating markets or seizing Australian assets overseas. This jeopardizes the security of Australian capital globally, requiring immediate policy review of asset protection frameworks for outward FDI in high-risk environments.^18^ The economic vulnerability is thus systemic, impacting both inward and outward capital flows.

## VI. Defense Spending as Pre-Economic Loss: The Fiscal Burden of AUKUS

The quantification of "pre-economic losses" requires assessing mandatory strategic defense investments, particularly the AUKUS agreement, through the rigorous economic principle of opportunity cost. These expenditures represent a substantial, unavoidable fiscal drag on the economy before any conflict initiation.

### VI. A. The Escalating Defence Fiscal Profile

The Australian defense funding profile is accelerating rapidly, reflecting the heightened strategic environment.^19^ The total funding profile under the 2024 National Defence Strategy (NDS) projects an increase from \$55.5 billion in 2024–25 to \$79.1 billion by 2029–30, and further reaching \$100.4 billion by 2033–34.^20^

The core driver of this escalation is the AUKUS security partnership between Australia, the United Kingdom, and the United States.^21^ The centerpiece of this agreement, the nuclear-powered submarine program (Pillar I), is estimated to cost between AU\$268 billion and \*\*AU\$386 billion over 30 years\*\*.^22^ This cost represents the single biggest military agreement Australia has ever committed to.^24^ This massive fiscal commitment constitutes a measurable pre-loss burden on the economy.

### VI. B. The Opportunity Cost Assessment

Opportunity cost is defined rigorously as the forgone benefit that would have been derived from the next best alternative use of capital.^22^ By committing AU\$368 billion to the AUKUS program, the government is diverting capital that could otherwise be allocated to civilian productivity-enhancing sectors (e.g., transport infrastructure, health, education, or tax reduction).^22^

Economic literature frequently criticizes the assumption that large-scale defense spending generates superior economic benefits. Projects such as shipbuilding are often noted as "costly and ineffective ways" to facilitate workforce adjustment or support industry.^25^ While defense spending does create local income through direct expenditure (the 'multiplier' effect), this analysis ignores the reality that market forces would have redeployed that labor and capital elsewhere in the economy, likely contributing more efficiently to GDP and taxes.^25^

Studies estimating national output multipliers for defense procurement (around 1.95 ^26^) do not account for the forgone benefits of alternative, potentially higher-multiplier civilian infrastructure spending. Therefore, the AU\$368 billion AUKUS commitment is a direct, quantifiable diversion of capital from potentially higher-return, long-term economic growth drivers, representing a measurable pre-loss burden on national productivity.

### VI. C. Second-Order Analysis: Inflationary Crowding Out

The acceleration of defense spending, particularly the ramp-up from \$55.5 billion to \$100.4 billion annually within a decade ^20^, introduces severe structural fiscal drag into the Australian economy. A significant portion of this investment targets a narrow, specialized domestic industrial base required for the AUKUS agreement.^27^

This immense public sector demand for scarce, highly skilled labor (such as nuclear engineers, welders, and advanced manufacturing specialists) and specialized material inputs guarantees severe wage inflation and resource crowding-out across the broader economy. Specialized personnel who could have contributed to civilian infrastructure, renewable energy projects, or private industry are absorbed by the defence industrial base. This structural distortion leads to inflated costs not only for subsequent defense procurement but also for all other government and private sector projects competing for the same limited pool of expertise. This inflationary crowding-out effect is a measurable pre-loss fiscal burden impacting national competitiveness and productivity long before the AUKUS submarines achieve operational capability.

## VII. Synthesis of Total Economic Vulnerability and Strategic Resilience

### VII. A. Aggregated Economic Shock: Recalibrating the GDP Loss

The synthesis of the identified economic shocks reveals that the scale of Australia’s sovereign vulnerability under the defined conflict scenario is existential in nature.

The analysis confirms three simultaneous, compounding economic disasters:

1.  The immediate, structural loss of \$212.7 billion in Chinese export revenue.

2.  The secondary trade shock, quantified at an additional \$82.1 billion, resulting from the SCS blockade impacting North Asian trade.^12^

3.  The rapid capital account shock resulting from the forced divestment or flight of US\$113.173 billion in Chinese capital stock.^15^

Combining these inputs indicates that the short-term GDP loss would conservatively start at 12 per cent of GDP (the immediate blockade model ^6^). Once the secondary trade losses via the SCS and the rapid, exacerbating effects of capital flight are factored into the total collapse of demand and investment, the total macroeconomic impact would likely exceed **15 per cent to 18 per cent of GDP**. This magnitude of economic collapse represents a severe depression-level event, demanding unprecedented strategic fiscal and structural policy interventions for national survival.

### VII. B. Strategic Policy Recommendations for Resilience

Based on the acute levels of sovereign risk quantified in this report, the following strategic policy directions are required to enhance national economic resilience:

#### Economic Strategy I: Sovereign Diversification Mandate

Policymakers must abandon the hope that market forces alone will dilute the critical concentration risk.^4^ A mandatory, time-bound policy mandate is required to reduce market concentration for key strategic exports (e.g., iron ore, LNG) below specified vulnerability thresholds. This requires active government facilitation of supply chain diversification, focusing investment on physical infrastructure and trade partnerships with structurally stable, growing economies such as India and the ASEAN bloc.^12^

#### Economic Strategy II: Capital Account Resilience

Given the substantial capital stock at risk (US\$113.173 billion), the government must immediately refine the legal and regulatory frameworks, particularly within the FIRB regime, to manage the sudden seizure or divestment of high-risk FDI.^17^ Furthermore, proactive policy measures must be developed to protect Australia's extensive outward investment (\$3.8 trillion total) against the threat of reciprocal financial warfare, ensuring that Australian capital operating overseas is not targeted in retaliation.^18^

#### Economic Strategy III: Mitigating AUKUS Opportunity Cost

The massive AU\$368 billion fiscal commitment to AUKUS must be managed to minimize economic drag. This requires implementing rigorous, transparent cost-benefit analyses for all non-essential defense spending and ensuring that the investment is maximized for civilian economic benefit. Mechanisms must be institutionalized to channel defense industrial base development into dual-use technologies and civilian manufacturing spin-offs to partially offset the fiscal opportunity cost and inflationary crowding-out identified.^26^

#### Economic Strategy IV: Maritime Supply Chain Hardening

The \$82.1 billion trade vulnerability exposed by the SCS blockade scenario necessitates a strategic shift in maritime policy. This includes investing in redundancy and resilience measures for maritime trade routes, specifically those linking Australia to key North Asian partners (Japan and ROK). Policy must accept and plan for a necessary "sovereign premium"—higher long-term transport costs required to ensure the stability and security of critical maritime trade arteries against geopolitical blockade.
