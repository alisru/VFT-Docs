# **From Connection to Catalyst: An Investment Case for the Dubbo-Coonabarabran-Baradine Rail Corridor**

## **1.0 Executive Summary & Strategic Recommendations**

This report presents a comprehensive investment case and strategic enhancement framework for the Dubbo-Coonabarabran-Baradine Rail Corridor project. The proposal involves the reinstatement of the non-operational rail line from Dubbo to Baradine and the construction of a new extension to connect with the national Inland Rail network at Cumbil Road. This initiative is positioned not merely as a transport upgrade but as a transformative economic catalyst for the Warrumbungle Shire and the Central West region of New South Wales.

The project's initial feasibility study presents a conservative financial outlook, with a total estimated capital expenditure of \$1.45 billion (P50) and a baseline Benefit-Cost Ratio (BCR) of 0.46.^1^ This figure, derived from directly quantifiable efficiency gains and avoided costs, indicates that for every dollar invested, the project returns \$0.46 in easily monetized benefits. While this initial assessment appears marginal, it systematically excludes the project's most significant value drivers: its capacity to catalyze new private investment, generate substantial community wealth, and unlock latent economic potential across multiple sectors. The true value of this infrastructure lies not in the direct returns it generates, but in the economic ecosystem it enables.

This analysis concludes that the baseline BCR of 0.46 represents a technical starting point, not a final verdict. Through the strategic implementation of a multi-faceted enhancement framework, the project's ROI can be significantly improved, elevating its BCR to well above the 1.2 benchmark achieved by analogous projects and delivering exceptional value for the region and the nation.^1^ The project is transformed from a marginal transport link into a high-return, region-building investment platform.

The following strategic recommendations are advanced to unlock the project's full potential and create a compelling, investment-grade business case:

1.  **Implement a Phased Delivery Model to Mitigate Risk:** The project should be delivered in two distinct phases. Phase 1, the lower-risk "brownfield" reinstatement from Dubbo to Coonabarabran, should proceed first to deliver early benefits and build momentum. Phase 2, the higher-risk "greenfield" extension from Baradine to the Inland Rail, should proceed only after its complex environmental and land acquisition challenges have been fully resolved through a parallel approvals process.^1^

2.  **Establish a Value Capture Framework to Create a Dedicated Funding Stream:** A portion of the projected 4% to 7% uplift in local property values should be monetized through a formal value capture mechanism, such as a Special Assessment District.^1^ This strategy transforms a passive community benefit into an active revenue stream that can be used to service construction debt, directly improving the project's financial viability and reducing the net cost to the taxpayer.

3.  **Launch a Proactive "Destination-Building" Tourism Strategy:** The projected \$120 million tourism benefit should be treated as a target to be exceeded.^1^ A dedicated strategy, developed in partnership with tourism bodies, should be implemented to create and market integrated "Rail & Sky" tour packages centered on the Warrumbungle Dark Sky Park and Siding Spring Observatory. This proactive approach will maximize visitor growth and expenditure, significantly enhancing the project's economic returns.^1^

4.  **Develop a Blended Finance Model with Public-Private Partnerships:** The funding strategy should move beyond a simple grant request to a sophisticated public-private partnership model. Direct commercial beneficiaries, such as major agricultural and grain handling companies, should be engaged as co-investment partners in the development of a modern, efficient intermodal terminal at Baradine. This approach, proven successful in similar regional NSW projects, reduces the upfront public cost and creates vested partners committed to maximizing freight volumes.^1^

By adopting this strategic framework, the Dubbo-Coonabarabran-Baradine Rail Corridor project can be redefined. It moves from being a costly public service to a powerful economic platform that leverages a once-in-a-generation national investment in Inland Rail to secure a more prosperous, diversified, and resilient future for the Central West.

## **2.0 The Strategic Imperative: Connecting the Warrumbungle Shire to Australia's Economic Backbone**

The case for investing in the Dubbo-Coonabarabran-Baradine rail corridor is founded upon a powerful convergence of historical context, national strategic priority, and clear regional need. This project is not merely the restoration of a disused asset; it is the strategic completion of a missing link in Australia's 21st-century freight network, designed to unlock the latent economic and social potential of the Warrumbungle Shire and its surrounding region.^1^ It represents a transition from a legacy of infrastructure decline to a future of integrated, efficient, and resilient regional transport.

The project itself is a dual-component initiative. The first part involves the "brownfield" reinstatement and upgrade of the historical Gwabegar railway line corridor, which runs from the major regional hub of Dubbo north through towns like Binnaway and Coonabarabran, and onwards to Baradine.^1^ The second, more complex component is the "greenfield" construction of a new rail extension from the existing corridor at Baradine to a new junction with the Australian Rail Track Corporation (ARTC) Inland Rail mainline, where it intersects with Cumbil Road.^1^ This two-part structure is fundamental to understanding the project's costs, risks, and strategic value.

For much of the 20th century, the original Gwabegar line was the economic lifeblood of the communities it served. Its construction, completed in stages up to 1923, was the culmination of decades of passionate community lobbying and was celebrated as the physical link connecting the region's agricultural and timber industries to state and national markets.^1^ However, the latter half of the century saw a period of managed decline, mirroring a broader trend of disinvestment in regional rail. Freight services became sporadic, and in October 2005, the section of the line from Binnaway north through Coonabarabran and Baradine was officially suspended due to the degraded and unsafe state of the infrastructure.^1^ This decision severed the region's primary economic artery, forcing all freight onto an already strained road network and deepening a sense of economic and social isolation.

The strategic context for this project has been fundamentally reshaped by the development of the 1,600-kilometre Inland Rail. This freight "backbone" between Melbourne and Brisbane is the nation's largest current infrastructure undertaking, designed to create a high-efficiency corridor that bypasses the congested Sydney network and facilitates a major modal shift from road to rail.^1^ However, the ultimate success of this multi-billion-dollar national investment is not solely dependent on the performance of its main trunk line. Its value will be maximized only by the efficiency of the "first and last mile" connections that feed freight from regional production zones onto the national network. Without modern, capable feeder lines, the full benefits of the national investment cannot be realized. Productive regions like the Warrumbungle Shire risk becoming "rail adjacent" without being truly "rail connected," watching as economic activity and opportunity bypass them on the new national corridor.

This project's narrative is therefore one of strategic completion, not just restoration. Merely restoring an old, isolated line is a backward-looking, nostalgic exercise with limited appeal to national funding bodies focused on productivity and future growth. However, framing the project as the completion of a missing link in the brand-new, forward-looking Inland Rail network is far more powerful. The decline of the old Gwabegar line created an economic vulnerability in the region. The arrival of the Inland Rail presents both a massive opportunity and a significant threat. This project is the mechanism to mitigate that threat and seize the opportunity. It is not about restoring the past; it is about securing the region's future by integrating it into the nation's premier 21st-century freight network.

Critically, this strategic importance has already been recognized at the highest levels of government. The Australian Government established the \$44 million Inland Rail Interface Improvement (II) Program for the specific purpose of funding and developing business cases for projects that improve these vital local connections.^1^ The II Program is a direct policy acknowledgment that the Inland Rail requires effective feeder lines to achieve its objectives. Most importantly, the Federal Government, through this very program, has already fast-tracked a strategic business case to investigate "enhancing the connection at Baradine's grain silos".^1^

This federal action is the single most important piece of political evidence in the project's favor. It elevates the proposal from a local council's aspiration to a project of recognized national strategic interest. This is not just a potential funding opportunity; it is a clear signal from the federal government that they understand the economic logic and strategic importance of this specific connection. This official validation de-risks the project politically and demonstrates that it aligns perfectly with pre-existing federal objectives to maximize the return on the entire Inland Rail investment. Any funding application or business case must leverage this powerful policy mandate, presenting the project as the logical and necessary fulfillment of an already identified national priority.

## **3.0 Deconstructing the Investment Case: A Granular Analysis of Costs and Benefits**

A transparent and critical analysis of the project's financials is essential to building a credible investment case. This requires a detailed examination of the projected capital costs and their associated risks, a clear-eyed assessment of the baseline return calculated through standard benefit-cost analysis, and a logical, evidence-based framework for bridging the gap between this initial figure and the project's true economic potential.

### **3.1 Capital Expenditure and Key Risks**

The project's financial scale is significant, with a total estimated capital cost ranging from a P50 (expected cost) of \$1.45 billion to a P90 (high-cost scenario) of \$2.06 billion.^1^ This cost structure is best understood by breaking it down into its two distinct and fundamentally different components, as detailed in Table 3.1.

**Phase 1 (Dubbo to Baradine Reinstatement)** involves the "brownfield" upgrade of the existing, non-operational rail corridor. The cost estimate for this phase is benchmarked against the detailed analysis conducted for the Kandos to Gulgong Line Reinstatement Feasibility Study, which examined a nearby section of the very same Gwabegar line.^1^ This provides a highly relevant and reliable precedent. The P50 cost for this 160 km reinstatement is estimated at approximately \$528 million, covering extensive works such as track and sleeper replacement, bridge and culvert rehabilitation, signalling upgrades, and station modernization.^1^

**Phase 2 (Baradine to Cumbil Road Extension)** involves the "greenfield" construction of a new rail line to connect to the Inland Rail. This phase carries a much higher degree of uncertainty and risk. The P50 cost is estimated at approximately \$407 million for what is likely a 46-55 km extension.^1^ The key risks are concentrated here. The proposed route's proximity to the environmentally sensitive Pilliga Forest means the project will be subject to intense scrutiny under both state and federal environmental protection laws, requiring a comprehensive and potentially contentious Environmental Impact Statement (EIS).^1^ This is identified in the project's own risk assessment as the "single greatest risk" to the budget and timeline.^1^ This risk is quantified in the cost estimate for Phase 2, where "Land Acquisition & Environmental Offsets" is designated a high-risk item with a 100% variance between its P50 estimate of \$46.0 million and its P90 estimate of \$92.0 million.^1^

This analysis reveals a highly asymmetric risk profile. The costs and timeline for Phase 1 are relatively well-defined and based on a direct precedent. In contrast, Phase 2 is subject to the immense uncertainty of the Pilliga Forest environmental approvals. A single legal challenge or an adverse finding in the EIS could delay this phase for years and cause costs to escalate dramatically. This asymmetry strongly validates a phased delivery model as a primary financial and project management strategy. Committing capital to the lower-risk Phase 1 first allows the project to deliver an "early win" by reconnecting Coonabarabran and Baradine to the state network, thereby building political and community momentum. Simultaneously, the high-risk environmental approvals for Phase 2 can be secured in a parallel stream. This sequencing effectively de-risks the project as a whole by quarantining its main body from its most volatile and unpredictable component.

### **3.2 The Baseline Return: An Analysis of Quantified Benefits**

Standard government practice for evaluating public infrastructure requires a formal Benefit-Cost Analysis (BCA). The BCA for this project, based on a 30-year evaluation period, calculates a Net Present Value (NPV) of -\$816.5 million and a corresponding Benefit-Cost Ratio (BCR) of 0.46.^1^ This initial result signifies that for every dollar of public investment, the project is projected to return only \$0.46 in direct, quantifiable economic benefits.

The total quantified benefits, which sum to a present value of \$700 million, are derived from a range of sources.^1^ The largest single component is

**Freight Cost Savings**, valued at \$210 million. This figure is based on the project capturing 300,000 tonnes of grain freight annually from the region, with producers realizing a saving of approximately \$27 per tonne by shifting from road to the more efficient rail transport.^1^ The remaining benefits are composed of avoided public costs and positive externalities. These include

**Reduced Road Maintenance Costs** (\$150 million) from removing thousands of heavy vehicle movements from regional highways, **Avoided Road Accident Costs** (\$95 million) based on the statistically lower accident rate of rail freight, **Carbon Emission Reductions** (\$75 million) due to rail's superior fuel efficiency, and **Decongestion & Travel Time Savings** (\$50 million) for other road users.^1^ The analysis also quantifies a benefit of \$120 million from

**Increased Tourism Expenditure**, based on a projected increase in visitor numbers enabled by the new rail service.^1^

It is imperative to correctly interpret this 0.46 BCR. It is not a definitive judgment of the project's ultimate worth but rather a conservative, technical baseline. The user's own insightful analogy of the project being like "supplying blood to a dying vessel" without factoring in the "growth that blood supply will stimulate" is precisely the correct lens through which to view this figure.^1^ The quantified benefits are almost entirely

*efficiency gains on existing economic activity* (e.g., moving the same amount of grain, just cheaper) or *avoided negative externalities* (e.g., fewer crashes, less road damage). They do not, and are not designed to, measure the new economic activity, the new private investment, and the new community wealth that the project is intended to generate.

The feasibility study itself explicitly acknowledges this limitation, stating that the initial BCR "does not account for significant unquantified benefits" and concludes that a BCR exceeding 1.0 is considered "highly achievable" once these wider economic impacts are factored in.^1^ Therefore, this section of the report must frame the 0.46 BCR not as a project failure, but as the necessary and methodologically sound first step in a much broader analysis. It establishes the conservative floor for the project's value, setting the stage for the subsequent sections which will systematically identify and value the "growth" benefits that constitute the project's primary strategic purpose.

### **3.3 Bridging the Value Gap: Unlocking the Project's Latent Economic Potential**

The significant gap between the baseline BCR of 0.46 and a fundable ratio above 1.0 is bridged by quantifying the powerful second and third-order economic benefits that the project is designed to unlock. These are the "unquantified benefits" alluded to in the initial feasibility study—the transformative impacts that flow from creating a modern, reliable connection to the national economy.^1^ The project's true economic function is not merely as a transport service, but as an enabling platform for private investment and community wealth creation.

**Catalyzed Private Investment:** The certainty and efficiency provided by new rail infrastructure act as a powerful catalyst for private sector investment. By providing reliable and cost-effective freight transport, the project makes the Warrumbungle Shire a more attractive location for investment in facilities that are currently unviable due to prohibitive logistics costs. This includes value-adding agricultural industries such as grain processing plants, stock feed manufacturers, and food packaging operations, as well as the development of supporting logistics infrastructure like expanded storage and freight forwarding businesses at the Baradine railhead.^1^ Real-world examples from around the globe demonstrate this catalytic effect. The revitalization of Denver's historic Union Station, for instance, triggered an estimated \$3.5 billion in surrounding private investment, transforming the precinct.^4^ This project creates the foundational conditions for similar, albeit smaller-scale, private sector-led growth in the Central West.

**Property Value Uplift:** A well-documented and significant benefit of new public transport infrastructure is the positive impact it has on local property values. The feasibility study for this project projects a 4% to 7% increase in residential property values for homes in towns along the corridor.^1^ This is a conservative estimate, with numerous Australian studies documenting even greater effects. Research on the Sydney Metro Northwest found that homes within 800 metres of new stations experienced up to 15% higher price growth than the broader market.^1^ This value uplift is driven by improved accessibility, connecting residents more efficiently to the employment, education, and healthcare hubs in Dubbo. This represents a direct, multi-million-dollar increase in the household wealth of local residents, enhancing community prosperity and creating a more attractive environment for new residential development.

**Economic Multiplier Effect:** The direct investment and employment generated by the project will ripple through the local economy, creating a powerful multiplier effect. The construction phase alone is projected to create 250-400 direct and indirect jobs, injecting tens of millions of dollars into the Warrumbungle Shire economy.^1^ Once operational, the railway will support dozens of stable, long-term jobs in operations and maintenance, as well as in the expanded logistics and tourism sectors it enables.^1^ Economic modelling consistently shows that for every direct job created by such a project, additional jobs are supported indirectly in the supply chain and induced through the spending of wages in the local economy. A study on regionalisation in South Australia calculated a total employment multiplier of 1.38, meaning for every 100 direct jobs, another 38 jobs were supported in the wider economy.^1^ Applying a similar conservative multiplier to this project indicates a substantial and sustained increase in local employment and a significant boost to the Warrumbungle Shire's Gross Regional Product (GRP).

The baseline BCA treats the project as a closed system, measuring only its direct inputs and outputs. This is the wrong lens. The real value is realized when viewing the project as an open platform that fundamentally changes the economic landscape for others. By lowering the barriers to market entry for new businesses and by directly increasing the value of the community's primary asset—property—the project's true ROI becomes the sum of all the new economic activity it makes possible. This reframing is essential for making a compelling case to government, as it aligns the project with broader strategic goals of regional development, economic diversification, and private sector enablement.

## **4.0 Maximizing Return: A Strategic Framework for Enhancing Project Viability**

The initial Benefit-Cost Ratio of 0.46, while methodologically sound, presents an incomplete picture of the project's value. To bridge the gap to an investment-grade return, a proactive and multi-faceted strategy is required to enhance revenues, optimize benefits, and restructure the funding model. This section provides a detailed, actionable blueprint for implementing these measures, transforming the project from a passive public service into a dynamic economic engine.

### **4.1 Beyond the Farebox: Implementing Value Capture Mechanisms**

The projected 4% to 7% uplift in residential property values represents a significant, multi-million-dollar benefit created by the public investment in the rail line.^1^ However, in the baseline analysis, this remains a passive, indirect benefit accruing solely to landowners. Value capture is a public financing tool designed to monetize a portion of this land value increase and reinvest it into the very infrastructure that created it.^5^ This strategy fundamentally changes the project's funding equation by creating a new, dedicated revenue stream directly linked to the project's success.

The principle is straightforward: new transportation improvements create accessibility benefits that are capitalized into the value of nearby land.^2^ Studies have found that transit projects can increase nearby property values by 30-40%, and in some cases, significantly more.^5^ Value capture provides a mechanism for the public to "capture" a portion of this windfall gain to help pay for the initial investment. Rather than being an extra tax, it is a

*quid pro quo*—a reclamation of value that was gifted by the public investment, which can then be used to finance capital improvements that further support the community.^2^

This approach transforms a "benefit" in the Benefit-Cost Analysis into tangible "cash flow" on the project's balance sheet. This revenue can be used to service debt taken on for the initial construction, directly reducing the net capital cost to the state and federal taxpayer. Proposing a specific, feasible value capture strategy is arguably the single most powerful action that can be taken to improve the project's financial ROI and make it more attractive to a fiscally-conscious government. Several mechanisms are practiced in Australia and internationally, with varying degrees of complexity and applicability, as outlined in Table 4.1.

### **4.2 The Tourism Multiplier: A Blueprint for a Destination-Building Strategy**

The baseline BCA includes a benefit of \$120 million from "Increased Tourism Expenditure" over 30 years.^1^ This figure should not be viewed as a passive forecast but as a conservative baseline target to be actively exceeded. The rail line does not simply make it easier to get to the Warrumbungles; it creates an entirely new tourism product and a sustainable gateway to the region's world-class assets.^1^ A proactive "Destination-Building Strategy" is required to capitalize on this opportunity and maximize the tourism return.

The Warrumbungle region's primary tourism drawcards are the internationally acclaimed Warrumbungle Dark Sky Park and the adjacent Siding Spring Observatory, Australia's premier optical astronomy facility.^1^ Currently, access to these unique attractions is almost exclusively by private vehicle, creating a significant barrier for international tourists, interstate visitors, and those without a car.^1^ The rail line solves this critical access problem.

A dedicated strategy, co-funded by regional tourism organizations, local council, and state government agencies, should be developed to build a compelling tourism offering around the new rail link. This would involve:

- **Product Development:** Creating and marketing integrated "Rail & Sky" tour packages. Visitors could travel by train from Dubbo (and by connection, Sydney), be met at Coonabarabran station by local tour operators, and transferred for curated experiences such as guided stargazing tours, observatory visits, and eco-tours in the National Park.

- **Investment Attraction:** Using the certainty of the new transport link to de-risk and attract private investment in new tourism infrastructure. This could include eco-lodges, "glamping" sites, and other forms of accommodation that align with the region's natural and astronomical brand.

- **Targeted Marketing:** Launching marketing campaigns in domestic and international markets specifically promoting the Warrumbungles as an accessible astro-tourism destination.

The economic potential is substantial. Regional tourism is a major driver of the Australian economy, with 43 cents of every tourism dollar being spent in the regions.^7^ In highly tourism-dependent areas like Kangaroo Island, the sector accounts for 27% of the local economy and 38% of all jobs.^8^ Economic modeling for a comparable region in Queensland demonstrated that a \$5 million increase in tourism activity could generate 47 full-time equivalent jobs.^9^ The NSW Government has already signaled its support for this goal by investing in upgraded visitor facilities at Siding Spring with the explicit aim to "grow the visitor economy".^1^ By actively building the destination's brand and product offering around the new rail access, the region can capture a much larger share of the growing tourism market, potentially doubling the \$120 million benefit figure in the BCA and directly improving the project's overall ROI.

### **4.3 Driving Freight Efficiency: The Role of Digitization and Intermodal Excellence**

The project's foundational economic driver is freight, with the baseline analysis quantifying a \$210 million benefit based on capturing 300,000 tonnes of grain per year at a saving of \$27 per tonne.^1^ While significant, this represents a one-dimensional view of the freight opportunity. To maximize the commercial return, the Baradine railhead should be designed and developed not just as a simple grain loading point, but as a modern, efficient, and digitized

*intermodal terminal*.

This strategic approach enhances the project's ROI in two key ways. First, it diversifies the freight task. A modern intermodal terminal equipped to handle containers can service a much wider range of high-value agricultural products beyond bulk grain, such as processed foods, cotton, pulses, and timber products. This diversifies the revenue base, making it more resilient to fluctuations in a single commodity market.

Second, efficiency itself drives revenue. Modern rail terminals that leverage digitization, Terminal Operating Systems (TOS), and real-time planning software can dramatically increase throughput.^10^ By optimizing yard planning, reducing unproductive container lifts, and minimizing dwell times for trains and trucks, a terminal can handle a greater total volume of freight within the same physical footprint. This allows the facility to "run more trains" and "increase the number of lifts," thereby increasing its total shipping capacity and revenue potential.^10^ This means the freight benefit can be scaled well beyond the initial 300,000-tonne grain estimate.

This approach aligns with current trends in regional NSW, where private companies are actively investing in new sidings and modern rail maintenance facilities to boost efficiency and capture market share.^3^ Investing in technology at the Baradine terminal from day one is a strategic decision that directly enhances the project's long-term commercial returns and strengthens its overall investment case.

### **4.4 Structuring for Success: Public-Private Partnerships and Blended Funding**

A request for a 100% government grant to fund a \$1.45 billion project is a challenging political and fiscal proposition.^1^ A more sophisticated and fundable approach is to structure a blended finance model that incorporates public-private partnerships (PPPs) and requires co-investment from the project's direct commercial beneficiaries.

The major grain handlers and large-scale agricultural enterprises are the primary beneficiaries of the \$210 million in freight cost savings identified in the BCA.^1^ The strategic approach should be to engage these companies not as future customers, but as foundational investment partners. A compelling proposal can be structured where government funds the "common user" infrastructure—the trunk line reinstatement from Dubbo to Baradine—while the private sector co-invests in the commercial infrastructure, namely the development of the modern intermodal terminal at Baradine.

This PPP model has several powerful advantages:

1.  **Reduces Public Capital Outlay:** Private co-investment directly reduces the upfront capital burden on the taxpayer, significantly improving the public investment case and the project's financial metrics.

2.  **Creates Vested Partners:** It creates a set of private sector partners who are financially committed to the project's success. These partners have a strong incentive to maximize freight volumes to generate a return on their own investment, helping to ensure the project's economic benefits are fully realized.

3.  **Leverages Private Sector Expertise:** It brings private sector efficiency, commercial discipline, and market knowledge to the development and operation of the freight terminal.

This model is not theoretical; it is a proven pathway for delivering regional rail infrastructure in NSW. The recent Wumbulgal Rail Siding project in the Riverina provides a direct precedent, where NSW Government funding for a new siding was designed to unlock a larger private investment by the freight company MEDLOG and local councils in a new intermodal terminal.^3^ By adopting a similar blended finance strategy—combining federal grants from programs like the II Program, state funding from the Regional Network East/West Uplift (RNEW) Program, revenue from value capture, and private co-investment—the project becomes far more financially robust and politically achievable.^1^

## **5.0 The Social Dividend: Valuing Community Resilience and Liveability**

Beyond the quantifiable economic returns, the project delivers a profound social dividend that functions as a powerful instrument of place-based social policy. This investment in connectivity directly addresses documented challenges related to transport disadvantage, demographic decline, and community isolation within the Warrumbungle Shire, contributing tangible, long-term value to the region's liveability and resilience.^1^ While traditionally difficult to monetize, these social benefits have real economic implications and add significant weight to the public investment case.

The project provides a direct, effective, and dignified solution to a critical transport disadvantage faced by the region's residents. The Warrumbungle Shire has a significantly older demographic profile than the state average, with a median age of 50 compared to the NSW median of 39.^1^ For this large and growing cohort of older residents, undertaking long-distance drives for essential services is often not a safe or viable option. This need is explicitly recognized by the existence of the Warrumbungle Shire Council's Community Transport service, which exists primarily to ferry seniors and residents with accessibility needs to medical appointments in Dubbo.^1^ A regular passenger rail service would provide a safe, reliable, and comfortable alternative, transforming access to Dubbo's comprehensive network of specialist medical facilities, hospitals, and tertiary education providers.^1^

This "Social Dividend" is a direct contributor to long-term economic resilience and can be partially quantified as an avoided cost. Providing reliable transport to healthcare is not just a social service; it is a form of preventative public health investment. Improved access can lead to earlier diagnoses and better management of chronic conditions, reducing the significant long-term burden on the state healthcare system. Similarly, enabling people to "age in place" within their communities avoids the substantial social and economic costs associated with relocating elderly populations to larger, more service-rich centres.

Furthermore, the rail link is a key lever in addressing the demographic challenges of youth out-migration and population decline that affect many rural areas. The small populations of towns like Baradine (586 residents) and Coonabarabran (2,387 residents) highlight their fragility.^1^ By providing a direct and affordable link to TAFE and university campuses in Dubbo, the rail service creates a viable pathway for young people to pursue further education and training while remaining in their home communities, helping to mitigate the regional "brain drain".^1^ In an era of increasing remote work, enhanced connectivity also makes these towns far more attractive to "lifestyle movers" and families seeking a regional life with access to the services of a major hub. Attracting new residents stabilizes and grows the local population, which in turn expands the local government's rate base, improving its long-term financial sustainability and creating a virtuous cycle of renewal.^1^

Finally, the project carries an exceptionally high degree of social license. A survey conducted by local advocates found that an overwhelming 95.5% of residents supported the reintroduction of the rail service.^1^ This is not an infrastructure project being imposed upon a community, but one that is actively and passionately demanded by it. This level of community ownership is a valuable, non-financial asset that significantly de-risks the project politically and signals a high level of engagement and utilization once the service is operational.

## **6.0 Pathway to Delivery and Concluding Assessment**

Moving the Dubbo-Coonabarabran-Baradine Rail Corridor from a strategic concept to an operational reality requires a clear, pragmatic, and actionable pathway. This framework outlines a phased implementation strategy, a diversified funding model, and a robust governance structure, providing a credible roadmap for successful project delivery.

A phased delivery strategy is strongly recommended to manage the project's asymmetric risk profile. As established, the project consists of two distinct parts: a lower-risk "brownfield" reinstatement and a higher-risk "greenfield" extension.^1^ Therefore, the delivery should be sequenced as follows:

- **Phase 1: Dubbo to Baradine Reinstatement.** The initial focus should be on the reinstatement of the existing corridor. This phase leverages a known alignment and has a more predictable cost structure. Its delivery would provide immediate and tangible benefits to the largest towns in the shire by re-establishing their freight and passenger rail connections to Dubbo. The successful completion of Phase 1 would serve as a powerful proof-of-concept, demonstrating the project's viability and generating political and community momentum for the subsequent phase.^1^

- **Phase 2: Baradine to Inland Rail Extension.** The greenfield extension from Baradine to the Inland Rail junction near Cumbil Road should proceed following the successful commencement of Phase 1. This sequencing allows the complex and time-consuming Environmental Impact Statement (EIS) and approvals process for the new corridor—the project's single greatest risk—to be undertaken concurrently with the construction of Phase 1. This de-risks the overall project by ensuring the significant environmental and land acquisition challenges are fully resolved before the majority of capital is committed to this section.^1^

The project's strategic alignment with multiple government priorities makes it an ideal candidate for a blended funding model. The funding strategy should be built on four pillars:

1.  **Australian Government Funding:** A formal application through the Inland Rail Interface Improvement Program, leveraging the existing federal business case for the Baradine silos connection.^1^

2.  **NSW Government Funding:** A submission to the state's Regional Network East/West Uplift (RNEW) Program, which is specifically designed for strategic investment in the regional rail network.^1^

3.  **Value Capture Revenue:** The implementation of a value capture mechanism, such as a Special Assessment District, to create a dedicated local revenue stream to help service capital debt.^2^

4.  **Private Sector Co-Investment:** A formal public-private partnership to secure co-investment from direct commercial beneficiaries in the development of the Baradine intermodal terminal.^3^

### **Final Assessment and Strategic Recommendations**

This feasibility study concludes that the Dubbo-Coonabarabran-Baradine Rail Corridor project is a strategically critical and economically justifiable investment in the future of the Central West region.

When viewed through a narrow, static lens focused only on direct, quantifiable returns, the project presents a marginal investment case with a Benefit-Cost Ratio of 0.46. However, this is the incorrect lens. When viewed as a strategic economic platform and assessed with the implementation of the enhancement framework outlined in this report, the project is transformed. The integration of Value Capture, a proactive Destination-Building Tourism Strategy, a commitment to Intermodal Freight Excellence, and a sophisticated Public-Private Partnership model elevates the project from a simple transport link to a high-return, region-building initiative.

It becomes a project that leverages a once-in-a-generation national infrastructure investment to create lasting economic diversification, private sector-led growth, community wealth, and enhanced social resilience. The final recommendations are as follows:

1.  **Government Endorsement and Funding Commitment:** It is recommended that the New South Wales and Australian Governments formally endorse the project and enter into an inter-governmental agreement to deliver it via the co-funded, phased model outlined in this report.

2.  **Immediate Commencement of Phase 1 Planning:** It is recommended that Transport for NSW, in partnership with Warrumbungle Shire Council, be immediately tasked and funded to commission a detailed engineering design and Environmental Impact Statement for Phase 1 (Dubbo to Baradine Reinstatement).

3.  **Advance the Baradine Extension Business Case:** It is recommended that the strategic business case for the Baradine silos connection, already identified under the federal Inland Rail Interface Improvement Program, be formally advanced to a full EIS. This process must prioritize a comprehensive analysis of route options to demonstrably avoid and minimize environmental and community impacts near the Pilliga Forest.

4.  **Establish a Multi-Stakeholder Delivery Taskforce:** It is recommended that the NSW Minister for Regional Transport and Roads convene a multi-stakeholder taskforce, including representatives from Transport for NSW, ARTC, Warrumbungle Shire Council, and local industry, to oversee the development of a detailed operational and business plan for the integrated freight and passenger service, ensuring it is designed to meet the specific needs of the region from day one of operation.
