# **Reconnecting the Central West: A Feasibility Study and Return on Investment Analysis for the Dubbo-Coonabarabran-Baradine Rail Corridor**

## **Executive Summary**

This report presents a comprehensive feasibility study and Return on Investment (ROI) analysis for the proposed reactivation and extension of the Gwabegar railway line, creating a modern freight and passenger corridor from Dubbo through Coonabarabran to Baradine. The project is strategically positioned to function as a vital feeder line to the national Inland Rail network, unlocking significant economic potential for the Warrumbungle Shire and the broader Central West region of New South Wales.

The project scope encompasses three core components: (1) the full reinstatement and upgrade of the approximately 160 km of non-operational track between Dubbo and Coonabarabran; (2) the greenfield construction of a new rail line from Coonabarabran, passing through Baradine to a junction with the Inland Rail mainline near Cumbil Road; and (3) the development of a functional interface with the Inland Rail network at a junction near Cumbil Road to ensure seamless national connectivity. The operational mandate is for a dual-purpose service, facilitating light freight (primarily agricultural commodities) and establishing a new regional passenger service connecting the corridor's towns to the major service centre of Dubbo.

The analysis projects a total capital expenditure in the range of \$850 million to \$1.4 billion, a figure derived from detailed benchmarking against analogous rail reinstatement and construction projects in regional Australia. While the direct financial return from freight and passenger fares is not expected to cover this capital outlay, the project's true value is revealed through a comprehensive Benefit-Cost Analysis (BCA). The project is projected to deliver a strongly positive Benefit-Cost Ratio (BCR), driven by substantial positive externalities. These include significant freight cost savings for agricultural producers, multi-million-dollar reductions in long-term road maintenance expenditure, quantifiable road safety benefits from removing heavy vehicles from regional highways, and monetised environmental benefits from reduced carbon emissions.

The economic impact on the Warrumbungle Shire is forecast to be transformative. The construction phase will provide a major short-term stimulus, creating hundreds of jobs and injecting tens of millions of dollars into the local economy. In the operational phase, the project will support ongoing employment and catalyze new investment in value-adding agriculture, logistics, and tourism infrastructure. The improved connectivity is projected to generate a measurable uplift in Gross Regional Product (GRP) and stimulate a 4-7% increase in residential property values in towns along the corridor, enhancing local wealth.

Beyond the quantifiable economics, the project delivers a profound social dividend. It directly addresses identified transport disadvantages for an aging regional population by providing reliable access to essential health, education, and social services in Dubbo. It creates new opportunities for youth retention, attracts new residents, and combats the social and physical isolation of rural communities.

This report concludes that the Dubbo-Coonabarabran-Baradine Rail Corridor is a strategically vital and economically sound investment. It leverages the multi-billion-dollar national investment in Inland Rail to restore a historical economic artery and create a modern, resilient transport link for the future. The final recommendations advocate for a co-funded, phased delivery model, led by the NSW and Australian Governments, to unlock the immense potential of this project and secure a more prosperous and connected future for the region.

## **1.0 Strategic Rationale: Reconnecting a Region to the National Network**

The case for investing in the Dubbo-Coonabarabran-Baradine rail corridor is founded upon a powerful convergence of historical context, national strategic priority, and clear regional need. This project is not merely the restoration of a disused asset; it is the strategic completion of a missing link in Australia's 21st-century freight network, designed to unlock the latent economic and social potential of the Warrumbungle Shire and its surrounding region. It represents a transition from a legacy of infrastructure decline to a future of integrated, efficient, and resilient regional transport.

### **1.1 The Latent Potential of the Gwabegar Corridor**

The Gwabegar railway line was, for much of the 20th century, the economic lifeblood of the communities it served. Its construction, undertaken progressively from 1882 to its completion in 1923, was the result of decades of persistent and passionate community lobbying, particularly from the residents of Mudgee who formed a Railway League as early as 1875 to advance their cause.^1^ The line's opening was celebrated as the realisation of a long-held dream, cementing the connection of these towns to the state's economic heartland.^1^ For generations, it provided the primary means of transporting the region's rich output of grain, timber, and livestock to market, while also offering a vital passenger link for residents.^2^

However, the latter half of the century saw a period of managed decline, mirroring a broader trend of disinvestment in regional rail infrastructure across New South Wales.^4^ Passenger services beyond Mudgee were replaced by coaches in the early 1980s, and freight services became increasingly sporadic.^2^ The critical blow came in October 2005, when the section of the line from Binnaway through Coonabarabran to Gwabegar was suspended by the Rail Infrastructure Corporation, citing safety concerns stemming from degraded infrastructure.^2^ This decision effectively severed the rail connection for the northern part of the corridor, forcing the region's freight onto an already strained road network and deepening a sense of isolation for its communities.

Today, the physical corridor remains largely intact—a dormant public asset slumbering in the foothills of the Warrumbungles.^3^ While officially "booked out of use," it represents a significant repository of latent economic potential.^2^ The reactivation of this corridor is therefore not an exercise in nostalgia, but a pragmatic and necessary step to reclaim and modernise a valuable piece of public infrastructure that was foundational to the region's historical prosperity and is essential for its future growth.

### **1.2 The Inland Rail Imperative: A Catalyst for Regional Integration**

The strategic context for regional rail investment in Australia has been fundamentally reshaped by the development of the Inland Rail project. This 1,600-kilometre freight "backbone" is the nation's largest current infrastructure undertaking, designed to forge a high-efficiency corridor between Melbourne and Brisbane, crucially bypassing the congested Sydney metropolitan network.^6^ The primary objective of this multi-billion-dollar investment is to facilitate a significant modal shift of long-haul freight from road to rail, thereby enhancing national productivity, improving safety, and reducing carbon emissions.^10^

The ultimate success of Inland Rail, however, is not solely dependent on the performance of its main trunk line. Its value will be maximised by the efficiency of the "first and last mile" connections that feed freight from regional production zones onto the national network. Without modern, capable feeder lines, the full benefits of the national investment cannot be realised, and productive regions risk becoming "rail adjacent" without being truly "rail connected." This strategic necessity has been explicitly recognised by both government and key infrastructure bodies. The Australian Government established the \$44 million Inland Rail Interface Improvement Program (II Program) for the specific purpose of developing business cases for projects that improve these vital local connections and enhance supply chain productivity.^13^ Concurrently, Infrastructure Australia has highlighted the critical need to upgrade the state's Country Regional Network (CRN) branch lines to a higher 25 Tonne Axle Load (TAL) standard, ensuring interoperability with the more robust Inland Rail infrastructure and maximising freight productivity.^16^

This project aligns perfectly with these national strategic directives. The Federal Government, through the II Program, has already fast-tracked a strategic business case to investigate "enhancing the connection at Baradine's grain silos".^13^ This action provides a direct and powerful policy mandate, confirming that the proposed Baradine connection is not merely a local aspiration but is already identified at a federal level as a project of strategic interest. This is further reinforced by formal submissions from the Warrumbungle Shire Council, which has strongly advocated for the completion of Inland Rail and the associated interface improvements to support the region's agricultural producers.^18^ The project, therefore, represents a direct opportunity to leverage the massive national investment in Inland Rail to deliver tangible, targeted benefits to the Central West.

### **1.3 Project Definition: Scope, Objectives, and Vision**

The project is defined by a clear and logical three-part scope, designed to create a fully integrated and functional transport corridor.

**Scope:**

1.  **Reinstatement (Dubbo to Coonabarabran):** This phase involves the comprehensive upgrade of the approximately 160 km of non-operational railway between the major regional hub of Dubbo, where it connects to the Main Western Line, and Coonabarabran.^19^ The line will be reinstated to a modern standard capable of safely and efficiently handling both light freight and passenger rolling stock.

2.  **Extension (Coonabarabran to Inland Rail):** This phase involves the greenfield construction of a new rail line from Coonabarabran, passing through Baradine to a junction with the Inland Rail mainline near Cumbil Road.^74^ This extension is critical as it will establish a railhead in one of the region's most productive grain-growing areas, directly serving the silos that were previously rail-connected and are the subject of the federal business case investigation.

3.  **Integration (Connection to Inland Rail):** This component ensures the development of a seamless and efficient interface between the reactivated branch line and the Inland Rail mainline. This will occur at a strategic junction with the Inland Rail mainline near Cumbil Road, north of Baradine, designed to facilitate multi-directional connectivity and full interoperability between the two networks, minimising operational delays.^74^

**Objectives:**

The project is guided by a set of clear economic and social objectives designed to address specific regional challenges and opportunities:

- **Economic Objectives:**

  - To significantly reduce freight transport costs for agricultural producers in the Warrumbungle Shire, enhancing their profitability and competitiveness in domestic and export markets.

  - To stimulate economic diversification and investment, particularly in the tourism sector, by creating a new, sustainable transport gateway to the region's world-class natural and scientific attractions.

  - To create direct and indirect employment during both the construction and operational phases, boosting the Gross Regional Product of the Warrumbungle Shire.

- **Social Objectives:**

  - To establish a reliable, safe, and modern passenger rail service connecting Baradine, Coonabarabran, and intermediary towns to the comprehensive services hub of Dubbo.

  - To improve social equity by addressing identified transport disadvantages, particularly for the region's aging population, youth, and residents without access to private vehicles.

  - To enhance community connectivity and reduce the physical and social isolation of the region's towns, fostering greater community resilience and vibrancy.

The overarching vision is for a modern, dual-purpose rail corridor that functions as both an economic enabler and a community connector, securing a more prosperous, sustainable, and integrated future for the region.

## **2.0 Infrastructure Assessment and Capital Expenditure Analysis**

A robust assessment of the required infrastructure works and a detailed, evidence-based projection of capital expenditure are fundamental to establishing the project's feasibility. This analysis distinguishes between the reinstatement of the existing "brownfield" corridor and the construction of the new "greenfield" extension, drawing on cost benchmarks from directly comparable regional rail projects to develop a credible financial model.

### **2.1 State of the Asset: Engineering Survey of the Dubbo-Coonabarabran Corridor**

The rail corridor between Dubbo and Coonabarabran, particularly the section north of Binnaway, has been formally suspended from use since 2005, a decision prompted by the deteriorated state of the infrastructure.^2^ While the physical alignment, earthworks, and major structures remain, the track itself is considered unfit for modern operations. A comprehensive on-the-ground engineering assessment is a necessary precursor to final design, but based on the condition of similar disused regional lines in NSW, a significant program of works can be anticipated.

The *Kandos to Gulgong Line Reinstatement Feasibility Study*, which examined a nearby section of the same Gwabegar line, provides an excellent technical proxy for the scope of works required.^21^ That study found that required asset interventions ranged from "spot component renewals to full track reconstruction".^21^ Applying this precedent, the reinstatement of the Dubbo-Coonabarabran corridor will necessitate a comprehensive program including:

- **Track and Civil Works:** Extensive replacement of degraded timber sleepers (likely with more durable steel or concrete alternatives), renewal of worn rail sections, and replacement of fastenings. The entire section will require ballast cleaning, replenishment, and re-tamping to restore the track geometry and elasticity required for safe and comfortable operation.

- **Structures:** Detailed structural assessments and rehabilitation of all bridges, culverts, and drainage systems to ensure they meet contemporary load and flood immunity standards.

- **Signalling and Communications:** Re-establishment of a safe working system for the line. A cost-effective system such as Train Order Working, as proposed for the Kandos-Gulgong section, would likely be sufficient for the anticipated traffic volumes.^21^

- **Level Crossings:** A full audit and upgrade of all public and private level crossings to comply with modern Australian safety standards (AS 1742.7), which may involve upgrading passive controls (signage) to active controls (flashing lights and boom gates) at higher-risk locations.^22^

- **Station Precincts:** Reactivation and modernisation of station facilities at key towns along the route, including Coonabarabran. This would involve ensuring platforms, shelters, and access pathways are compliant with the *Disability Standards for Accessible Public Transport* (DSAPT).

### **2.2 Greenfield Development: Route Analysis for the Extension to Inland Rail**

The construction of the new greenfield rail link from Coonabarabran, passing through Baradine to a junction with the Inland Rail mainline near Cumbil Road presents a more complex set of engineering and regulatory challenges.^74^ The proposed corridor traverses a landscape of significant agricultural value and environmental sensitivity, demanding careful planning and rigorous assessment.

**Geotechnical and Hydrological Profile:** The route will cross an area characterised by Pilliga sandstone formations and extensive alluvial plains associated with the Castlereagh River catchment.^23^ Geotechnical assessments undertaken for the nearby Inland Rail corridor identified areas of poor ground conditions, including alluvial and colluvial soils, which require more complex and costly earthworks methodologies.^24^ Furthermore, the route will intersect with numerous ephemeral creeks that flow from the Warrumbungle Mountains, an area known for fast-flowing water during storm events. This presents a significant hydrological challenge, requiring extensive culvert and bridge infrastructure designed to withstand substantial water flows and avoid creating a "levee bank" effect that could exacerbate local flooding—a major concern raised by landholders in relation to the Inland Rail alignment.^25^

**Environmental Constraints and Regulatory Framework:** The proximity of the proposed corridor to the Pilliga Forest is the project's most significant environmental consideration. The Pilliga is the largest remaining temperate woodland in eastern Australia, a designated National Biodiversity Hotspot, and an internationally recognised Important Bird Area.^26^ It provides critical habitat for numerous threatened species and contains areas of high cultural significance.^26^

Any major infrastructure project in this landscape will be subject to intense scrutiny under a dual regulatory framework: the New South Wales *Environmental Planning and Assessment Act 1979* (EP&A Act) and the Commonwealth *Environment Protection and Biodiversity Conservation Act 1999* (EPBC Act).^29^ Given its scale and potential impacts, the project would almost certainly be classified as State Significant Infrastructure (SSI), necessitating the preparation of a comprehensive Environmental Impact Statement (EIS).^29^

The experience of other major projects in the region, such as the Narrabri Gas Project, demonstrates the complexity and contentiousness of development in the Pilliga. Key issues that the EIS for the rail extension will need to rigorously address include:

- Direct impacts from vegetation clearing and the scale of the disturbance footprint.

- Indirect impacts such as habitat fragmentation, which is recognised as a Key Threatening Process under the NSW *Biodiversity Conservation Act 2016*.^28^

- Cumulative impacts when considered alongside existing agricultural, forestry, and potential resource extraction activities.^30^

- Impacts on surface and groundwater hydrology.

- Disturbance from construction noise, dust, and lighting.^30^

The environmental approval process for this greenfield section represents the most significant risk to the project's budget and timeline. A thorough, transparent, and scientifically robust assessment, coupled with early and genuine community consultation, will be essential for navigating this process successfully.

### **2.3 Capital Cost Projections: A Detailed Financial Breakdown**

Developing a credible capital cost estimate requires careful benchmarking against similar projects. This analysis leverages the most relevant available data to build a detailed projection.

**Benchmarking - Reinstatement (Dubbo to Coonabarabran):** The most reliable cost proxy for this brownfield component is the *Kandos to Gulgong Line Reinstatement Feasibility Study*.^21^ That detailed analysis, conducted on a 93 km section of the same Gwabegar line, produced a final capital cost estimate of \$298.3 million to upgrade the line to a 25 TAL standard, suitable for modern freight operations. This yields a per-kilometre rate of approximately

**\$3.21 million**. Applying this highly relevant benchmark to the approximately 160 km Dubbo-Coonabarabran section results in a baseline reinstatement cost of **\$513.6 million**.

**Benchmarking - Greenfield Construction (Coonabarabran to Baradine):** Estimating the cost of new regional rail construction is more variable. High-end projects like urban metros or high-speed lines, with costs exceeding \$100 million per kilometre, are not appropriate comparisons.^31^ A more suitable benchmark is the Inland Rail project itself. While its total cost has escalated to over \$31 billion for 1,700 km, its average cost of approximately

**\$18.2 million per kilometre** provides a useful, albeit high-level, reference for a major greenfield freight corridor.^33^ However, a single-track, non-electrified line with simpler signalling requirements would be less expensive. A cost range of

**\$10 million to \$15 million per kilometre** is considered a realistic and prudent estimate for this type of regional construction, particularly given the potential for complex environmental mitigations and land acquisition. For the 46 km extension to Baradine, this yields a cost range of **\$460 million to \$690 million**.

**Total Estimated Capital Cost:** Combining these components and incorporating allowances for new rolling stock (e.g., two 3-car bi-mode train sets), station facilities, and a necessary project contingency, the total estimated capital expenditure for the project is projected to be in the range of **\$850 million to \$1.4 billion**. ### **2.4 Operational and Lifecycle Maintenance Modelling**

Beyond the initial capital investment, a sustainable operational and maintenance model is crucial for the long-term success of the rail line.

**Maintenance Costs:** The ongoing cost of maintaining the rail infrastructure is a significant operational expenditure. Historical data from the Australian Rail Track Corporation (ARTC) indicates an average operating expenditure of approximately \$7,773 per track kilometre on its simpler, non-NSW network, rising to \$19,697 per track kilometre on the more complex NSW network.^34^ Industry-wide forecasts predict that maintenance activity will grow by nearly 20% in real terms over the next decade, driven by an expanding national network and the increasing frequency of climate-related impacts such as floods and fires.^35^ For planning purposes, an annual maintenance budget in the range of

**\$15,000 to \$25,000 per track kilometre** is considered a prudent and realistic estimate. For the completed 206 km corridor, this equates to a total annual lifecycle maintenance cost of **\$3.1 million to \$5.2 million**.

**Operational Models for Low-Patronage Services:** It is anticipated that the passenger service will, at least initially, operate with low patronage levels. This is a common challenge for regional rail services globally. International research and case studies offer valuable models for ensuring viability. Studies from Sweden, for example, have demonstrated that improving off-peak and all-day service frequency can lead to substantial patronage growth, including during traditional peak hours.^36^ This is because a more frequent, all-day service significantly increases the overall utility and flexibility for potential passengers, making it a more attractive travel option even for those who do not regularly travel off-peak.

A hybrid operational model, where revenue from light freight movements helps to subsidize the fixed operational costs of the passenger service, presents a viable pathway. Furthermore, the adoption of modern rolling stock, such as the bi-mode (diesel-electric) trains currently being introduced to the NSW TrainLink regional fleet, offers significant advantages. These trains can operate more efficiently, reduce carbon emissions and diesel particulates, and provide greater operational flexibility compared to the legacy diesel fleet.^37^

## **3.0 Commercial Viability: Market and Demand Forecast**

The commercial viability of the proposed rail corridor rests on a "braided" demand model, where multiple, distinct market segments—agriculture, local passenger transit, and tourism—collectively generate a sustainable level of usage. While freight provides the foundational economic driver, the passenger and tourism components are critical for maximising the project's overall utility and delivering its full social and economic dividend.

### **3.1 Freight Market Analysis: Unlocking Agricultural Value Chains**

The agricultural sector is the cornerstone of the Warrumbungle Shire's economy. In 2016, the sector generated over \$260 million in output, accounting for 32.1% of the Shire's total economic production and directly employing 900 people, or 29% of the local workforce.^38^ The region is a significant producer of high-value commodities, including cereals (wheat, barley), oilseeds (canola), pulses (chickpeas), and livestock.^38^ The gross value of agricultural production in the broader region has historically exceeded \$100 million annually.^41^

A primary constraint on the profitability and growth of this sector is the high cost and volatility of freight transport. Supply chain costs can account for as much as 30% of a grain producer's total cost of production.^42^ The region's reliance on road transport exposes producers to a freight market where rates can fluctuate dramatically, from as low as 7 cents per tonne-kilometre during periods of low demand to as high as 22 cents per tonne-kilometre during peak export periods.^43^ For a typical 450 km road journey from Baradine to the Port of Newcastle, this variability represents a major and unpredictable operating expense.

Rail transport offers a fundamentally more efficient, reliable, and environmentally sustainable mode for bulk haulage.^4^ The economic case for the Inland Rail project itself is predicated on its ability to reduce freight costs by an estimated \$10 per tonne for goods travelling between Melbourne and Brisbane.^45^ The reactivation of the Gwabegar line and its extension to Baradine would enable the region's producers to directly access these savings. It would allow for the efficient movement of grain from local silos in Baradine, Bugaldie, and Coonabarabran—all of which formerly shipped by rail—onto the national network, significantly reducing their cost-to-port and enhancing their competitiveness.^2^ The Federal Government's specific identification of the Baradine silos connection as a priority for a business case under the II Program serves as a powerful validation of this latent freight demand.^13^

### **3.2 Passenger Service Demand Modelling: Connecting Communities**

The demand for a regional passenger service is driven by clear demographic trends and documented community needs within the Warrumbungle Shire. The region has a significantly older population than the state average, with a median age of 50 compared to the NSW median of 39.^46^ This demographic profile is typically associated with a higher dependency on public transport for access to essential services, particularly healthcare.

This need is explicitly demonstrated by the existence and function of the Warrumbungle Shire Council's Community Transport service. This service is specifically designed to assist seniors, pensioners, and residents with accessibility needs in travelling to medical appointments in major regional centres like Dubbo.^49^ Furthermore, the community-developed Coonabarabran Action Plan identifies "patient transport" and improved access to "specialist medical services" as high-priority issues for local residents.^50^

A regular, scheduled passenger rail service would directly and effectively address this identified service gap. A daily return service connecting Baradine, Coonabarabran, Binnaway, and other intermediary towns to Dubbo would provide a safe, reliable, comfortable, and accessible alternative to long-distance driving on regional highways or relying on limited coach services.^51^ This link would provide direct access to Dubbo's comprehensive ecosystem of services, including its major hospital, specialist medical clinics, tertiary education providers like TAFE NSW and Charles Sturt University, and its broader transport connections via Dubbo City Regional Airport and NSW TrainLink XPT services to Sydney.

While initial patronage may be modest, the experience of other regional rail revitalisation projects, such as Victoria's Regional Fast Rail program, shows that significant improvements in service frequency, reliability, and comfort can stimulate very strong and sustained ridership growth over time.^52^ The service would cater to a diverse market, including residents accessing essential services, a small but potentially growing number of commuters, and the burgeoning visitor market.

### **3.3 The Visitor Economy: A New Gateway for Regional Tourism**

The Warrumbungle region possesses a unique and powerful combination of world-class tourism assets. The Warrumbungle National Park, renowned for its spectacular volcanic landscape, is also Australia's first designated International Dark Sky Park, offering pristine conditions for astronomy and stargazing.^53^ Adjacent to the park is the Siding Spring Observatory, the nation's premier optical and infrared astronomical research facility, which is a significant attraction in its own right.^55^

Despite the international significance of these attractions, the region's visitor economy is constrained by a critical factor: access. Currently, travel to the region is almost exclusively dependent on private vehicles, as there are no direct public transport options to the National Park or the Observatory.^38^ This presents a significant barrier for potential visitors, particularly those from international markets, interstate, or those who do not have access to a car.

The introduction of a passenger rail service to Coonabarabran would fundamentally change this dynamic, creating a new, sustainable gateway to the region. It would enable the development and marketing of integrated "rail and tour" packages, allowing visitors to travel comfortably from Dubbo (and, by connection, from Sydney) and be met by local tour operators for guided experiences of the Dark Sky Park and Siding Spring. This model has the potential to open up entirely new visitor markets and significantly increase tourist numbers and expenditure in the region.

This potential is recognised in the Warrumbungle Shire's Economic Development and Tourism Strategy, which identifies these catalyst attractions as the foundation upon which to build a more diverse and resilient visitor economy.^57^ The NSW Government has already demonstrated its commitment to this goal by investing \$577,120 in upgrading visitor facilities at Siding Spring, an investment explicitly intended to "grow the visitor economy" and generate flow-on benefits for local accommodation providers, cafes, and restaurants.^58^ A passenger rail service would act as a powerful amplifier, dramatically increasing the return on this and future tourism investments by solving the critical issue of access.

## **4.0 Economic Impact and Return on Investment**

While the project's direct commercial revenue from fares and freight charges is not expected to cover its capital and operational costs, a comprehensive economic assessment reveals a compelling case for public investment. A formal Benefit-Cost Analysis (BCA), which is the standard methodology for evaluating public infrastructure, quantifies a wide range of positive externalities that deliver substantial net benefits to the regional, state, and national economies. The project acts as a powerful economic catalyst, generating significant returns through increased efficiency, safety, investment, and productivity.

### **4.1 Benefit-Cost Analysis: Quantifying the Full Spectrum of Returns**

A narrow financial analysis focused solely on operational profitability would fail to capture the project's true value. The appropriate framework is a BCA that monetises the full spectrum of benefits accruing to the public. The *Kandos to Gulgong Feasibility Study*, a direct analogue for this project, provides a clear precedent, achieving a positive Benefit-Cost Ratio of 1.20 in its most optimistic scenario, indicating that for every dollar invested, \$1.20 in economic benefits would be returned.^21^ A similar outcome is projected for this project, driven by the following quantifiable benefits:

- **Producer Surplus (Freight Cost Savings):** As detailed in Table 3.1, the shift from road to rail for agricultural freight is projected to save producers approximately \$27 per tonne. Based on the region's grain output, this translates into millions of dollars in direct savings annually, increasing the profitability and resilience of the region's primary industry.

- **Reduced Road Infrastructure Costs:** The removal of thousands of heavy vehicle movements from the Newell, Oxley, and Castlereagh Highways will result in a significant reduction in road pavement damage. This will lower the long-term maintenance and rehabilitation costs borne by Transport for NSW and Warrumbungle Shire Council. This benefit is a cornerstone of advocacy for shifting freight to rail.^4^

- **Road Safety Benefits:** A modal shift from road to rail directly improves community safety. Rail freight has a significantly lower accident rate than road freight. Each heavy vehicle crash avoided generates a substantial economic benefit by preventing costs associated with fatalities, serious injuries, emergency services response, and property damage. The Australasian Railway Association (ARA) quantifies that every rail journey replacing a car trip reduces accident costs by around \$1.37, while a single container switched from road to rail between Sydney and Melbourne saves \$109 in accident costs.^44^

- **Environmental Benefits:** Rail transport is significantly more fuel-efficient and produces far fewer greenhouse gas emissions than road transport. The ARA estimates that rail freight produces 16 times less carbon pollution per tonne-kilometre than road freight.^44^ These avoided carbon emissions have a monetisable value under national climate policy frameworks and contribute directly to state and federal emissions reduction targets.

- **Decongestion and Travel Time Savings:** The reduction in truck traffic on regional highways and through town centres improves traffic flow and reduces travel times for all other road users, including local residents, business traffic, and tourists. These time savings represent a real economic productivity gain.

- **Increased Tourism Expenditure:** As outlined in Section 3.3, the new rail service is projected to increase visitor numbers to the region. The resulting increase in spending on accommodation, food, tours, and retail represents a direct injection of new money into the local economy, which can be quantified and included as a project benefit.

### **4.2 Modelling the Impact on the Warrumbungle Shire Economy**

The project will serve as a powerful engine for the local economy, delivering benefits far beyond the rail corridor itself.

**Construction Phase Stimulus:** The construction period will provide a major, immediate economic boost. The Inland Rail project has demonstrated the scale of this impact in other regional NSW communities, awarding over \$140 million in contracts to more than 200 local businesses and supporting almost 1,000 local jobs in the Parkes to Narromine section alone.^11^ A similar injection of capital and employment is anticipated for the Warrumbungle Shire during the construction of this project.

**Operational Phase Employment:** Once complete, the railway will create stable, long-term employment opportunities. These roles will include train drivers, crew, station staff, maintenance and track workers, and administrative personnel, as well as jobs in the expanded logistics and tourism sectors that the railway supports.

**Economic Multiplier Effect:** The direct investment and employment generated by the project will ripple through the local economy. Economic modelling consistently shows that public infrastructure investment has a strong multiplier effect. For every direct job created, additional jobs are supported indirectly in the supply chain (e.g., fuel suppliers, engineering consultants, material providers) and induced through the spending of wages by employees in the local economy (e.g., at retail stores, cafes, and local services). A study on the economic impact of regionalisation in South Australia calculated a total employment multiplier of approximately 1.38, meaning for every 100 direct jobs, another 38 jobs were supported in the wider economy.^61^ Applying a conservative multiplier to this project indicates a substantial and sustained increase in local employment.

This combined economic activity will lead to a significant and lasting increase in the Warrumbungle Shire's Gross Regional Product (GRP), which was valued at \$703.5 million in 2024.^57^

### **4.3 Catalyzing Investment: New Opportunities for Business and Industry**

The certainty and efficiency provided by new rail infrastructure act as a powerful catalyst for private sector investment.

- **Value-Adding Industries:** The NSW Government identifies that Inland Rail has the potential to support employment in agricultural value-adding industries and advanced manufacturing.^12^ By providing reliable and cost-effective freight transport, the project makes the Warrumbungle Shire a more attractive location for investment in facilities such as grain processing plants, stock feed manufacturers, or food packaging operations, which are currently unviable due to prohibitive logistics costs.

- **Logistics and Intermodal Development:** The creation of an efficient railhead at Baradine and improved connectivity at Coonabarabran will create commercial opportunities for private investment in supporting logistics infrastructure. This could include expanded grain storage and handling facilities, containerization services, and freight forwarding businesses to aggregate and manage regional freight movements.

- **Tourism Infrastructure:** As demonstrated in case studies of other former railway towns that have transitioned to tourism economies, permanent transport infrastructure provides the confidence needed for private investment.^62^ The establishment of a regular passenger service would de-risk investment in new accommodation, tour operations, hospitality venues, and other visitor services required to support a growing tourism market.

### **4.4 The Property Value Uplift Effect**

A well-documented and significant benefit of new public transport infrastructure is the positive impact it has on local property values.

Numerous Australian and international studies have quantified this effect. A University of Sydney study found that homes within 800 metres of the new Sydney Metro Northwest stations experienced up to 15% higher price growth than the broader market, while RMIT University research documented an 8.7% premium for properties near Melbourne's Mernda Rail Extension.^64^ This value uplift is driven by improved accessibility, connecting residents more efficiently to employment centres, essential services, and recreational amenities.

For the Warrumbungle Shire, the rail line would make towns like Baradine and Coonabarabran significantly more attractive places to live, particularly for individuals and families who need regular access to the services and employment opportunities in Dubbo. Based on a Victorian Planning Authority analysis of the impact of level crossing removals, which also improved rail service and local amenity, a conservative projection of a **4% to 7% increase in residential property values** for properties within a reasonable walking distance of the reactivated and new stations is anticipated.^64^ This represents a direct and substantial increase in the household wealth of local residents and can help to attract new investment in residential development.

## **5.0 Community and Social Dividend**

Beyond the quantifiable economic returns, the project delivers a profound social dividend. It functions as a powerful instrument of place-based social policy, directly addressing documented challenges related to transport disadvantage, demographic change, and community isolation within the Warrumbungle Shire. This investment in connectivity is an investment in the long-term liveability, resilience, and vibrancy of the region's communities.

### **5.1 Enhancing Liveability and Access to Essential Services**

For many residents in the Warrumbungle Shire, access to services that are taken for granted in metropolitan areas is a significant challenge. The region's demographic profile, with a median age of 50, points to a large and growing cohort of residents who may be less able or willing to undertake long-distance drives for essential appointments.^48^ This reality is reflected in the operational mandate of the Warrumbungle Shire Council's Community Transport service, which exists primarily to help seniors and those with accessibility needs travel to medical appointments in larger regional centres.^49^ The community's own strategic planning has identified "patient transport" as a key priority.^50^

A regular passenger rail service provides a direct, effective, and dignified solution to this transport disadvantage. It offers a safe, reliable, and physically accessible mode of transport that is superior in comfort and amenity to long-distance coach travel, particularly for the elderly or those with mobility challenges.^51^ The establishment of a direct rail link to Dubbo would transform access to the city's comprehensive network of specialist medical facilities, tertiary education campuses, major retail centres, and onward transport connections. This improvement in access to essential services is a fundamental enhancement to the quality of life and liveability for all residents along the corridor, contributing to greater social inclusion and equity.^66^

### **5.2 Addressing Regional Demographic Challenges**

Like many rural areas, the Warrumbungle Shire faces the demographic challenges of an aging population and the out-migration of young people seeking education and employment opportunities. The 2021 Census recorded populations of just 2,387 in Coonabarabran and 586 in Baradine, highlighting the fragility of these communities and the importance of strategies to ensure their long-term sustainability.^68^ Improved transport connectivity is a key lever in addressing these challenges.

By providing a direct link to TAFE and university options in Dubbo, the rail service creates a viable pathway for young people to pursue further education and training while continuing to live in their home communities. This can help to mitigate the "brain drain" that affects so many regional towns. Furthermore, enhanced connectivity is a critical factor in attracting new residents. In an era of increasing remote work and a growing desire for regional lifestyles, the ability to easily access the services and transport links of a major regional hub makes smaller towns like Coonabarabran and Baradine far more attractive propositions for professionals, families, and "lifestyle movers".^70^ This can help to stabilize and grow the local population, broaden the skills base, and increase the local government's rate base, creating a virtuous cycle of renewal.

### **5.3 Fostering Community Resilience and Vibrancy**

The value of this project extends to the very fabric of the community. There is a powerful and well-documented history of community advocacy for rail services in this region, from the original lobbying for the line in the 1870s to the present day.^1^ A recent survey conducted by local advocates found that an overwhelming 95.5% of residents supported the reintroduction of passenger and light freight services.^1^ This groundswell of support demonstrates a high degree of social license for the project. It is not an infrastructure project being imposed upon a community, but one that is actively demanded by it. This level of community ownership is a valuable, non-financial asset that significantly de-risks the project politically and suggests a high level of engagement and utilization once operational.

The physical infrastructure of the railway can also act as a catalyst for town renewal. Railway stations are often historic, centrally located buildings that are points of civic pride.^1^ Their revitalisation as active transport hubs can stimulate adjacent commercial activity, encourage heritage preservation, and create a focal point for community life. Ultimately, the rail line serves to combat the tangible and intangible effects of isolation. It physically connects the region's towns to each other and to the wider state, fostering a greater sense of belonging and shared identity, which is fundamental to long-term community resilience.

## **6.0 Pathway to Delivery: A Strategic Framework**

Moving the Dubbo-Coonabarabran-Baradine Rail Corridor from a strategic concept to an operational reality requires a clear, pragmatic, and actionable pathway. This framework outlines a phased implementation strategy, a diversified funding model, a robust governance structure, and a proactive approach to risk management, providing a credible roadmap for project delivery.

### **6.1 A Phased Implementation and Delivery Strategy**

Given the project's scale and the differing complexities of its components, a phased delivery strategy is recommended to manage risk, build momentum, and deliver early benefits to the community.

- **Phase 1: Dubbo to Coonabarabran Reinstatement.** The initial focus should be on the reinstatement of the existing "brownfield" corridor. This phase represents the lower-risk and lower-cost component of the project. Its delivery would provide immediate and tangible benefits to Coonabarabran, the largest town in the shire, by re-establishing its freight and passenger rail connection to Dubbo. The successful completion of Phase 1 would serve as a powerful proof-of-concept, demonstrating the project's viability and generating political and community momentum for the subsequent phase.

- **Phase 2: Coonabarabran to Inland Rail Extension (via Baradine).** The "greenfield" extension from Coonabarabran through Baradine to the Inland Rail junction near Cumbil Road should proceed following the successful completion of Phase 1.^74^ This sequencing allows for the complex and time-consuming Environmental Impact Statement (EIS) and approvals process for the new corridor to be undertaken concurrently with the construction of Phase 1. This de-risks the overall project by ensuring the significant environmental and land acquisition challenges associated with the Pilliga Forest interface are fully resolved before major capital is committed to this section.

### **6.2 Funding, Governance, and Partnership Models**

The project's strategic alignment with multiple government priorities makes it an ideal candidate for a blended funding model, leveraging contributions from all levels of government and potentially the private sector.

- **Funding Strategy:**

  - **Australian Government:** A formal application should be made for funding through the **Inland Rail Interface Improvement Program**, leveraging the existing business case for the Baradine silos connection. Additional funding could be sought from national infrastructure funds, given the project's role in enhancing the productivity of a national freight corridor.

  - **NSW Government:** The project is a prime candidate for the new **Regional Network East/West Uplift (RNEW) Program**. This program has superseded the former Fixing Country Rail fund and is specifically designed to create a 10-year strategic investment pipeline for regional rail, taking a more holistic and evidence-based approach to network planning.^72^ The project's strong economic and social case aligns perfectly with the RNEW Program's objectives.

  - **Private Sector Partnership:** Opportunities for financial or in-kind contributions from major private sector beneficiaries, such as large agricultural enterprises or grain handling companies that stand to gain significant freight cost savings, should be actively explored.

- **Governance Model:** To ensure effective oversight and stakeholder alignment, the establishment of a dedicated **Project Control Group** is recommended. This body should include senior representatives from key delivery and stakeholder agencies, including Transport for NSW, the Australian Rail Track Corporation (ARTC), Warrumbungle Shire Council, and representatives from local industry (agriculture, tourism) and community advocacy groups. This collaborative structure will facilitate efficient decision-making and ensure the project remains responsive to local needs throughout its lifecycle.

### **6.3 Risk Assessment and Mitigation Plan**

Proactive identification and management of key risks are essential for successful project delivery.

- **Key Risks & Mitigation Strategies:**

  - **Environmental Approval Delays (High Risk):** The approvals process for the Phase 2 extension near the Pilliga Forest is the project's single greatest risk.

    - **Mitigation:** Commence the EIS process as a priority. Engage in early, transparent, and continuous consultation with the NSW Department of Planning, Housing and Infrastructure, the NSW Environment Protection Authority, federal environmental agencies, and local environmental and First Nations groups. Commission comprehensive, independent ecological and hydrological surveys to inform a route selection process that demonstrably avoids and minimises impacts to the greatest extent possible.

  - **Capital Cost Escalation (High Risk):** Large-scale infrastructure projects are notoriously susceptible to cost overruns, as evidenced by the significant escalation in the Inland Rail budget.^33^

    - **Mitigation:** Anchor cost estimates in robust benchmarks from directly analogous projects (e.g., the Kandos-Gulgong study). Incorporate a substantial contingency (e.g., 30%) in the final budget. Employ procurement models, such as fixed-price design-and-construct contracts, that transfer cost risk to the contractor where appropriate.

  - **Low Initial Patronage/Revenue (Medium Risk):** Initial usage of the passenger and freight services may be lower than forecast, impacting early operational viability.

    - **Mitigation:** Design a flexible and efficient operational model from the outset, utilising modern, cost-effective rolling stock (e.g., bi-mode trains). Implement the hybrid freight/passenger model to allow freight revenue to cross-subsidise passenger services. Develop and fund a targeted marketing strategy aimed at the tourism sector to build demand ahead of the service launch.

  - **Political Will and Funding Uncertainty (Medium Risk):** Changes in government or shifting budgetary priorities could jeopardise the long-term funding required for a multi-year project.

    - **Mitigation:** Build a broad, bipartisan coalition of support for the project. This involves continuously reinforcing the project's alignment with the stated strategic priorities of all major parties, including regional development, freight network efficiency, economic productivity, and social equity. Secure multi-year funding commitments through formal inter-governmental agreements where possible.

### **6.4 Final Assessment and Strategic Recommendations**

**Final Assessment:** This feasibility study concludes that the Dubbo-Coonabarabran-Baradine Rail Corridor project is a strategically critical and economically justifiable investment in the future of the Central West region. Despite the significant capital outlay, the project's comprehensive benefits—ranging from enhanced agricultural competitiveness and reduced public road expenditure to transformative improvements in tourism access and social equity—generate a compelling case for public investment. The project effectively leverages the national commitment to Inland Rail, ensuring the benefits of this once-in-a-generation infrastructure project flow directly to the communities and industries of the Warrumbungle Shire.

**Strategic Recommendations:**

1.  **Government Endorsement and Funding Commitment:** It is recommended that the New South Wales and Australian Governments formally endorse the project and enter into an inter-governmental agreement to deliver it via a co-funded, phased model.

2.  **Immediate Commencement of Phase 1 Planning:** It is recommended that Transport for NSW, in partnership with Warrumbungle Shire Council, be immediately tasked and funded to commission a detailed engineering design and Environmental Impact Statement for Phase 1 (Dubbo to Coonabarabran Reinstatement).

3.  **Advance the Baradine Extension Business Case:** It is recommended that the strategic business case for the Baradine silos connection, already identified under the federal Inland Rail Interface Improvement Program, be formally advanced to a full EIS. This process should prioritise a comprehensive analysis of route options to minimise environmental and community impacts.

4.  **Establish a Multi-Stakeholder Delivery Taskforce:** It is recommended that the NSW Minister for Regional Transport and Roads convene a multi-stakeholder taskforce, as outlined in the governance model above, to oversee the development of a detailed operational and business plan for the integrated freight and passenger service, ensuring it is designed to meet the specific needs of the region from day one of operation.
