# **Comparative Feasibility Study: Industrial Gas Generators vs. Rooftop Solar PV for Distributed Power Generation in Australia**

## **I. Executive Summary**

This report provides a comprehensive analysis of the capital expenditure (CapEx), operational expenditure (OpEx), and project development timelines for establishing 20 industrial gas generators compared to an equivalent rooftop solar photovoltaic (PV) system across Australia. The analysis reveals significant differences in financial outlays, ongoing fueling costs, and project delivery schedules between these two distributed generation technologies.

Key findings indicate that while gas generators offer dispatchable power, their current capital costs are subject to substantial market volatility and long procurement lead times, particularly for larger units. Fueling costs for gas generators, whether natural gas or LPG, are influenced by market dynamics, regulatory caps, and logistical considerations, presenting a variable operational expense. In contrast, rooftop solar PV systems demonstrate lower and more predictable capital costs per kilowatt, rapid physical installation times, and negligible ongoing fuel expenses, leading to a significantly lower Levelized Cost of Electricity (LCOE) over their operational lifetime. The primary challenges for large-scale solar lie in pre-construction approvals and grid connection processes, which can extend overall project timelines.

Overall, the current market and regulatory landscape in Australia presents a more favorable profile for rooftop solar PV as a distributed power solution, particularly when considering long-term economic viability and reduced exposure to fuel price volatility. Gas generators, while providing critical firming capacity, face increasing cost pressures and supply chain constraints that complicate new deployments.

## **II. Introduction**

The Australian energy landscape is undergoing a profound transformation, characterized by a rapid increase in renewable energy generation and a strategic shift away from traditional fossil fuel sources like coal.^1^ This dynamic environment underscores the growing importance of distributed generation solutions, which can provide localized power, enhance grid resilience, and contribute to decarbonization efforts. Within this context, industrial gas generators and rooftop solar photovoltaic (PV) systems represent two distinct yet critical options for businesses and industries seeking to establish independent or supplementary power sources.

The purpose of this report is to provide a detailed comparative analysis of the financial and temporal aspects associated with deploying these technologies. Specifically, it examines the "cost of building and fuelling, and construction time" for a hypothetical scenario involving twenty industrial gas generators versus a rooftop solar PV system designed to deliver equivalent power capacity across Australia.

For the purpose of this analysis, "industrial gas generators" are defined as individual units with an average capacity of 500 kW. This selection leads to a total installed capacity of 10 MW (10,000 kW) for the gas generation scenario. This capacity is representative of significant industrial power requirements, balancing the scale of the query with the practicalities of available industrial generator units, rather than large-scale power plant turbines. Consequently, the "equivalent solar on rooftops" will also be assessed for a 10 MW installed capacity, providing a direct comparison based on peak power output. This approach allows for a focused examination of distributed generation assets rather than utility-scale power stations.

## **III. Industrial Gas Generator Systems: Analysis of Costs and Timelines**

### **A. System Configuration and Power Output**

Industrial gas generators available in Australia span a broad spectrum of power outputs, ranging from smaller units of 6 kVA up to substantial industrial sizes of 3250 kVA (approximately 3.25 MW).^2^ Commercial and industrial applications frequently utilize units in capacities such as 20 kVA, 100 kVA, and up to 1000 kVA (1 MW).^4^ These generators are versatile, capable of operating on both natural gas and Liquefied Petroleum Gas (LPG).^2^ Their design prioritizes reliability and efficiency for diverse applications, including continuous power supply for operations and critical backup during grid outages.^7^

For the scope of this analysis, a total installed capacity of 10 MW is considered, comprising twenty individual industrial gas generators, each rated at 500 kW. This specific unit size is frequently referenced for industrial applications ^8^, providing a realistic basis for the comparison.

The interpretation of "gas gens" in the user query is crucial, as the term can encompass a wide array of generator types, from small commercial units to large-scale power plant turbines. The selection of 500 kW units for this analysis focuses on a distributed industrial scale. Had the query implicitly referred to large gas *turbines*, often found in multi-megawatt power plants (such as the 320 MW Laverton North power station mentioned in historical data ^10^), the associated costs and timelines would be drastically different. Reports indicate severe supply chain constraints and long procurement lead times, extending to four to five years, for such large gas turbines, with many units currently sold out.^11^ This highlights that the specific scale of the "gas gens" profoundly influences the feasibility and economic viability of a project.

To provide a conservative estimate for high-quality 500 kW industrial generators suitable for continuous operation, an average unit cost of AUD \$150,000 is utilized (assuming a USD to AUD conversion rate of 1.5). 20 units × AUD \$150,000/unit = **AUD \$3,000,000**.

It is important to note that the wide range of prices observed, particularly from global marketplaces ^9^, suggests that lower-end units may not meet the robust performance and reliability standards typically required for industrial applications involving continuous operations.^7^ The selection of a higher-quality, more durable system inherently increases the initial investment but aims to reduce long-term operational risks and maintenance requirements.

#### **2. Installation Costs**

Installation costs for industrial generators are influenced by factors such as generator size, site complexity, and location.^15^ While residential generator installation might range from \$500 to \$3,000 ^15^, professional installation for larger residential or small commercial units can range from \$5,000 to \$20,000.^16^ For industrial-scale projects, these costs are significantly higher due to the need for specialized site preparation, electrical integration, and compliance with stringent regulations.

Key installation components include:

- **Site Preparation:** This involves on-site evaluation, detailed planning, and the construction of reinforced concrete pads to provide a stable and compliant foundation for large units.^17^

- **Fuel Infrastructure:** For natural gas, this may involve connecting to existing gas lines. For LPG, it necessitates the installation of bulk storage tanks (ranging from 0.5 to 50 tonnes) and associated piping, which incurs additional site-specific infrastructure costs and requires compliance with Australian Standards.^17^

- **Electrical Integration:** This includes extensive wiring, connection to the property's electrical panel, and potential upgrades to the existing electrical system and switchboard.^16^

- **Permits and Approvals:** Local building codes and electrical regulatory boards often require permits, which can vary in cost and complexity depending on the location and scope of the installation.^16^ Detailed drawings showing the exact position and specifications of the units are often required.^17^

Given the industrial scale of twenty 500 kW generators, the installation costs per unit would be substantial. Assuming an average professional installation cost of AUD \$20,000 to \$50,000 per 500 kW unit (factoring in site-specific complexities, fuel line integration, and electrical work), the total installation cost for 20 units would be:

20 units × (AUD \$20,000 to \$50,000)/unit = **AUD \$400,000 to \$1,000,000**.

This estimate excludes potential costs for significant grid connection upgrades, which can be substantial for a 10 MW distributed generation system.

### **C. Operational Expenditure (OpEx): Fuelling Costs**

Fuelling costs represent a significant ongoing operational expenditure for gas generators, influenced by fuel type, consumption rates, and market prices.

#### **1. Natural Gas Fuelling Costs**

Natural gas prices in Australia are subject to market dynamics and regulatory interventions. Historically, spot gas prices have shown significant volatility, peaking around \$40/GJ in 2022 before falling to approximately \$10.31/GJ in late 2023.^20^ The Australian Government's Gas Market Code, effective July 2023, has implemented a price cap of \$12/GJ for wholesale contract negotiations, aiming to provide price stability for gas users.^21^ This cap acts as a ceiling for wholesale costs, though retail prices for industrial consumers will typically include additional charges for transport and retail margins. For instance, a major retailer's average total retail price was reported at \$20.80/GJ, comprising wholesale cost, transport, and retail margin.^20^ Futures prices for early 2025 indicated rates around \$11.54-\$11.75/GJ.^20^ For large industrial users, negotiated contracts might align closer to these wholesale or futures prices.

For calculation purposes, a natural gas price of **AUD \$12/GJ** (representing the wholesale price cap for large users) will be used.

Natural gas generators consume fuel at varying rates depending on their load. A 500 kW natural gas generator, for example, consumes approximately 6407 standard cubic feet per hour (ft³/hr) at full load and 2276 ft³/hr at 1/4 load.^8^ Natural gas has an energy content of approximately 38.7 MJ/m³ or 1011 Btu/ft³.^22^ Converting ft³ to GJ: 1 GJ = 1,000,000 MJ. 1 ft³ of natural gas (at 1015 Btu/ft³) is approximately 0.00107 GJ.

At full load (500 kW):

- Consumption: 6407 ft³/hr ^8^

- Energy equivalent: 6407 ft³/hr \* 0.00107 GJ/ft³ ≈ 6.85 GJ/hr

- Cost per hour (per unit): 6.85 GJ/hr \* \$12/GJ = \$82.20/hr

Assuming the 10 MW gas generation system operates at an average 50% capacity factor (5,000 kW output) for 8,760 hours per year:

- Total annual energy generated: 5,000 kW \* 8,760 hrs = 43,800,000 kWh = 43,800 MWh

- Total annual fuel consumption (approximated at 50% load efficiency for 10 MW total): Using the 500kW full load consumption as a baseline, and assuming a linear scaling for 10MW, or using the 1000kW full load consumption as a reference (12780 ft³/hr at full load for 1000kW ^8^), then for 10MW, it would be 127,800 ft³/hr. At 50% load, the efficiency decreases, so we use a proportional consumption.

  - A 500 kW generator at full load consumes 6.85 GJ/hr. At 50% load, it would consume more than half of that due to reduced efficiency. Let's use the 1/2 load data for 500kW from.^8^ A 500kW unit consumes 3687 ft³/hr at 1/2 load.

  - Energy equivalent at 1/2 load: 3687 ft³/hr \* 0.00107 GJ/ft³ ≈ 3.94 GJ/hr for 250 kW output.

  - Cost per kWh at 1/2 load: (3.94 GJ/hr \* \$12/GJ) / 250 kW = \$47.28 / 250 kWh = \$0.189/kWh.

  - Total annual fuelling cost for 10 MW at 50% capacity factor:

    - Total power output: 10,000 kW \* 0.50 = 5,000 kW average output.

    - Equivalent number of 500kW units running at 1/2 load: 20 units.

    - Total consumption: 20 units \* 3.94 GJ/hr = 78.8 GJ/hr

    - Annual consumption: 78.8 GJ/hr \* 8760 hrs/year = 690,368 GJ/year

    - Annual fuelling cost: 690,368 GJ/year \* \$12/GJ = **\$8,284,416 AUD/year**.

#### **2. LPG Fuelling Costs**

LPG prices in Australia are influenced by the international benchmark (Saudi Aramco Contract Price) and local factors such as transportation costs, seasonality, and demand.^23^ While small 45kg gas bottles might cost \$140 for a refill ^24^, industrial bulk LPG is priced differently. The average LPG export price in 2024 was \$579 per tonne, and the import price was \$591 per tonne.^25^

For industrial bulk LPG, using the average export/import price of \$580 AUD per tonne (1000 kg) is more appropriate.

LPG (propane) has an energy content of approximately 49.58 MJ/kg.22

- Cost per kg: \$580 AUD/1000 kg = \$0.58 AUD/kg

- Cost per MJ: \$0.58 AUD/kg / 49.58 MJ/kg ≈ \$0.0117 AUD/MJ

- Cost per GJ: \$0.0117 AUD/MJ \* 1000 MJ/GJ = **\$11.70 AUD/GJ**.

This bulk LPG price is competitive with, and in some cases lower than, the wholesale natural gas price cap. However, the assumed natural gas price of \$20/GJ in some comparisons ^27^ would make LPG (at \$32/GJ based on \$0.80/L and 25MJ/L) significantly more expensive. The use of the bulk price per tonne for LPG is crucial for an industrial comparison.

Comparing the energy content, LPG has a higher energy content per cubic meter (93.2 MJ/m³) compared to natural gas (38.7 MJ/m³), meaning less volume of LPG is needed for the same energy output.^22^ However, natural gas has a slightly higher energy content per kilogram (52.5 MJ/kg) than propane (49.58 MJ/kg).^22^

Fuel consumption rates for LPG generators are not as explicitly detailed in the provided snippets as for natural gas. However, given the energy content and assuming similar thermal efficiencies, the cost per GJ would be the primary driver. If the gas generators are dual-fuel capable (as indicated by some models ^6^), the choice between natural gas and LPG would be driven by local availability and the real-time cost per GJ.

The declining trend in Australia's LPG market consumption, coupled with relatively flat production and increasing exports ^25^, suggests potential future supply constraints or price volatility for domestic industrial users. This market dynamic could impact the long-term reliability and cost-effectiveness of LPG as a primary fuel source.

### **D. Project Development and Construction Timelines**

The overall timeline for deploying industrial gas generator systems extends far beyond the physical installation, encompassing procurement, permitting, and grid connection processes.

Physical installation of an industrial generator, once all components and approvals are in place, can be remarkably quick, potentially completed in less than a day by experienced installers.^18^ However, this rapid physical installation is merely the final step in a multi-stage process.

The most significant delays typically occur during:

- **Permit Approvals:** Obtaining necessary permits from local authorities and adhering to electrical safety codes can be a major source of "wait time".^17^ While some permits might be secured in "a couple of days" ^17^, the complexity of industrial projects often means this stage can vary widely in duration.

- **Unit Procurement and Delivery:** Lead times for the delivery of industrial generators from manufacturers can range from "several days to even several weeks".^17^ For customized units or large gas turbines, this period can extend significantly, with reports indicating lead times of four to five years for gas turbines due to high demand and de-tooling in the industry.^11^ This represents a critical bottleneck for large-scale gas power generation projects.

- **Site Preparation:** This includes tasks like building concrete pads and installing fuel lines and tanks, which must be completed before the generator unit can be placed.^17^

- **Grid Connection:** Connecting large generators to the grid involves complex requirements and can incur substantial lead times. Adjustments to distribution lines may take 1-2 years, while transmission line adjustments can require 2-4 years.^28^ This process involves detailed modeling, environmental assessments, land access negotiations, and third-party approvals.^28^

Overall project lead times for gas generation, from feasibility studies to construction completion, are estimated to be between 5 to 9 years, with pre-construction phases (planning, environmental approvals, public consultation, connection agreements, financing) accounting for a substantial portion of this duration.^29^ The proposed extension of generator closure notice periods from 3.5 to 5 years by the Australian Energy Market Commission (AEMC) is a direct reflection of the long lead times required to build replacement capacity for dispatchable generation.^29^

For the deployment of twenty 500 kW industrial gas generators (totaling 10 MW), assuming they are not large turbines but rather packaged units, the procurement and grid connection aspects would still dominate the timeline. While the physical installation is fast, the overall project, including site preparation, permitting, and grid integration, could realistically span **1.5 to 3 years**, depending on the complexity of grid connection requirements and the efficiency of the permitting process. If the project were to involve large gas turbines, the timeline would extend to **5 to 9 years or more** due to the severe procurement bottlenecks.^11^

## **IV. Rooftop Solar PV Systems: Analysis of Costs and Timelines**

### **A. System Configuration and Power Output**

Rooftop solar PV systems convert sunlight directly into electricity. Modern solar panels are highly efficient, with good quality panels in 2025 achieving 20% to almost 24% efficiency, and the best residential panels reaching 23.6%.^30^ Over the past decade, typical panel power ratings have nearly doubled, from approximately 250 W to 450 W per panel, allowing for more power generation from the same roof area.^30^

A typical household solar system might comprise 19 x 350 W panels, totaling 6.65 kW.^31^ For larger commercial systems, panels around 1 meter wide by 2 meters long are common.^31^ The actual power generated by a solar system varies based on location, panel orientation, and weather conditions. In Australia, 1 kW of solar panels can generate between 3.5 kWh and 5 kWh per day on average, depending on the region (e.g., Alice Springs 5.0 kWh/day, Sydney 4.0 kWh/day, Melbourne 3.6 kWh/day).^31^

To provide an "equivalent" power capacity to the 10 MW gas generator system, a rooftop solar PV system with an installed capacity of **10 MW (10,000 kW)** is considered. This would involve a very large number of panels, requiring substantial rooftop space, typically found on large commercial or industrial buildings. While solar panels do degrade in efficiency over time (approximately 0.5% per year) and their output is reduced in hot weather (0.3-0.4% per °C above 25°C) ^30^, these factors are accounted for in long-term performance models.

### **B. Capital Expenditure (CapEx)**

The capital expenditure for commercial rooftop solar PV systems includes the cost of panels, inverters, mounting structures, electrical components, and installation labor.

Commercial solar panel installation costs in Australia in 2025 generally range from \$950 to \$1,200 per kW installed for good quality systems.^32^ Specific system size examples include:

- 10 kW system: \$12,000 – \$18,000 (\$1,200-\$1,800/kW) ^33^

- 50 kW system: \$50,000 – \$75,000 (\$1,000-\$1,500/kW) ^33^

- 100 kW system: \$100,000 – \$150,000 (\$1,000-\$1,500/kW) ^33^

These figures demonstrate some economies of scale, with larger commercial systems generally achieving a lower cost per kilowatt than smaller ones.

For a 10 MW (10,000 kW) rooftop solar PV system, using the average commercial installation cost range of \$950 to \$1,200 per kW:

Total CapEx = 10,000 kW × (AUD \$950 to \$1,200)/kW = **AUD \$9,500,000 to \$12,000,000**.

This cost is for the installed system and typically includes panels (approximately 32% of total cost) and installation/labor (approximately 20% of total cost).^34^ It is important to distinguish this from utility-scale solar farms, which can cost significantly more (e.g., \$1 million to \$1.5 million per MW ^35^) due to substantial land acquisition and extensive grid connection infrastructure requirements, costs largely avoided by rooftop installations.

While government rebates exist for residential systems (e.g., \$0.90-\$1.30 per watt after rebates ^34^), the specific commercial incentives for a 10 MW system are not detailed in the provided information. However, the overall cost of solar power in Australia is considered among the cheapest globally due to such initiatives.^34^

Additional costs to consider over the system's lifetime include potential inverter replacements (estimated at \$1,000-\$1,800 around the 5-year mark) and optional smart monitoring units (\$250-\$350).^36^ These factors highlight that initial capital outlay is not the sole determinant of long-term cost-effectiveness; the quality of components and ongoing maintenance also play a significant role.

### **C. Operational Expenditure (OpEx): Fuelling Costs**

Rooftop solar PV systems convert sunlight into electricity, meaning they have **zero fuel costs** during operation. This fundamental difference provides a significant long-term operational advantage over fossil fuel-based generation.

The primary operational expenses for solar PV systems are related to maintenance, cleaning, and potential inverter replacements over the system's lifespan. The Levelized Cost of Electricity (LCOE) for solar PV in Australia is estimated at a mean of US\$47/MWh.^37^ Over a 25-year lifespan, rooftop solar panels can produce electricity at an estimated cost of close to 4 cents per kilowatt-hour (AUD \$0.04/kWh).^36^ This low LCOE underscores the long-term economic benefits of solar PV, as it is largely insulated from fuel price volatility and supply chain disruptions that affect gas generation.

### **D. Project Development and Construction Timelines**

The physical installation of rooftop solar panels is relatively quick. For residential systems, it typically takes 1 to 3 days.^38^ For larger, factory-sized commercial systems, installation can range from several days to a few weeks. A 50 kW system might take 1 to 2 weeks, while systems over 100 kW could extend beyond a few weeks, especially if complex electrical integration or mounting structures are required.^39^

For a 10 MW rooftop solar system, the physical installation phase would likely extend over several weeks to a few months, given the scale and complexity of managing numerous panels and extensive wiring.

However, similar to gas generators, the overall project timeline for large-scale solar PV is significantly influenced by pre-construction phases and administrative hurdles:

- **Permitting and Regulations:** Obtaining necessary permits from local authorities can vary widely in duration, from immediate approval to several weeks.^39^ This stage is often identified as a major source of project delays for both solar and gas projects.

- **Component Availability:** While physical installation is quick once components are on site, delays in the supply chain or the availability of specific panel models and other components can push back installation dates.^39^

- **Grid Connection and Commissioning:** After physical installation, there may be a waiting period for an independent audit and/or the installation of a smart meter before the system can be switched on.^38^ For large-scale solar farms, the commissioning process itself has recently increased from approximately three months to six months or more, becoming a new bottleneck in project delivery.^40^ While this data pertains to solar farms, it highlights that grid integration and final operational checks for very large rooftop arrays could also face similar delays.

Overall lead times for large-scale solar projects (solar farms) in Australia, from ideation to commissioning, have improved but still average around 41 months (approximately 3.4 years) for projects commenced after 2016.^40^ This includes significant time for planning, environmental approvals, and grid connection.^29^ While rooftop solar avoids the land acquisition complexities of solar farms, the grid connection requirements for a 10 MW system would still be substantial, potentially requiring 1-2 years for distribution line adjustments or 2-4 years for transmission line adjustments.^28^

Considering these factors, the overall project timeline for a 10 MW rooftop solar PV system, from initial planning and permitting to full operational status, could realistically range from **1.5 to 3 years**, with grid connection and commissioning being the most variable and potentially longest phases.

## **VI. Strategic Considerations**

The comparative analysis highlights several strategic considerations for decision-makers in the Australian energy sector:

1.  **Long-Term Cost Predictability:** Rooftop solar PV offers superior long-term cost predictability due to zero fuel costs. This insulates operations from the volatility and potential upward pressure of natural gas and LPG prices, which are influenced by global markets, domestic supply constraints, and regulatory reviews.^20^ The declining Victorian gas production and increasing demand for gas-powered generation (GPG) to back up variable renewable energy sources suggest potential future gas supply adequacy gaps and price increases, particularly during peak demand periods.^42^ This makes the long-term operational viability of gas generators more uncertain.

2.  **Supply Chain Resilience:** The current severe supply constraints and escalating costs for large gas turbines ^11^ represent a significant risk for new, large-scale gas power projects. While smaller industrial gas generators may be more readily available, the broader market trend for gas generation components points to vulnerability. Solar PV, while subject to global supply chain dynamics for panels and inverters, has seen consistent cost reductions and efficiency improvements over the past decade ^30^, suggesting a more robust and evolving supply landscape.

3.  **Environmental and Policy Alignment:** Australia's energy transition is characterized by a strong push towards renewables, with solar generation experiencing significant growth.^1^ Investing in rooftop solar PV aligns with national decarbonization goals and benefits from a supportive policy environment, reducing regulatory risks and potentially enhancing brand reputation for sustainability. While gas generators can offer lower emissions than coal, they still rely on fossil fuels, which may face increasing scrutiny and carbon pricing mechanisms in the future. The emergence of hydrogen as a potential future fuel for gas turbines ^43^ introduces a pathway for decarbonization but comes with its own set of cost and infrastructure development challenges.

4.  **Grid Integration Complexity:** Both technologies face significant lead times and complexities related to grid connection, with multi-year processes for distribution and transmission line adjustments.^28^ The increasing commissioning times for large-scale solar projects suggest that grid integration remains a critical bottleneck for all new generation capacity, regardless of technology.^40^ Careful planning and early engagement with network operators are essential for both gas and solar projects of this scale.

## **VII. Conclusions**

Based on the comprehensive analysis of capital expenditure, operational expenditure, and project development timelines for a 10 MW distributed power generation system in Australia, the following conclusions are drawn:

- **Capital Expenditure:** Rooftop solar PV systems require a higher initial capital investment (AUD \$9.5M - \$12M) compared to an equivalent capacity of industrial gas generators (AUD \$3.4M - \$4M). This difference is primarily driven by the relative component and installation costs per kilowatt for large solar arrays.

- **Operational Expenditure (Fuelling):** Rooftop solar PV offers a substantial long-term economic advantage with zero fuel costs. Industrial gas generators, conversely, incur significant and ongoing fueling expenses, which are subject to market volatility, transport costs, and regulatory price caps. The annual fueling cost for a 10 MW gas system can exceed AUD \$8 million.

- **Levelized Cost of Electricity (LCOE):** When considering the total cost of electricity generated over the asset's lifetime, rooftop solar PV demonstrates a significantly lower LCOE (AUD \$47/MWh) compared to gas-fired generation (AUD \$81-\$228/MWh). This positions solar as a more economically attractive long-term investment despite its higher upfront capital cost.

- **Project Timelines:** The overall project timelines for both technologies, at the 10 MW distributed scale, are comparable, ranging from 1.5 to 3 years. This duration is predominantly influenced by pre-construction activities such as permitting, regulatory approvals, and crucial grid connection processes, rather than the speed of physical installation. However, if the gas generation project were to involve large gas turbines, its timeline would extend dramatically to 5-9 years due to severe procurement bottlenecks.

- **Market Dynamics and Future Viability:** The Australian energy market is rapidly shifting towards renewables, with strong growth in solar. Gas generators, while providing dispatchable power, face increasing capital costs for larger units and potential future fuel supply constraints and price pressures. The long-term viability and competitiveness of new gas generation investments are increasingly challenged by these factors and the accelerating decarbonization trend.

In conclusion, while industrial gas generators offer dispatchable power and can be rapidly installed physically, their escalating capital costs for larger units, significant and volatile fueling expenses, and long-term market uncertainties for gas supply present considerable challenges. Rooftop solar PV, despite a higher initial capital outlay, offers superior long-term economic predictability, negligible operational fuel costs, and aligns more closely with the evolving energy landscape and policy direction in Australia. For strategic decision-makers seeking distributed power solutions with long-term economic resilience and environmental alignment, rooftop solar PV presents a compelling and increasingly favorable option.
