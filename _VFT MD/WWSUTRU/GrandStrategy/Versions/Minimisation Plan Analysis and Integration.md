# The Minimisation Plan: An Integrated Assessment of the Sino-Russian Multi-Domain War Against the West

## Executive Summary

This report synthesizes and substantiates the 'Minimisation Plan' theory, a multi-decade, coordinated grand strategy by the People's Republic of China (PRC) and the Russian Federation to dismantle the post-Cold War, US-led international order.^1^ The ultimate objective is to systematically erode the power and influence of the United States and its democratic allies, replacing the current unipolar system with a multipolar framework more favorable to authoritarian models of governance.^2^ This assessment integrates diplomatic, military, economic, and information warfare intelligence to present a unified strategic picture, arguing that a series of seemingly disparate global events are, in fact, interconnected components of this coherent plan.^1^

The strategy is executed through four interconnected pillars. The foundational pillar, developed from 2001 to 2021, involved the deliberate forging of a shared anti-hegemonic worldview, creating the ideological and institutional architecture for long-term cooperation through non-Western forums like the Shanghai Cooperation Organisation (SCO) and BRICS.^1^ The second pillar, activated in 2022, is the Ukraine Gambit, wherein Russia acts as a strategic "battering ram" to challenge NATO and drain Western resources, while simultaneously executing a brutal demographic 'reset' to eliminate a generation of potential domestic opposition and ensure long-term regime security.^1^ The third pillar is the Taiwan 'bait', a cost-imposing strategy designed to engineer Western strategic exhaustion by using calibrated military pressure to compel the United States into a state of perpetual, high-cost over-commitment in the Indo-Pacific.^1^ The fourth and final pillar is the construction of a 'digital fortress' in China, a project to achieve information autarky, thereby creating an unassailable domestic platform from which to execute the plan's more aggressive phases.^1^

The core Sino-Russian axis is actively co-opting strategic partners to amplify its efforts and create multiple fronts. This report details the integration of North Korea as a critical munitions supplier and secondary provocateur, and Iran as a key military-technical partner whose economy is being insulated from Western sanctions by China.^1^ It also provides a nuanced analysis of Israel's complex position, arguing that its necessary strategic balancing act between the US, Russia, and China inadvertently serves the plan's information warfare objectives by acting as a "hypocrisy amplifier".^1^

A central component of the strategy is a sophisticated information war aimed at executing a "Reputation Flip," designed to permanently damage the moral authority of the United States and portray it as the true "objective evil" on the world stage.^1^ This report provides a detailed investigation into the weaponization of the Jeffrey Epstein scandal as the primary mechanism for this objective, analyzing its use as leverage against key political figures and as the focal point of Russian and Chinese state-sponsored propaganda.^1^ The analysis concludes that the war in Ukraine, escalating tensions over Taiwan, persistent North Korean provocations, and coordinated disinformation campaigns like QAnon are not isolated crises. They are interconnected fronts in a single, integrated campaign of ideological and economic warfare.^1^ The primary implication for the United States and its allies is the urgent need to recognize this integrated threat and to develop an equally integrated, long-term, and multi-domain counter-strategy that addresses the military, economic, technological, and ideological dimensions of this competition with the same strategic patience and coherence demonstrated by the architects of the Minimisation Plan.^1^

## Section 1: A Coherent Grand Strategy - The Four Pillars of the Minimisation Plan

The actions undertaken by the People's Republic of China and the Russian Federation since the turn of the century represent the constituent parts of a coherent, patient, and multi-domain grand strategy.^2^ This strategy, termed the 'Minimisation Plan,' is aimed at systematically eroding the power and influence of the United States and its democratic allies.^1^ The causal links between the plan's four pillars—ideological convergence, Russian strategic sacrifice in Ukraine, the engineering of US strategic exhaustion via Taiwan, and the construction of Chinese information autarky—are too consistent, logically sequential, and mutually reinforcing to be dismissed as coincidence.^1^ The timeline reveals a clear strategic progression from preparation to active confrontation, executed with a shared vision for a new global order.^1^

### 1.1 Foundations of Convergence: Forging an Anti-Hegemonic Worldview (2001-2021)

The strategic alignment between Beijing and Moscow is not a recent phenomenon born from the crisis in Ukraine, but the culmination of a two-decade project to build the ideological, diplomatic, and institutional foundations for a sustained challenge to the US-led international order.^1^ This foundational period was characterized by the deliberate codification of a shared opposition to a "unipolar" world, the creation of alternative multilateral forums to legitimize this worldview, and the initiation of practical military and economic cooperation designed to test both the partnership's efficacy and the West's response.^2^

The formal basis of the modern Sino-Russian partnership was established on July 16, 2001, with the signing of the Treaty of Good-Neighborliness and Friendly Cooperation.^2^ While ostensibly a document to finalize post-Soviet border demarcations, its strategic significance was far greater. It represented the first formal, post-Cold War codification of a shared strategic outlook, establishing a partnership that implicitly countered the perceived unilateralism of the United States, which at the time was the undisputed global power.^1^ This legal instrument laid the groundwork for what would become an increasingly "special relationship".^2^

Almost concurrently, this shared worldview was given its first institutional expression. In June 2001, China and Russia, along with several Central Asian states, established the Shanghai Cooperation Organisation (SCO).^2^ While its public mandate focused on combating terrorism, separatism, and extremism, the SCO's geopolitical function quickly evolved into a platform for challenging US influence in their "common adjacent regions".^1^ This became explicit at the 2005 SCO summit in Astana, where member states issued a joint declaration directly condemning "unipolar" policies and calling for a timetable for the withdrawal of Western military forces from Central Asia.^1^

Over the subsequent two decades, this narrative was consistently reinforced and expanded through a series of joint statements issued within the SCO and the burgeoning BRICS (Brazil, Russia, India, China, South Africa) forum. These communiques developed a persistent and sophisticated rhetorical challenge to the existing global order, repeatedly calling for the establishment of a "multipolar," "democratic, fair and rational political and economic international order".^1^ The language used is critical; it frames their challenge not as a revisionist power grab, but as a necessary reform to make global governance more "just, equitable... representative, legitimate, democratic and accountable".^1^ This narrative is designed to appeal to a broader "Global South" constituency, positioning China and Russia as the leaders of a movement to reform institutions like the United Nations and the Bretton Woods system.^1^

The institutional architecture of the SCO and BRICS was not merely an alternative to Western institutions but a vehicle for ideological replication. The language used in their communiques reveals a consistent focus on "non-interference," "state sovereignty," and opposition to "color revolutions".^1^ This language is a direct external projection of their domestic authoritarian governance models. They are not just creating a political bloc; they are attempting to reshape international norms to make the world safe for their internal political systems. The expansion of these organizations to include countries like Iran is not just about building a bigger coalition; it's about creating a critical mass of states for whom this authoritarian-friendly interpretation of sovereignty is appealing. They are building an ecosystem where their model of governance is normalized and validated on the world stage, creating a powerful ideological counterweight to liberal democracy.^1^

The ideological convergence was paralleled by tangible steps in military and economic cooperation. The first major test of military interoperability came in August 2005 with the "Peace Mission 2005" exercise. Conducted under the SCO framework, its scale, involving nearly 9,000 troops and strategic assets like Tu-95MS strategic bombers, and its focus on capabilities like amphibious landings, were vastly in excess of what was required for counter-terrorism and clearly oriented toward a conventional conflict.^1^ On the economic front, the partnership began laying the groundwork for an alternative financial architecture through de-dollarization efforts, which gained momentum after the 2014 Crimea sanctions.^1^ By establishing bilateral currency swap lines and reducing the share of US dollars in their reserves, they began building a parallel economic system shielded from the reach of US sanctions—a foundational capability that would prove critical in the decade to come.^1^

### 1.2 The Ukraine Gambit: Russia's Demographic Reset and Strategic Subordination

The full-scale invasion of Ukraine in February 2022 marks the transition of the Sino-Russian partnership from a phase of ideological preparation to one of active, kinetic confrontation with the West.^1^ This conflict is not merely an isolated act of Russian aggression but a calculated, multi-purpose phase of the broader Minimisation Plan. For Russia, the war serves a crucial dual function: externally, it acts as a battering ram against the NATO alliance; internally, it functions as a brutal tool of social engineering designed to ensure long-term regime security.^1^ For the partnership, it cements Russia's role as the subordinate junior partner, absorbing the immediate costs of confrontation while allowing China to preserve its strength.^1^

The timing of the invasion was not coincidental. It occurred just weeks after Vladimir Putin and Xi Jinping issued their landmark February 4, 2022, joint statement, declaring a partnership with "no limits".^1^ This declaration was the strategic green light for Russia's military action, providing the ideological justification by framing the invasion as a preemptive strike against NATO expansion and a Western-backed "color revolution"—a narrative consistently amplified by Chinese state media.^1^

While the external objectives are clear, a central tenet of the Minimisation Plan theory is that the conflict's staggering human cost serves a deliberate internal purpose for the Putin regime.^1^ Russia entered the 2020s in a state of demographic crisis, with a declining birth rate and a significant "deficit of men," who constituted only 46% of the population.^1^ The war has weaponized this crisis. The conflict has been prosecuted with a near-total disregard for human life, resulting in an estimated over one million Russian casualties (killed and wounded).^1^ This has been compounded by the emigration of 600,000 to 800,000 predominantly young, educated, and urban men fleeing conscription.^1^ Combined, this represents the removal of approximately 5% of the entire Russian male population between the ages of 20 and 40.^1^

This seemingly self-destructive outcome appears irrational, particularly when juxtaposed with Putin's frequent calls for boosting the Russian birth rate.^1^ However, this contradiction is resolved when the objective is understood not as the strengthening of the Russian nation, but as the indefinite preservation of the ruling regime. A large cohort of young, potentially disaffected men represents a classic revolutionary threat. The war physically eliminates this threat on an industrial scale.^1^ The result is a future Russia that is smaller, weaker, and poorer, but also more politically quiescent and therefore more governable.^1^

This strategic calculus appears to have assigned Russia the role of a "battering ram," a deliberate division of labor wherein Moscow absorbs the immediate costs of confrontation to the benefit of the broader partnership.^1^ Russia, the weaker partner with a declining demographic and less dynamic economy, is assigned the "kinetic" role. It expends its national vitality to achieve the shared goal of tying down and exhausting NATO.^1^ This act of strategic self-immolation serves China's long-term interests profoundly. A Russia that has permanently crippled its own demographic and intellectual potential cannot realistically pivot back to the West, even in a post-Putin era. It becomes locked into a subordinate relationship, guaranteeing Beijing a secure strategic rear and a reliable supply of natural resources for decades to come, transforming Russia's "demographic reset" into a massive strategic gain for China.^1^

The war and subsequent Western sanctions have cemented Russia's strategic subordination to China. Cut off from Western markets, Moscow became almost entirely dependent on Beijing for its economic survival. Bilateral trade surged to \$245 billion in 2024, but in a structurally imbalanced way that formalizes Russia's role as a raw material appendage to China's industrial economy.^1^ Russia has been forced to cede monetary sovereignty through the rapid "yuanization" of its economy, with the yuan's share in its National Wealth Fund rising to 60%.^1^ Militarily, the dynamic has reversed; Russia's defense industry, crippled by sanctions and combat losses, is now increasingly dependent on China for critical dual-use components like microelectronics to sustain its war effort.^1^ This strategic division of labor allows China to preserve its economic power and continue its own military modernization unhindered, while Russia expends its national strength to weaken the Western alliance.^1^

### 1.3 The Taiwan 'Bait': Engineering Western Strategic Exhaustion

The third pillar of the Minimisation Plan involves the deliberate use of Taiwan as a focal point to engineer a state of perpetual, high-cost military and political over-commitment from the United States and its allies.^1^ This strategy, which escalated dramatically following the 2022 invasion of Ukraine, functions as a form of economic and industrial attrition. By calibrating a constant stream of military pressure and psychological warfare below the threshold of open conflict, China, with Russian support, forces the West into a reactive posture that drains immense financial resources, strains defense-industrial capacity, and exacerbates internal political divisions.^1^

The period following Russia's invasion of Ukraine witnessed a sharp, quantifiable escalation in joint Sino-Russian military activities in the Indo-Pacific. Of the approximately 90 joint exercises conducted since 2003, a staggering 26—nearly one-third of the total—have occurred since February 2022.^1^ The nature of these exercises has evolved from earlier counter-terrorism drills to a clear emphasis on naval and aerial power projection in strategically sensitive regions directly relevant to a Taiwan conflict scenario.^1^ This military pressure is underpinned by the People's Liberation Army's (PLA) decades-long modernization program, which has focused on developing the capabilities required to annex Taiwan.^1^ The PLA Navy (PLAN) is now the world's largest by number of ships, projected to grow to 435 platforms by 2030, compared to the U.S. Navy's projected 294.^1^ The core of this modernization is a formidable Anti-Access/Area-Denial (A2/AD) capability, designed specifically to deter or defeat US military intervention with systems like the DF-21D and DF-26 "carrier-killer" missiles.^1^

The "Taiwan Bait" is a cost-imposing strategy that creates a feedback loop of escalating commitment and internal division within the US. China and Russia conduct joint exercises near Taiwan at a relatively low operational cost.^1^ The US perceives this as a credible threat and responds with massive, multi-billion-dollar investments in the Pacific Deterrence Initiative and arms sales to Taiwan.^1^ This massive expenditure becomes a point of political contention within the US, fueling debates about resource allocation and empowering isolationist voices, thus weakening domestic political consensus.^1^ Simultaneously, the constant threat signaling erodes Taiwanese confidence in the US, with polling showing over 40% of Taiwanese believe the US is unlikely to intervene.^1^ This forces the US to make even more visible and costly displays of support to reassure its partner. This creates a self-reinforcing cycle: low-cost actions by the axis trigger high-cost reactions from the US, which in turn create political friction and psychological doubt, achieving the goal of strategic exhaustion without firing a shot.^1^

The final dimension of this strategy is psychological. The relentless military pressure creates a "damned if you do, damned if you don't" dilemma for the United States. Within Taiwan, the sustained intimidation has fostered growing skepticism regarding US reliability as a security partner. A Brookings Institution report analyzing polls from early 2025 found that 40.5% of Taiwanese respondents held a negative view of the United States, a sharp increase from 24.2% in July 2024.^1^ This growing distrust is a strategic victory for Beijing, as a population's "will to fight" is significantly correlated with its belief that external military assistance will be forthcoming.^1^ By sowing doubt, China directly undermines Taiwanese morale. The ultimate goal is to induce a state of strategic paralysis, making the defense of Taiwan seem simultaneously too costly for the United States to undertake and too unreliable for Taiwan to count upon, thereby creating the conditions for a collapse of resistance.^1^

### 1.4 The Digital Fortress: China's Pursuit of Information Autarky and Domestic Control

The fourth pillar of the Minimisation Plan is the methodical construction of a technologically sealed and socially compliant information environment within China.^1^ This "digital fortress" is a critical prerequisite for the strategy's more aggressive phases, designed to ensure absolute domestic stability and immunize the population against external influence, particularly during a major crisis like a Taiwan contingency.^1^ This project is not merely an instrument of domestic repression; it is a system of strategic inoculation. By preventing exposure to external narratives while simultaneously removing the desire to seek them, the CCP creates a closed-loop information ecosystem. This transforms the population from a potential liability in a conflict—susceptible to US information operations—into a unified, mobilized strategic asset, ideologically primed

*before* a conflict begins. This internal consolidation is the necessary foundation for executing aggressive foreign policy without fear of domestic collapse, a key lesson learned from observing Western divisions during past conflicts.^1^

#### 1.4.1 Technical Isolation

For two decades, China's Great Firewall (GFW) operated primarily on a "blacklist" model, which could be circumvented by savvy users employing Virtual Private Networks (VPNs).^1^ In late 2021, this paradigm shifted. The GFW deployed a new capability to passively detect and block fully encrypted traffic in real-time, marking a strategic evolution from blocking known forbidden content to blocking any traffic that is unidentifiable.^1^ This represents a move from a reactive "blacklist" approach to a proactive "whitelist" model, where all communication is implicitly forbidden unless it uses a state-sanctioned, inspectable protocol.^1^ This has been accompanied by the emergence of regional, provincial-level firewalls, first documented in Henan Province in August 2023, which employ more aggressive and volatile blocking policies, allowing Beijing to "beta test" stricter measures on a regional population.^1^

The ultimate defensive measure is the ability to sever China's connection to the global internet entirely. On August 20, 2025, for over an hour, the GFW blocked all traffic on TCP port 443, the standard for secure HTTPS connections, effectively cutting off access to the vast majority of the foreign internet.^1^ This event strongly suggests a deliberate test of a "digital kill switch." In the opening hours of a major conflict, the ability to achieve total information autarky would be a decisive advantage. It would instantly neutralize any attempt by the United States to conduct information warfare operations aimed at the Chinese populace, ensuring that the Chinese Communist Party's (CCP) domestic narrative is the only one heard by its 1.4 billion citizens.^1^

#### 1.4.2 Social Engineering and Ideological Inoculation

The final layer of the digital fortress is social, not technical. It involves engineering a compliant society through the Social Credit System (SCS) and coordinated disinformation. As of 2025, the SCS has evolved into a two-tiered framework focused primarily on corporate compliance and the enforcement of legal judgments, such as travel bans on individuals with unpaid fines.^1^ The primary focus is the Corporate SCS, which rates businesses on regulatory compliance to enforce the Party's will on the economy.^6^ The Individual SCS is a more fragmented system of blacklists used mainly to enforce legal judgments rather than policing minor social behaviors.^1^ The system is designed to automate 'individual responsibility' and ensure loyalty to the CCP.^8^

This is complemented by a sophisticated disinformation campaign, conducted in close alignment with Russia, that seeks to discredit democratic institutions and promote a worldview favorable to authoritarianism.^1^ This external propaganda serves a crucial internal purpose: the long-running Patriotic Education Campaign. Initiated in 1991 and intensified after the 1989 Tiananmen Square protests, this is a massive ideological re-education effort targeting youth.^9^ It systematically replaced the old class-struggle narrative with a new "victimization narrative" that blames the West for China's "hundred years of humiliation" and positions the CCP as the sole defender and savior of the nation.^9^ Under Xi Jinping, this has evolved to target "evil others," fostering a new generation of nationalists who are intolerant of any criticism of the CCP regime and hostile to Western values.^12^ By consistently portraying the West as decadent and declining, the CCP creates an information environment that reinforces its domestic narrative of superiority and justifies its iron-fisted control, ensuring the stability of the regime as it executes its long-term strategy.^1^

## Section 2: The Expanding Axis - Co-opting Strategic Partners

The Minimisation Plan is not limited to the bilateral Sino-Russian relationship. Its architects are actively expanding their coalition by co-opting other state actors who share a common interest in challenging the US-led order.^1^ These strategic partners, while possessing their own distinct motivations and objectives, serve to amplify the pressure on the West, create new fronts of conflict, and provide critical logistical and military support.^1^ This section analyzes the roles of North Korea, Iran, and the complex case of Israel within this expanding authoritarian axis.

### 2.1 The Northern Arsenal: North Korea's Role as Munitions Supplier and Secondary Front

North Korea has been integrated into the Minimisation Plan as a key logistical and strategic asset. Its primary functions are to serve as a vast munitions depot for Russia's high-attrition war in Ukraine and to act as a persistent provocateur in Northeast Asia, creating a secondary front that drains US and allied military resources and strategic attention.^1^

Since Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine, its cooperation with North Korea has escalated dramatically. Driven by Russia's desperate need for artillery ammunition and North Korea's need for food, energy, and advanced military technology, the relationship has transformed into a comprehensive military partnership.^1^ In the fall of 2024, North Korea began deploying military personnel to Russia, with estimates suggesting as many as 15,000 troops had been sent by April 2025 to fight against Ukraine under Russian command.^1^ Even more critically, North Korea has become Russia's primary foreign arms supplier, shipping millions of artillery shells and other munitions. South Korean intelligence estimated that by June 2024, North Korea had sent at least 10,000 containers to Russia, capable of holding nearly 5 million artillery shells.^1^

In exchange for this support, Russia has provided North Korea with vital economic aid and, more alarmingly, advanced military technology that could significantly enhance Pyongyang's nuclear and missile programs. This includes potential assistance with military satellites, nuclear submarines, and ICBMs, effectively underwriting the advancement of a key US adversary and creating a direct threat to the US homeland.^1^ This burgeoning alliance was formalized in June 2024 with the signing of a "Treaty on Comprehensive Strategic Partnership," which includes a mutual defense clause pledging assistance if either country comes under attack, reviving a Cold War-era security guarantee.^1^

North Korea also functions as a strategic "spoiler" and a "second front" by design. Its cycle of provocations, which included an unprecedented 68 missile tests in 2022, is not a series of random acts by a rogue state but a calibrated asymmetric warfare strategy.^1^ These actions are calculated to serve the broader axis goal of strategic exhaustion. Each missile test forces the United States, South Korea, and Japan into a high-cost defensive posture, requiring joint military exercises, the deployment of advanced missile defense systems, and the diversion of high-level strategic bandwidth that would otherwise be focused on China, which the US National Defense Strategy identifies as the primary "pacing threat".^1^ This creates a strategic dilemma: every asset dedicated to deterring North Korea is an asset not available for a potential Taiwan contingency. The axis is thus using North Korea to fix a significant portion of US Indo-Pacific forces in Northeast Asia, preventing them from being flexibly deployed to Southeast Asia and thereby weakening the overall US posture against China. This classic military strategy of fixing an enemy force in one location to achieve superiority elsewhere perfectly complements the "Taiwan Bait" strategy, creating a multi-front resource drain.^1^

### 2.2 The Middle Eastern Flank: Iran's Integration into the Anti-Western Bloc

Parallel to the developments in Northeast Asia, the Minimisation Plan has actively cultivated a southern flank in the Middle East through the strategic integration of the Islamic Republic of Iran.^1^ Iran's long-standing anti-American posture, its growing military-industrial capabilities, and its strategic location make it a natural and crucial partner for the Sino-Russian axis. This partnership provides military support to Russia, deepens economic ties with China, and solidifies an anti-US front in a region of vital strategic importance.^1^

The military dimension of the Iran-Russia partnership has evolved significantly, moving from tactical cooperation to strategic interdependence. Their collaboration was forged and tested during their joint intervention in the Syrian civil war beginning in 2015, where they worked in concert to prop up the Assad regime and roll back US influence.^1^ This battlefield experience laid the groundwork for a deeper relationship that became critical following Russia's 2022 invasion of Ukraine. Facing heavy losses and the limitations of its own defense industry under sanctions, Russia turned to Iran for critical military hardware. Tehran became a key supplier of low-cost but effective unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs) and other munitions, which have been used extensively against Ukrainian infrastructure and military targets.^1^ This role reversal—from Iran being a customer of Russian arms to a key supplier—demonstrates a new level of military-technical cooperation and mutual reliance.^1^

Economically, Iran has been drawn firmly into China's orbit, a process accelerated by the pressure of US sanctions. The centerpiece of this relationship is the Iran-China 25-year Cooperation Program, signed in March 2021.^1^ This sweeping agreement reportedly involves up to \$400 billion in Chinese investment into Iran's energy, banking, telecommunications, and transportation sectors. In exchange, China is guaranteed a steady and heavily discounted supply of Iranian oil, securing its energy needs while providing a vital economic lifeline that allows Tehran to withstand the "maximum pressure" campaign of Western sanctions.^1^ This agreement firmly integrates Iran into China's Belt and Road Initiative, strengthening its economic orbit and reducing the leverage the US holds over Iran.^1^

The Iran-China-Russia triangle creates a "sanction-proof" economic and military ecosystem that mutually reinforces each member against US pressure. The United States and its allies impose heavy sanctions on Russia for its actions in Ukraine and on Iran for its nuclear program and regional activities.^1^ China, seeking to secure its energy supply and expand its influence, signs a massive 25-year deal with Iran, effectively nullifying the impact of sanctions by providing a guaranteed market and investment.^1^ Iran, its economy stabilized by China, is then able to provide Russia with low-cost but effective military hardware like drones, helping Russia sustain its war effort against the West.^1^ Russia, in turn, provides diplomatic cover for Iran at the United Nations and engages in military-technical cooperation.^1^ This creates a closed loop where Chinese economic power insulates Iran, which then provides military support to Russia, which in turn provides strategic support to Iran. This trilateral relationship is designed to systematically undermine the primary tool of Western foreign policy: economic sanctions.^1^

### 2.3 The Israeli Dilemma: Navigating the Strategic Triangle and the 'Hypocrisy Amplifier' Role

Israel occupies a uniquely complex and precarious position within the framework of the Minimisation Plan. Unlike North Korea or Iran, it is not a willing or ideological partner in the anti-Western axis.^1^ As a key US ally and a liberal democracy, its fundamental strategic interests align with the West. However, the geopolitical realities of the Middle East have forced it into a delicate and continuous balancing act between the United States, Russia, and China.^1^ This policy of strategic ambiguity, born of necessity, inadvertently makes Israel a useful, if unwitting, tool for the Minimisation Plan's information warfare objectives, functioning as what one of the foundational documents terms a "Hypocrisy Amplifier".^1^

Israel's relationship with China has deepened significantly since the early 2000s, driven by mutual economic interests. China views Israel as a vital source of advanced technology and a strategic node in its Belt and Road Initiative.^1^ This has led to a surge in bilateral trade, which reached \$22.7 billion in 2024, and major Chinese investments in Israeli infrastructure, including the new port in Haifa.^1^ For Israel, China represents a massive market and a way to diversify its economy away from an over-reliance on the US and Europe.^1^ This burgeoning economic partnership, however, has created friction with Washington, which has raised concerns about the security risks of Chinese involvement in critical infrastructure and access to sensitive technology.^1^

Simultaneously, Israel has cultivated a pragmatic and surprisingly cordial relationship with Vladimir Putin's Russia.^1^ This relationship is not based on shared values but on a cold calculation of security interests, primarily centered on Syria. Following Russia's military intervention in 2015, Moscow became the de facto power broker controlling Syrian airspace.^1^ To continue its campaign of striking Iranian and Hezbollah targets within Syria, Israel requires Russia's tacit approval and active deconfliction to avoid a direct clash between their forces.^1^ This critical security requirement has forced Israel to adopt a position of official neutrality on the war in Ukraine. Despite pressure from Washington, Jerusalem has refused to send lethal military aid to Kyiv or join in the sanctions regime against Moscow, a stance that has been a persistent source of frustration for the US and its Western allies.^1^

This delicate balancing act inadvertently serves the Minimisation Plan's narrative goals. Israel's need to maintain a working relationship with Russia creates a visible fissure in the Western coalition against Moscow's aggression.^1^ More potent, however, is its role as a "Hypocrisy Amplifier".^1^ Unwavering US diplomatic and military support for Israeli policies that draw condemnation from other parts of the international community provides a powerful tool for Russian and Chinese propaganda. This allows them to frame the US-led "rules-based international order" as a sham, arguing that the US applies principles of international law and human rights selectively to its allies and adversaries.^1^ This narrative is particularly effective in the Global South, where it resonates with historical grievances and erodes US moral authority.^1^ Israel's necessary strategic choices thus become a tool for the axis to weaken the very order Israel relies on for its long-term survival.^1^

## Section 3: The War of Attrition - Quantifying the Western Resource Drain

A central pillar of the Minimisation Plan is a sophisticated war of economic and industrial attrition. The strategy is not to defeat the United States in a direct, head-to-head military conflict, but to induce a state of "strategic exhaustion" by compelling it to expend vast resources across multiple fronts simultaneously.^1^ By creating and sustaining credible threats in both Europe and the Indo-Pacific, the Sino-Russian axis forces the US into a reactive posture of perpetual, high-cost military readiness.^1^ This section quantifies this resource drain, analyzing the immense financial commitments to the Pacific theater and applying the theory of "imperial overstretch" to assess the long-term sustainability of the current US global posture.

### 3.1 The Pacific Commitment: A Cost-Benefit Analysis of the US Response

The calibrated military pressure from the Sino-Russian axis has elicited a direct and massive financial response from the United States, creating a significant and sustained resource drain that exemplifies a successful cost-imposing strategy.^1^ A direct causal link can be drawn between the post-2022 escalation in the Taiwan Strait and the substantial growth of US defense budgets allocated to the region.^1^

The primary instrument for this spending has been the Pacific Deterrence Initiative (PDI). The Pentagon's budget request for the PDI grew from \$9.1 billion for Fiscal Year 2024 to a record \$9.9 billion for FY2025.^1^ Demonstrating a sense of urgency that often outpaces the executive branch, the US Congress has consistently authorized funding far exceeding these requests, approving a total of \$14.71 billion for the PDI in FY2024—over 60% more than the Pentagon had asked for.^1^ This funding surge is directly mirrored in the rapid acceleration of US arms transfers to Taiwan. From the beginning of 2022 to early 2025, the Biden administration notified Congress of over \$8.3 billion in potential Foreign Military Sales (FMS).^1^ Recognizing the lengthy timelines of the FMS process, Congress also authorized unprecedented new avenues for arms transfers, including \$1.5 billion via Presidential Drawdown Authority (PDA) and the first-ever provision of Foreign Military Financing (FMF) grants.^1^

These specific, Taiwan-focused expenditures are part of a much larger, multi-decade financial commitment. From 2016 to 2019 alone, the Department of Defense spent approximately \$20.9 billion in Japan and \$13.4 billion in South Korea on military salaries, facility construction, and maintenance.^1^ The sustained pressure campaign forces the United States to not only maintain this costly baseline presence but to continually augment it with new, technologically advanced, and extremely expensive capabilities. This dynamic creates a highly asymmetric return on investment for the Sino-Russian partnership; for the relatively low operational cost of conducting joint exercises, they compel the United States to engage in a multi-billion dollar annual cycle of high-end procurement and infrastructure development, a clear strategy of economic attrition.^1^

### 3.2 Strategic Exhaustion and Imperial Overstretch: Assessing the Sustainability of US Posture

The multi-front pressure campaign executed under the Minimisation Plan appears deliberately designed to exploit and accelerate a classic geopolitical vulnerability: "imperial overstretch".^1^ First theorized by historian Paul Kennedy, this concept describes the process by which a great power extends itself beyond its military-economic capabilities, leading to the exhaustion of domestic resources and an eventual decline.^1^ The current US strategic posture, characterized by vast global commitments and simultaneous, large-scale resource allocation to two major conflict theaters, fits this model with alarming precision.^1^

The United States is currently funding massive military aid packages and maintaining a high operational tempo in two separate, major theaters of conflict: supporting Ukraine against Russia in Europe, and deterring China while defending allies in the Indo-Pacific.^1^ This dual commitment is placing unprecedented strain on the US defense budget, industrial base, and political cohesion. The US defense budget is approaching \$1 trillion, and there are increasing calls for allies to raise their own defense spending to as much as 5% of GDP, a target that even the US does not meet and which is seen as unrealistic by many partners.^1^ This financial strain is compounded by a vast network of overseas military commitments, with one estimate citing 737 US bases in 63 foreign countries, leading to concerns that the US military could "drop from exhaustion" from trying to be everywhere at once.^1^

Complementing the primary pressure point of Taiwan, North Korea functions as a secondary but highly effective driver of this resource drain. Its persistent cycle of provocations forces the United States and its key regional allies into a costly and perpetual state of high military alert, diverting resources and strategic focus that would otherwise be directed toward countering China.^1^ The North Korean and Taiwanese fronts are not separate problems but a coordinated pincer movement designed to divide US resources and strategic focus. The US National Defense Strategy identifies China as the single "pacing challenge," meaning an optimal US strategy would concentrate its most advanced assets on this primary threat.^1^ However, North Korea's constant belligerence fixes a significant portion of US Indo-Pacific Command's forces in Northeast Asia, preventing Washington from concentrating its power and weakening its overall posture against China.^1^

The Minimisation Plan operates as a form of geopolitical jujutsu, using America's own global commitments and alliance system against it to induce self-inflicted strategic exhaustion. The US global network of alliances, long considered a core strategic strength, also represents a network of obligations that must be met to maintain US credibility.^1^ By simultaneously creating or exacerbating major crises in two core theaters (Europe via Ukraine, Asia via Taiwan/North Korea), the axis forces the US to honor these commitments at a massive scale, concurrently. This stretches the US defense-industrial base thin, as seen in the competition for munitions and air defense systems between the Ukrainian and Taiwanese fronts. It strains the US budget, forcing difficult trade-offs between domestic priorities and foreign commitments, which in turn fuels political polarization.^1^ The plan thus weaponizes America's greatest strategic asset—its alliance network—turning it into a massive liability and a driver of its own overstretch, all while the primary architects in Beijing conserve their strength for the long-term competition.^1^

### 4.1 The Anatomy of a Russian Information Weapon: From Pizzagate to QAnon

Russian information warfare campaigns, particularly the evolution from Pizzagate to QAnon, serve as a key component of the Minimisation Plan's objective to sow internal division and erode institutional trust in the West. The evolutionary path began with Pizzagate in 2016. The conspiracy was seeded using emails stolen from the Democratic National Committee by Russian military intelligence (GRU) and released via WikiLeaks.^15^ On forums like 4chan, anonymous users and state-backed actors falsely claimed that innocuous terms in the emails were code for a pedophile ring operating out of a Washington, D.C., pizzeria.^15^ This localized conspiracy served as the direct predecessor to QAnon, which emerged in October 2017.^18^ QAnon expanded the Pizzagate narrative into a global mythology, alleging a worldwide cabal of satanic, cannibalistic elites against whom President Donald Trump was secretly waging war.^19^

These campaigns rely on several core tactics. The most crucial is the exploitation of a "kernel of truth." The entire edifice of Pizzagate was built on the verifiable fact that John Podesta's hacked emails contained the word "pizza".^15^ Similarly, the Epstein narrative is anchored to the undisputed public record of his associations with powerful people.^1^ This factual anchor makes the broader, more outlandish claims seem more plausible to a susceptible audience. This raw material is then subjected to narrative laundering and amplification by state actors like the St. Petersburg-based Internet Research Agency (IRA). The IRA created thousands of fake American personas and groups across all major social media platforms to push divisive content, testing messages and then overwhelming the information space with those that resonated most strongly, focusing on polarizing issues like race, immigration, and anti-government conspiracies to "spread distrust towards the candidates and the political system in general".^17^

Unlike traditional top-down propaganda, these campaigns foster participatory disinformation. QAnon's cryptic "drops" encouraged followers to "do their own research," creating an interactive and self-reinforcing community of "bakers" who wove the "breadcrumbs" into the overarching narrative.^15^ This makes adherents active participants in, and evangelists for, the disinformation campaign. The ultimate strategic goal, as evidenced by the focus of these operations, was not merely to support one candidate but to degrade trust in democratic institutions, exacerbate social divisions, and ultimately "make democracy impossible in the U.S." by fueling a potential "homegrown coup".^17^

### 4.3 The Leverage Hypothesis and the Strategic Value of Ambiguity

A central and highly explosive element of the Epstein narrative is the allegation that his sex trafficking network was not merely a criminal enterprise but a sophisticated intelligence-linked blackmail operation designed to compromise powerful individuals.^1^ While this claim remains in the realm of speculation, its strategic value for the Minimisation Plan is not contingent on it being true. Its power lies in the unresolvable ambiguity and the deep-seated institutional distrust that the controversy generates.^1^

The official position of the United States government is unequivocal. A July 2025 memo from the Department of Justice and the FBI, released after a review of all evidence, stated that the investigation found "no incriminating 'client list'" and "no credible evidence...that Epstein blackmailed prominent individuals as part of his actions".^1^ The memo also reaffirmed the official finding that Epstein's death was a suicide.^1^

However, this official account is directly contradicted by a number of sources that allege an intelligence connection. The book *One Nation Under Blackmail* posits that Epstein's operation was the culmination of a long-standing nexus between US and Israeli intelligence agencies and organized crime, which perfected the use of sexual blackmail as a tool of control.^1^ This theory has been amplified by figures like former CIA officer John Kiriakou, who publicly described Epstein as a "textbook example of an access agent," adding that "the accusation is he was an access agent for the Israelis".^1^ In response, former Israeli Prime Minister Naftali Bennett issued a categorical denial, stating with "100% certainty" that Epstein never worked for the Mossad and that the accusation is a "lie being peddled by prominent online personalities".^1^

This creates a perfect scenario for adversarial information operations. There is no definitive, publicly trusted arbiter of truth. The DOJ's official statement is dismissed by a large segment of the population as a cover-up, a narrative that is reinforced by the very existence of these counter-claims from seemingly credible sources and by the administration's own prior stoking of conspiracy theories.^1^ For Russia and China, this outcome is ideal. They do not need to prove that the blackmail operation happened. They only need to amplify the controversy, the conflicting accounts, the official denials, and the counter-denials. This process itself achieves the strategic goal: it erodes public trust in the DOJ, the FBI, the media, and the entire political establishment. The debate itself becomes the weapon that weakens the target society from within.^1^
