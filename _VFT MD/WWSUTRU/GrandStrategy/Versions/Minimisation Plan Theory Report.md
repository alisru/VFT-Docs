# The Minimisation Plan: An Integrated Assessment of the Sino-Russian Multi-Domain Strategy to Erode Western Power

## Executive Summary

This report provides a comprehensive assessment of the 'Minimisation Plan' theory, which posits that the actions of the People's Republic of China (PRC) and the Russian Federation since the turn of the century are not isolated events but components of a coherent, multi-decade grand strategy. The ultimate objective of this strategy is to systematically erode the power and influence of the United States and its democratic allies, replacing the post-Cold War, US-led international order with a multipolar framework more favorable to authoritarian models of governance. This assessment integrates diplomatic, military, economic, and information warfare intelligence to argue that a series of seemingly disparate global events are, in fact, interconnected components of this coherent plan.

The strategy is executed through four interconnected pillars. The foundational pillar, developed from 2001 to 2021, involved the deliberate forging of a shared anti-hegemonic worldview, creating the ideological and institutional architecture for long-term cooperation through non-Western forums like the Shanghai Cooperation Organisation (SCO) and BRICS. The second pillar, activated in 2022, is the Ukraine Gambit, wherein Russia acts as a strategic "battering ram" to challenge NATO and drain Western resources, while simultaneously executing a brutal demographic 'reset' to eliminate a generation of potential domestic opposition and ensure long-term regime security. The third pillar is the Taiwan 'bait', a cost-imposing strategy designed to engineer Western strategic exhaustion by using calibrated military pressure to compel the United States into a state of perpetual, high-cost over-commitment in the Indo-Pacific. The fourth and final pillar is the construction of a 'digital fortress' in China, a project to achieve information autarky, thereby creating an unassailable domestic platform from which to execute the plan's more aggressive phases.

The core Sino-Russian axis is actively co-opting strategic partners like North Korea and Iran to amplify its efforts and create multiple fronts of pressure. A central component of the strategy is a sophisticated information war aimed at executing a "Reputation Flip," designed to permanently damage the moral authority of the United States. The analysis concludes that the war in Ukraine, escalating tensions over Taiwan, persistent North Korean provocations, and coordinated disinformation campaigns are not isolated crises but interconnected fronts in a single, integrated campaign. The primary implication for the United States and its allies is the urgent need to recognize this integrated threat and to develop an equally integrated, long-term, and multi-domain counter-strategy.

## Section 1: The Foundations of Convergence: Forging an Anti-Hegemonic Worldview (2001-2021)

The strategic alignment between the People's Republic of China and the Russian Federation is not a recent, opportunistic development but the culmination of a two-decade project to build the ideological, diplomatic, and institutional foundations for a sustained challenge to the US-led international order. This foundational period was characterized by the deliberate codification of a shared opposition to a "unipolar" world, the creation of alternative multilateral forums to legitimize this worldview, and the initiation of practical military and economic cooperation designed to test both the partnership's efficacy and the West's response.

### 1.1 Codifying Opposition: The 2001 Treaty and the Rise of the SCO and BRICS

The formal basis of the modern Sino-Russian partnership was established on July 16, 2001, with the signing of the Treaty of Good-Neighborliness and Friendly Cooperation.^1^ While ostensibly a document to finalize post-Soviet border demarcations and foster economic ties, its strategic significance was far greater.^4^ The treaty represented the first formal, post-Cold War codification of a shared strategic outlook, establishing a partnership that implicitly countered the perceived unilateralism and hegemonic posture of the United States.^2^ Critically, it included provisions for immediate consultation in the event of a security threat to either party and a firm Russian affirmation of the "One China" policy regarding Taiwan, laying the legal and diplomatic groundwork for what would become an increasingly "special relationship".^3^

Almost concurrently, this shared worldview was given its first institutional expression. In June 2001, China and Russia, along with several Central Asian states, established the Shanghai Cooperation Organisation (SCO).^8^ While its public mandate focused on combating the "three evils" of terrorism, separatism, and extremism, the SCO's geopolitical function quickly evolved into a platform for challenging US influence in their "common adjacent regions".^10^ This became explicit at the 2005 SCO summit in Astana, where the member states issued a joint declaration that directly condemned "unipolar" policies and called for a timetable for the withdrawal of Western military forces from Central Asia.^13^ This was a clear and early signal of a coordinated intent to roll back the US security presence from their strategic backyard.

Over the subsequent two decades, this narrative was consistently reinforced and expanded through the burgeoning BRICS (Brazil, Russia, India, China, South Africa) forum. These communiques developed a persistent and sophisticated rhetorical challenge to the existing global order, repeatedly calling for the establishment of a "multipolar," "democratic, fair and rational political and economic international order".^15^ The language used in these documents is critical; it frames their challenge not as a revisionist power grab, but as a necessary reform to make global governance more "just, equitable... representative, legitimate, democratic and accountable," a narrative designed to appeal to a broader "Global South" constituency.^15^

The durability of this ideological project stems from a deep-seated commonality between the two regimes that transcends mere geopolitical convenience. Both the Chinese Communist Party and the Kremlin-led Russian state share a profound "hostility to dissent, deep suspicion of Western interference and a strong desire to impose tighter controls over their own societies".^7^ Their emphasis on principles of absolute state sovereignty and non-interference in internal affairs, a cornerstone of SCO and BRICS declarations, is a direct external projection of their domestic governance models.^5^ This shared interest in making the world safe for their respective authoritarian systems provides a powerful and enduring basis for their strategic partnership, insulating it from transient disagreements and allowing for the pursuit of long-term, coordinated objectives.

### 1.2 Early Manifestations: Joint Military Exercises and De-Dollarization Efforts

The ideological convergence was paralleled by initial, tangible steps in military and economic cooperation that served as practical probes of the partnership's potential. The first major test of their military interoperability came in August 2005 with the large-scale joint exercise dubbed "Peace Mission 2005".^18^ Conducted under the SCO framework and officially designated as a counter-terrorism drill, its scale and composition signaled a far grander strategic ambition. The exercise involved nearly 9,000 troops, with Russia deploying strategic assets such as Tu-95MS strategic bombers and Tu-22M3 long-range bombers, and China contributing destroyers and amphibious assault forces.^18^ The capabilities demonstrated—including amphibious landings and long-range air strikes—were vastly in excess of what would be required for typical counter-terrorism operations and were clearly oriented toward a conventional, state-level conflict.^18^

On the economic front, the partnership began to lay the groundwork for an alternative financial architecture, insulated from Western, and particularly US, control. These de-dollarization efforts began more than a decade ago but gained significant momentum after the United States and its allies imposed sanctions on Russia for its 2014 annexation of Crimea.^19^ Recognizing their shared vulnerability to US financial leverage, the two countries began to systematically reduce their reliance on the US dollar.^20^ This involved a multi-pronged approach: establishing bilateral currency swap lines to facilitate trade in their national currencies, progressively reducing the share of US dollars in their central bank reserves, and promoting the use of the renminbi (RMB) in regional trade.^19^ While these initial steps were modest in their global impact, they represented a deliberate and strategic effort to build a parallel economic system shielded from the reach of US sanctions—a foundational capability that would prove critical in the decade to come.^19^

## Section 2: The Ukraine Gambit: Russia's Demographic Reset and Strategic Subordination (2022-Present)

The full-scale invasion of Ukraine in February 2022 marks the transition of the Sino-Russian partnership from a phase of ideological preparation to one of active, kinetic confrontation with the West. This conflict is not merely an isolated act of Russian aggression but a calculated, multi-purpose phase of the broader Minimisation Plan, serving both Russia's internal regime security and the partnership's broader strategic goals of weakening the West.

### 2.1 The "No Limits" Declaration as an Activation Trigger

The timing of the invasion was not coincidental. It occurred just weeks after Vladimir Putin and Xi Jinping met in Beijing on the eve of the Winter Olympics and issued their landmark February 4, 2022, joint statement, declaring a partnership with "no limits" and "no 'forbidden' areas of cooperation".^7^ This declaration was the critical inflection point, serving as the strategic green light for Russia's military action. The manifesto was explicit in its aims, with the two leaders pledging to "stand against attempts by external forces to undermine security and stability in their common adjacent regions" and, crucially, to "oppose color revolutions".^7^ This language directly provided the ideological justification for Russia's invasion, which the Kremlin framed as a preemptive strike against NATO expansion and a Western-backed "neo-Nazi" regime in Kyiv—a narrative that Chinese state media and officials would consistently amplify.^23^

### 2.2 War as a Tool of Internal Control: The Demographic 'Reset'

While the external objectives of the war are clear, a central tenet of the Minimisation Plan theory is that the conflict's staggering human cost serves a deliberate internal purpose for the Putin regime. Russia entered the 2020s in a state of severe demographic crisis, characterized by a declining birth rate (1.41 children per woman in 2024, far below the replacement rate of 2.1), high mortality, and a significant "deficit of men".^24^ Since the collapse of the Soviet Union, Russia has experienced long periods of net population loss, a trend exacerbated by the COVID-19 pandemic and now the war.^24^

The war in Ukraine has weaponized this crisis. The conflict has been prosecuted with a near-total disregard for human life, resulting in what credible Western intelligence estimates suggest are over one million Russian casualties (a combination of killed and wounded) by mid-2025.^29^ This immense battlefield attrition has been compounded by a massive wave of emigration, with an estimated 600,000 to 900,000 predominantly young, educated, and urban men fleeing the country to escape conscription.^32^

This seemingly self-destructive outcome appears deeply irrational, particularly when juxtaposed with Putin's frequent public calls for boosting the Russian birth rate.^27^ However, this contradiction is resolved when the objective is understood not as the strengthening of the Russian nation, but as the indefinite preservation of the ruling regime. A large cohort of young, potentially disaffected men, particularly in urban centers, represents a classic revolutionary threat to any authoritarian government. The war in Ukraine physically eliminates this threat on an industrial scale, either through battlefield attrition or forced exile. The result is a future Russia that is smaller, weaker, and poorer, but also more politically quiescent, demographically older, and therefore more governable.

### 2.3 Cementing Subordination: Economic and Military Dependency on China

The war and the subsequent imposition of unprecedented Western sanctions have served as the final mechanism for cementing Russia's strategic subordination to China. Cut off from Western markets and financial systems, Moscow became almost entirely dependent on Beijing for its economic survival.^7^ This dependency has manifested in three critical domains.

First, bilateral trade has exploded, but in a structurally imbalanced way that formalizes Russia's role as a raw material appendage to China's industrial economy. Total trade surged to \$245 billion in 2024, more than doubling the level of 2020.^39^ However, this trade is not between equals. Russia's exports consist almost entirely of fossil fuels and other natural resources like crude petroleum, petroleum gas, and coal, while China's exports are dominated by high-value manufactured goods such as cars, electronics, and industrial machinery.^39^

Second, Russia has been forced to cede a significant degree of its monetary sovereignty through the rapid "yuanization" of its economy. In a desperate move to find an alternative to the dollar and euro, Moscow dramatically increased its use of the Chinese yuan. By 2023, the yuan had become the most popular currency on the Moscow Exchange, and its share of Russian trade settlements surged to nearly 40% by early 2024.^39^ This shift provides Russia with an essential economic lifeline, but it comes at the cost of tying its financial system to the decisions of the People's Bank of China.^38^

Third, the immense attrition of Russian military hardware in Ukraine has fundamentally altered the military-technical relationship. For decades, China's military modernization was heavily reliant on the purchase and reverse-engineering of advanced Russian weapon systems.^45^ The war has reversed this dynamic. Russia's defense industry, crippled by sanctions and combat losses, is now increasingly dependent on China for critical dual-use components, such as microelectronics, to sustain its war effort.^37^

This complete role reversal is not an unintended consequence of the war but a planned and accepted outcome. Russia, as the weaker partner with a declining demographic and less dynamic economy, has been assigned the high-cost "kinetic" role of a strategic "battering ram." It expends its national vitality to achieve the shared goal of tying down and exhausting NATO. This act of strategic self-immolation serves China's long-term interests profoundly. A Russia that has permanently crippled its own demographic and intellectual potential cannot realistically pivot back to the West, even in a post-Putin era. It becomes locked into a subordinate relationship, guaranteeing Beijing a secure strategic rear and a reliable supply of natural resources for decades to come. Russia's "demographic reset" is thus transformed into a massive strategic gain for China.

## Section 3: The War of Attrition: Engineering Western Strategic Exhaustion

A central pillar of the Minimisation Plan is a sophisticated war of economic and industrial attrition. The strategy is not to defeat the United States in a direct military conflict, but to induce a state of "strategic exhaustion" by compelling it to expend vast resources across multiple fronts simultaneously. By creating and sustaining credible threats in both Europe and the Indo-Pacific, the Sino-Russian axis forces the US into a reactive posture of perpetual, high-cost military readiness, designed to exploit and accelerate the classic geopolitical vulnerability of "imperial overstretch."

### 3.1 The Taiwan 'Bait': Calibrating Military Pressure in the Indo-Pacific

The period following Russia's invasion of Ukraine witnessed a sharp, quantifiable escalation in the tempo and nature of joint Sino-Russian military activities in the Indo-Pacific. This shift was not merely symbolic but represented a deliberate campaign to create a credible and persistent military threat against Taiwan. Analysis of joint military exercises reveals a dramatic increase in frequency and a fundamental change in focus. Of the over 90 joint exercises conducted since 2003, nearly one-third have occurred since February 2022, with 2024 setting a new record with 11 distinct joint exercises.^47^ Crucially, the nature of these exercises has evolved from the earlier focus on land-based counter-terrorism drills to a clear emphasis on naval and aerial power projection.^47^ These drills are increasingly conducted in strategically sensitive maritime regions directly relevant to a Taiwan conflict scenario, including the Yellow Sea, the East China Sea, and the South China Sea.^47^

This campaign of military pressure is underpinned by the People's Liberation Army's (PLA) decades-long modernization program, which has focused primarily on developing the capabilities required to annex Taiwan. The PLA Navy is now the world's largest naval force by number of ships, and the core of this modernization effort is the development of a formidable Anti-Access/Area-Denial (A2/AD) capability, designed specifically to deter or defeat US military intervention.^45^

### 3.2 The Expanding Axis: Integrating North Korea and Iran to Create Multi-Front Pressure

The Minimisation Plan is not limited to the bilateral Sino-Russian relationship. Its architects are actively expanding their coalition by co-opting other state actors who share a common interest in challenging the US-led order.

North Korea has been integrated as a key logistical and strategic asset. Its primary functions are to serve as a vast munitions depot for Russia's high-attrition war in Ukraine and to act as a persistent provocateur in Northeast Asia. Since late 2023, North Korea has become Russia's primary foreign arms supplier, shipping an estimated 12 million 152mm artillery shells and other munitions.^49^ Pyongyang has also deployed thousands of troops to fight for Russia in Ukraine.^53^ In exchange for this support, Russia has provided vital economic aid and advanced military technology, a partnership formalized in the June 2024 "Treaty on Comprehensive Strategic Partnership," which includes a mutual defense clause.^55^

Parallel to developments in Northeast Asia, the plan has cultivated a southern flank through the strategic integration of Iran. The military dimension of the Iran-Russia partnership was forged during their joint intervention in Syria and became critical after 2022, with Tehran becoming a key supplier of low-cost but effective unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs) to Moscow.^57^ Economically, Iran has been drawn firmly into China's orbit, a process accelerated by the pressure of US sanctions. The centerpiece is the Iran-China 25-year Cooperation Program, signed in March 2021, which reportedly involves up to \$400 billion in Chinese investment into Iran's energy and infrastructure sectors in exchange for a steady supply of discounted Iranian oil.^61^ This trilateral relationship is designed to systematically undermine the primary tool of Western foreign policy: economic sanctions.

Israel occupies a uniquely complex position. As a key US ally, its fundamental interests align with the West. However, geopolitical realities have forced it into a delicate balancing act. Its economic relationship with China has deepened, particularly in the technology and infrastructure sectors.^67^ Simultaneously, its security interests in Syria necessitate a pragmatic relationship and an active deconfliction mechanism with Russia to continue its campaign of striking Iranian and Hezbollah targets.^71^ This balancing act inadvertently serves the plan's narrative goals. Unwavering US support for Israeli policies that draw international condemnation provides a powerful tool for Russian and Chinese propaganda to frame the US-led "rules-based international order" as a sham, eroding US moral authority, particularly in the Global South.

### 3.3 Quantifying the Resource Drain: The Pacific Deterrence Initiative and Imperial Overstretch

The calibrated military pressure from the Sino-Russian axis has elicited a direct and massive financial response from the United States, creating a significant and sustained resource drain. A direct causal link can be drawn between the post-2022 escalation in the Taiwan Strait and the substantial growth of US defense budgets allocated to the region. The primary instrument for this spending has been the Pacific Deterrence Initiative (PDI). The Pentagon's budget request for the PDI grew to a record \$9.9 billion for FY2025.^74^ Demonstrating a sense of urgency, the US Congress has consistently authorized funding far exceeding these requests, approving a total of \$14.71 billion for the PDI in FY2024—over 60% more than the Pentagon had asked for.^75^

This funding surge is directly mirrored in the rapid acceleration of US arms transfers to Taiwan. From the beginning of 2022 to early 2025, the executive branch notified Congress of over \$8.3 billion in potential Foreign Military Sales (FMS) to Taiwan.^76^ Congress also authorized new avenues for arms transfers, including Presidential Drawdown Authority (PDA) and Foreign Military Financing (FMF).^76^ This dynamic creates a highly asymmetric return on investment for the Sino-Russian partnership; for the relatively low operational cost of conducting joint exercises, they compel the United States to engage in a multi-billion dollar annual cycle of high-end procurement and infrastructure development, a clear strategy of economic attrition.

The axis is effectively weaponizing America's greatest strategic asset—its global alliance network—to induce self-inflicted strategic exhaustion. The US network of alliances also represents a network of obligations that must be met to maintain credibility. By simultaneously creating or exacerbating major crises in two core theaters (Europe via Ukraine, Asia via Taiwan/North Korea), the axis forces the US to honor these commitments at a massive scale, concurrently. This stretches the US defense-industrial base thin, as seen in the competition for munitions between the Ukrainian and Taiwanese fronts. It strains the US budget, forcing difficult trade-offs between domestic priorities and foreign commitments, which in turn fuels political polarization. The plan thus operates as a form of geopolitical jujutsu, turning the alliance network into a massive liability and a driver of its own overstretch, all while the primary architects in Beijing conserve their strength.

## Section 4: The Digital Fortress: China's Pursuit of Information Autarky and Domestic Control

The fourth pillar of the Minimisation Plan is the methodical construction of a technologically sealed and socially compliant information environment within China. This "digital fortress" is a critical prerequisite for the strategy's more aggressive phases, designed to ensure absolute domestic stability and immunize the population against external influence. This project is not merely an instrument of domestic repression; it is a system of strategic inoculation. By preventing exposure to external narratives while simultaneously engineering a population that is ideologically primed to support the state, the Chinese Communist Party (CCP) creates a closed-loop information ecosystem. This transforms the population from a potential liability in a conflict—susceptible to US information operations—into a unified, mobilized strategic asset, a necessary foundation for executing aggressive foreign policy without fear of domestic collapse.

### 4.1 Technical Isolation: Hardening the Great Firewall and Rehearsing the 'Kill Switch'

For two decades, China's Great Firewall (GFW) operated primarily on a "blacklist" model, blocking access to specific foreign websites and services.^80^ This system could be circumvented by technologically savvy users employing Virtual Private Networks (VPNs) or encrypted proxy protocols.^81^ In late 2021, this paradigm shifted fundamentally. The GFW deployed a new, more sophisticated censorship capability that allowed it to passively detect and block fully encrypted traffic in real-time.^82^ This upgrade marked a strategic evolution from blocking known forbidden content to blocking any traffic that is unidentifiable or "looks like nothing".^82^ The system no longer needs to know

*what* a user is accessing; it only needs to determine that the traffic is encrypted in a way that prevents inspection. This represents a move from a reactive "blacklist" approach to a proactive "whitelist" model, where all communication is implicitly forbidden unless it uses a state-sanctioned, inspectable protocol.^82^ This has been accompanied by the emergence of regional, provincial-level firewalls, first documented in Henan Province in August 2023, which employ more aggressive and volatile blocking policies, allowing Beijing to "beta test" stricter measures on a regional population.^17^

The ultimate defensive measure for China's information space is the ability to sever its connection to the global internet entirely. Evidence suggests that Beijing has been actively testing this "kill switch" capability. The most significant public event occurred on August 20, 2025, when for over an hour, the Great Firewall blocked all traffic on TCP port 443.^17^ As port 443 is the standard for secure HTTPS connections, this action effectively cut off access to the vast majority of the foreign internet. In the opening hours of a major conflict, such as an invasion of Taiwan, the ability to achieve total information autarky would be a decisive advantage. It would instantly neutralize any attempt by the United States or its allies to conduct information warfare operations aimed at the Chinese populace, ensuring that the CCP's domestic narrative is the only one heard by its 1.4 billion citizens.

### 4.2 Social Engineering: The Social Credit System and Ideological Inoculation

The final layer of the digital fortress is social, not technical. It involves engineering a compliant society through a combination of pervasive monitoring and sustained propaganda. The primary instrument of monitoring is the Social Credit System (SCS). As of 2025, the vision of a single, all-encompassing numerical score for every citizen has not materialized.^83^ Instead, the system has evolved into a more pragmatic, two-tiered framework. The primary focus is now on the corporate SCS, which evaluates businesses on their regulatory compliance, rewarding compliant firms with benefits and punishing non-compliant ones.^83^ The individual SCS is a more fragmented patchwork of regional pilot programs and blacklists, primarily used to enforce legal judgments, such as imposing travel bans on individuals with unpaid court fines, rather than policing everyday social behavior.^83^

This internal control is complemented by the long-running Patriotic Education Campaign, a massive ideological re-education effort targeting youth. Initiated after the 1989 Tiananmen Square protests, this is a systematic campaign to replace old class-struggle narratives with a new "victimization narrative" that blames the West for China's "hundred years of humiliation" and positions the CCP as the sole defender and savior of the nation.^86^ By consistently portraying the West as decadent and declining, the CCP creates an information environment that reinforces its domestic narrative of superiority and justifies its iron-fisted control, ensuring the stability of the regime as it executes its long-term strategy.

## Section 5: The 'Reputation Flip': Ideological Warfare and the Weaponization of Truth

The most ambitious and insidious component of the Minimisation Plan is a sophisticated campaign of ideological warfare designed to execute a global "Reputation Flip." The objective is to systematically dismantle the moral authority of the United States, transforming its global image from the leader of the free world into the "objective evil." This creates the ideological space for a "rehabilitated" Russia and a dominant China to present their authoritarian models as more stable and morally sound alternatives. The primary weapon for this campaign is the Jeffrey Epstein scandal, a controversy whose undisputed facts and unresolvable ambiguities provide the perfect material to create a simple, devastating, and permanent narrative of systemic corruption at the heart of the American establishment.

### 5.1 The Primary Weapon: State Media Weaponization of the Epstein Scandal

An analysis of Russian state-funded media (RT, Sputnik) coverage in 2019 by the German Marshall Fund's Hamilton 2.0 dashboard reveals that the Jeffrey Epstein scandal was their top story.^87^ The saga received more coverage than major geopolitical events like Brexit or the Hong Kong protests, and even more than Russian President Vladimir Putin himself.^87^ This disproportionate focus, on a topic with no direct connection to Russian interests, highlights a clear strategic priority: to paint a negative image of the West.^87^ The coverage consistently promoted narratives designed to erode trust in Western institutions by portraying the "mainstream media" as complicit in a cover-up, casting doubt on official explanations of Epstein's death, and framing the scandal as a metaphor for the systemic corruption and moral decay of the entire Western elite.^87^ This aligns with a broader pattern where Chinese state media has been shown to amplify and launder similar Russian disinformation narratives, such as the baseless conspiracy theory about US-run biological weapons labs in Ukraine.^23^

This creates a perfect scenario for adversarial information operations. There is no definitive, publicly trusted arbiter of truth. The debate itself becomes the weapon that weakens the target society from within. This represents an evolution of *kompromat* from a tool for controlling individuals to a weapon of mass societal demoralization, with the Epstein scandal's unresolvable ambiguity as its perfect fuel. The power of the scandal lies not in a single provable fact, but in the web of documented associations and the *unprovable* but plausible allegations of a deeper conspiracy.
