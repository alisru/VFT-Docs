## Labour Theory of Value (Marx, Ricardo)

**Core claim:** The value of a commodity is determined by the socially necessary labour time required to produce it. Profit is the surplus value extracted from labour above the subsistence wage paid to workers.

**Equations:**

Value of a commodity:
V = SNLT

- V = exchange value of the commodity (expressed in units of socially average labour time)
- SNLT = Socially Necessary Labour Time — the labour time required to produce a commodity under the prevailing average conditions of production, with the average degree of skill and intensity prevalent at the time. Not the actual time any individual worker takes — the social average. A slow worker does not add more value by taking longer.

Price of production (Marx's three-component breakdown):
P = c + v + s

- P = price of the commodity (the money form of its value)
- c = constant capital — the value of means of production consumed in the production process: raw materials used up, wear and tear on machinery, energy consumed. Called "constant" because constant capital does not generate new value — it only transfers its existing value into the product.
- v = variable capital — the wages paid to workers. Called "variable" because this is the component that generates new value beyond itself. The worker is paid v but produces v + s.
- s = surplus value — the unpaid portion of the worker's labour. The worker is paid enough to reproduce their labour power (v) but works longer than that. The additional time worked produces s, which the capitalist appropriates without payment.

Rate of surplus value (exploitation rate):
s' = s / v

- s' = rate of surplus value, also called the rate of exploitation
- s = surplus value (defined above)
- v = variable capital / wages (defined above)
- Interpretation: if s' = 1.0, the worker spends half their time working for their own wage and half producing unpaid surplus. If s' = 2.0, the worker produces twice their wage in unpaid surplus for every unit of wage received.

Rate of profit:
r = s / (c + v)

- r = rate of profit — the return on total capital invested
- s = surplus value appropriated
- c = constant capital invested (machinery, materials)
- v = variable capital invested (wages)
- c + v = total capital outlay
- Interpretation: r measures what the capitalist gets back above their total outlay. Marx's key insight is that r depends entirely on s, which comes entirely from labour — constant capital transfers value but does not create it. Rising organic composition of capital (c/v increasing as machinery replaces workers) tends to reduce r over time — the tendency of the rate of profit to fall.

The fundamental claim: s > 0 always when the worker is paid less than the full value they produce. The gap between v (what labour receives) and (v + s) (what labour produces) is the extraction vector.

**What it correctly identifies:** That labour is systematically undervalued relative to the surplus it generates. That the gap between what labour produces and what labour is paid is where extraction occurs. That the representation layer of neutral exchange conceals a directional extraction vector. These are real Q3 and Q6 observations about how capital accumulates.

**Where it breaks down:**

Single-variable model. Labour time is one input into a multi-variable system. LTV cannot account for why two products requiring identical labour time command radically different prices in practice — scarcity, urgency, renewability, and rarity are not in the model at all.

It treats all labour as homogeneous after socially necessary averaging. This destroys the information carried by urgency differentials and skill scarcity. A surgeon and an assembly line worker may require similar training hours but operate under completely different Reality Tensor conditions.

It has no frame dependence. Buyer and seller are not modelled as agents with distinct urgency vectors. The transaction is treated as a single-frame event rather than a relativistic negotiation between two masses.

It has no boundary condition. LTV has no mechanism for what happens when the extraction reaches a physical ceiling. The Reality Tensor produces a defined breakpoint (n/0 → n+1). LTV predicts revolution as an ideological event rather than a mathematical inevitability.

**Planes operated on:** Q3 (physical labour), Q6 (historical accumulation). Planes absent: Q1 (agent will), Q2 (possibility/substitution), Q5 (urgency mechanics), Q7 (systemic consequence).

**Reality Tensor resolution:** Labour time maps onto the transactional mass terms (P_you, P_other) as one component of need and pressure. Socially necessary labour time is a baseline social pressure scalar (P_soc). Surplus value extraction is the condition where the Lorentz factor is extreme — one party's urgency is near zero (the capitalist who can wait) while the other's approaches maximum (the worker who cannot). LTV correctly identifies the extraction but misattributes its source to labour alone rather than to the urgency differential operating across the full Reality Tensor.

## Neoclassical Supply and Demand (Marshall, Walras)

**Core claim:** Price is determined by the intersection of supply and demand curves at equilibrium. Rational agents with perfect information maximise utility. Markets self-correct toward equilibrium through price signals.

**Equations:**

Equilibrium condition (Walrasian):
Qd(P*) = Qs(P*)

- Qd(P*) = quantity demanded at price P* — the total amount buyers wish to purchase at that price
- Qs(P*) = quantity supplied at price P* — the total amount sellers wish to sell at that price
- P* = the market-clearing equilibrium price — the unique price where Qd = Qs and there is no excess demand or excess supply
- The equation states that equilibrium exists when the market clears: no unsatisfied buyers at that price, no unsold inventory at that price

Marshall's demand function:
Qd = f(P, I, Ps, Pc, T)

- Qd = quantity demanded — units buyers wish to purchase
- P = own price of the good — as P rises, Qd falls (law of demand)
- I = income of buyers — as I rises, Qd rises for normal goods; falls for inferior goods
- Ps = price of substitute goods — goods that can replace this one. As Ps rises, buyers switch to this good, so Qd rises
- Pc = price of complementary goods — goods consumed together with this one (e.g. cars and fuel). As Pc rises, demand for both falls, so Qd falls
- T = consumer tastes and preferences — a catch-all for everything not captured by the other variables, including fashion, habit, and advertising effects

Price elasticity of demand:
PED = (ΔQ/Q) / (ΔP/P)

- PED = price elasticity of demand — measures how sensitive quantity demanded is to a change in price
- ΔQ = change in quantity demanded
- Q = original quantity demanded
- ΔP = change in price
- P = original price
- ΔQ/Q = percentage change in quantity demanded
- ΔP/P = percentage change in price
- If |PED| > 1: demand is elastic — quantity is sensitive to price (luxury goods, goods with many substitutes)
- If |PED| < 1: demand is inelastic — quantity is insensitive to price (necessities, goods with no substitutes — i.e. high Ra in Reality Tensor terms)
- If |PED| = 0: perfectly inelastic — quantity does not change regardless of price. This is the Ra → 1 boundary condition. The neoclassical model has no mechanism for what this means at the system level. The Reality Tensor does.

General equilibrium (Arrow-Debreu):
Σi xi(p) = Σi ωi

- Σi = summation across all agents i in the economy
- xi(p) = demand vector of agent i at price vector p — the bundle of all goods agent i wishes to consume when prices are p
- p = price vector — a list of prices for every good in the economy simultaneously
- ωi = endowment vector of agent i — the bundle of goods agent i owns before trading
- Σi xi(p) = total demand across all agents for each good
- Σi ωi = total supply (endowments) across all agents for each good
- The equation states that at equilibrium price vector p*, total demand equals total supply for every good simultaneously. Excess demand = 0 everywhere. This requires a separate equation for each market, solved simultaneously.

Utility maximisation (consumer):
max U(x) subject to p·x ≤ w

- U(x) = utility function — a mathematical representation of the agent's preferences, mapping consumption bundles to a real number (higher = more preferred)
- x = consumption bundle — a vector listing quantities of each good consumed
- p = price vector — prices of each good
- p·x = inner product of price vector and consumption bundle = total expenditure = Σ(pi · xi)
- w = wealth / budget — the total monetary resources available to the agent
- p·x ≤ w = the budget constraint — total spending cannot exceed wealth
- max U(x) = maximise utility subject to the budget constraint

Lagrangian formulation:
L = U(x) − λ(p·x − w)

- L = Lagrangian — a combined function that incorporates both the objective (U) and the constraint
- λ = Lagrange multiplier — the shadow price of the budget constraint. Interpreted as the marginal utility of an additional unit of wealth. If λ is large, the constraint is binding hard; the agent strongly wants to spend more but cannot.
- (p·x − w) = the constraint expression — equals zero at the optimum when the budget is exactly spent
- First-order conditions: ∂U/∂xi = λ·pi for all i — at the optimum, the marginal utility of each good equals its price times the shadow cost of the budget. The agent buys each good up to the point where the last unit's utility equals its cost in terms of foregone alternatives.

**What it correctly identifies:** That price emerges from the interaction of two parties with different pressures. That scarcity raises price. That increased supply lowers price. These are correct observations about the transactional mass terms and the scarcity variable operating in isolation.

**Where it breaks down:**

Symmetric information assumption. Buyer and seller are modelled as operating in the same reference frame with equivalent information. The Reality Tensor treats them as agents in distinct frames with different urgency vectors — the Lorentz frame divergence is the mechanism that produces the actual negotiated price, not the equilibrium intersection.

**Planes operated on:** Q3 (physical exchange), Q5 (mechanism). Planes absent: Q1 (will and pressure), Q2 (substitution at boundary), Q6 (historical accumulation, renewability), Q7 (systemic consequence).

## Efficient Market Hypothesis (Fama)

**Core claim:** Asset prices reflect all available information. Markets are informationally efficient. Deviations from equilibrium are random walks that self-correct rapidly. Systemic crashes are unforeseeable black swans.

**Equations:**

Efficient price (semi-strong form):
Pt = E[Pt+1 | It] / (1 + r)

- Pt = current asset price at time t
- E[Pt+1 | It] = expected price at time t+1 given the information set It — the market's best forecast of tomorrow's price based on everything known today
- It = the information set at time t — all publicly available information: financial statements, news, macroeconomic data, analyst reports. Semi-strong EMH claims this includes everything public. Strong-form EMH claims it includes insider information too.
- r = risk-adjusted discount rate — the return required by investors to hold this asset given its risk level. Incorporates time preference (money now > money later) and risk premium (uncertain future cash flows are worth less than certain ones).
- 1 + r = the discount factor. Dividing by (1 + r) converts the expected future price into its present value.
- Interpretation: the current price already incorporates all available information about future value. No trading strategy based on public information can consistently beat the market.

Random walk model:
Pt+1 = Pt + εt

- Pt+1 = price at next time period
- Pt = price at current time period
- εt = random shock at time t — the unpredictable component of price change
- εt ~ N(0, σ²) = the shock is drawn from a normal distribution with mean zero and variance σ²
- Mean zero: price changes have no predictable direction — the best forecast of tomorrow's price is today's price
- Variance σ²: the spread of possible outcomes is constant over time (homoskedastic) — an assumption the GARCH model and empirical data both refute
- Independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.): each shock is independent of all previous shocks — markets have no memory. Directly contradicted by volatility clustering in real data.

Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM):
E(Ri) = Rf + βi · (E(Rm) − Rf)

- E(Ri) = expected return on asset i — the return an investor requires to hold this asset
- Rf = risk-free rate — the return on a theoretically riskless asset (typically proxied by short-term government bonds). The baseline return for any investment; the minimum threshold before taking on risk.
- βi = beta of asset i — measures the systematic (non-diversifiable) risk of asset i relative to the overall market. Calculated as Cov(Ri, Rm) / Var(Rm). If β = 1, asset moves with the market. If β > 1, asset amplifies market moves (higher risk, higher expected return required). If β < 1, asset is less volatile than market.
- E(Rm) = expected return on the market portfolio — the average return across all risky assets
- E(Rm) − Rf = equity risk premium — the additional return investors demand above the risk-free rate for holding risky assets. Assumed constant and stable in CAPM. In practice it is highly variable and sensitive to market conditions — i.e. to the thermodynamic temperature of the system.
- Interpretation: the only risk that commands a premium is systematic risk (β). Idiosyncratic risk (specific to one asset) can be diversified away and should not be compensated.

Black-Scholes option pricing:
C = S·N(d1) − K·e^(−rT)·N(d2)

- C = theoretical price (premium) of a European call option — the right but not obligation to buy the underlying asset at price K on date T
- S = current price of the underlying asset
- K = strike price — the price at which the option holder can buy the asset at expiration
- e^(−rT) = discount factor — reduces K to present value using continuous compounding at rate r over time T
- r = continuously compounded risk-free interest rate
- T = time to expiration of the option in years
- N(d1) and N(d2) = cumulative standard normal distribution functions — give the probability that a standard normal variable is less than d1 or d2 respectively
- N(d2) ≈ probability that the option expires in the money (S > K at expiration)
- N(d1) = delta of the option — the rate of change of option price with respect to the underlying asset price; also interpretable as a risk-adjusted probability

d1 = [ln(S/K) + (r + σ²/2)·T] / (σ·√T)

- ln(S/K) = natural log of the ratio of current price to strike price — measures how far in or out of the money the option currently is
- r + σ²/2 = drift term — the expected continuous growth rate of the asset adjusted for the variance
- σ = annualised volatility of the underlying asset's returns — the standard deviation of log returns
- σ² = variance of returns
- σ·√T = total volatility over the life of the option — scales volatility by the square root of time

d2 = d1 − σ·√T

- d2 is d1 minus the volatility scaling term, representing the probability of expiring in the money without the hedge adjustment

Critical assumption: log-normal price distribution — returns are normally distributed, tails are thin, extreme events are astronomically rare. Empirically false. Real return distributions have fat tails (power law α ≈ 3). Black-Scholes systematically underprices out-of-the-money options and catastrophically misprice tail risk — which is what blew up Long-Term Capital Management in 1998.

**What it correctly identifies:** That price signals aggregate distributed information. That in low-friction conditions with symmetric information, prices do tend toward informational efficiency. The underlying intuition that markets process information is correct.

**Where it breaks down:**

It has no model of urgency differentials. When one party is structurally forced to transact (high U) and the other is not (U → 1), the information content of the price signal is destroyed. The price no longer reflects value — it reflects the urgency differential. This is not a deviation from efficiency; it is the Lorentz transform operating on the transaction.

## Austrian School / Subjective Value Theory (Menger, Hayek, Mises)

**Equations:**

Marginal utility:
MU = ∂U / ∂q

- MU = marginal utility — the additional utility (satisfaction, preference-fulfilment) gained from consuming one additional unit of a good
- ∂U = infinitesimal change in total utility U
- ∂q = infinitesimal change in quantity consumed q
- ∂U/∂q = the partial derivative of the utility function with respect to quantity — the rate at which utility changes as more of the good is consumed
- Interpretation: the value of a good to a specific agent at a specific moment is not determined by the total utility of all units of that good (the "water-diamond paradox" — water is more useful than diamonds but cheaper) but by the utility of the marginal unit — the next unit consumed.

Diminishing marginal utility:
∂²U / ∂q² < 0

- ∂²U/∂q² = second derivative of the utility function with respect to quantity — the rate of change of marginal utility as more is consumed
- < 0 means the second derivative is negative — marginal utility is decreasing
- Interpretation: each additional unit consumed adds less satisfaction than the previous unit. First glass of water is very valuable; hundredth glass is nearly worthless. This is why demand curves slope downward — as price falls, buyers are only willing to buy more at lower valuations.

Equilibrium exchange condition:
MUa / Pa = MUb / Pb

- MUa = marginal utility of good a to this consumer
- Pa = price of good a
- MUa / Pa = marginal utility per unit of money spent on good a — what the consumer gets per dollar spent on a
- MUb = marginal utility of good b to this consumer
- Pb = price of good b
- MUb / Pb = marginal utility per unit of money spent on good b
- Condition: at the utility-maximising allocation, the consumer equates the bang-per-buck across all goods. If MUa/Pa > MUb/Pb, the consumer can increase total utility by shifting spending from b to a — they keep doing this until equality is restored.
- Extension to all n goods: MU1/P1 = MU2/P2 = ... = MUn/Pn = λ, where λ is the marginal utility of income (the Lagrange multiplier from the neoclassical formulation above — the Austrian and neoclassical equilibrium conditions are mathematically equivalent despite the philosophical differences).

## Keynesian Economics (Keynes)

**Core claim:** Markets do not self-correct automatically. Aggregate demand drives economic output. In downturns, private sector demand collapses and government must substitute demand through spending. Price levels are sticky — wages and prices do not fall smoothly to clear markets in the short run.

**Equations:**

National income identity:
Y = C + I + G + (X − M)

- Y = GDP (Gross Domestic Product) — the total monetary value of all final goods and services produced in an economy in a given period. Also equals total income and total expenditure in the circular flow.
- C = private consumption expenditure — spending by households on goods and services: food, housing, transport, entertainment. The largest component of GDP in most economies (~60-70%).
- I = private investment — spending by firms on capital goods: machinery, buildings, software, inventory accumulation. Also includes residential construction. Determined primarily by expected future profits and the interest rate.
- G = government expenditure on goods and services — public sector spending on defence, infrastructure, healthcare, education. Excludes transfer payments (welfare, pensions) which are not direct purchases of output.
- X = exports — domestic goods and services purchased by foreign buyers. Adds to domestic income.
- M = imports — foreign goods and services purchased by domestic buyers. Subtracts from domestic income because the spending leaves the domestic circular flow.
- X − M = net exports (trade balance). Positive = trade surplus; negative = trade deficit.
- Interpretation: all output must be purchased by someone. These four sectors are exhaustive — every unit of GDP is bought by households, firms, government, or foreigners.

Consumption function:
C = a + b(Y − T)

- C = total consumption expenditure (defined above)
- a = autonomous consumption — the level of consumption that occurs regardless of income. Households consume even when income is zero by drawing on savings or borrowing. Represents the floor of consumption.
- b = marginal propensity to consume (MPC) — the fraction of each additional unit of disposable income that is spent on consumption rather than saved. 0 < b < 1. If b = 0.8, households spend 80 cents of every additional dollar of income.
- Y = total income / GDP
- T = taxes — total tax payments to government
- Y − T = disposable income — income after tax, the actual income available for households to spend or save
- b(Y − T) = induced consumption — the consumption that depends on income level

Keynesian multiplier:
k = 1 / (1 − MPC) = 1 / (1 − b)

- k = the multiplier — the total increase in GDP produced by a unit increase in autonomous spending (G, I, or autonomous C)
- MPC = marginal propensity to consume = b (defined above)
- 1 − MPC = marginal propensity to save — the fraction of additional income that is saved rather than spent
- Mechanism: government spends $1 → recipient earns $1 → spends b of it → next recipient earns $b → spends b² of it → ... → total spending = 1 + b + b² + ... = 1/(1−b)
- If b = 0.8: k = 1/(1−0.8) = 5. A $1 increase in G produces a $5 increase in Y.
- The multiplier assumes all additional income circulates domestically and is spent (not saved, taxed away, or spent on imports). Each of these leakages reduces k.

IS curve (goods market equilibrium):
Y = C(Y − T) + I(r) + G

- Y = output / income (defined above)
- C(Y − T) = consumption as a function of disposable income (defined above)
- I(r) = investment as a function of the interest rate r — investment falls as r rises because borrowing becomes more expensive and the present value of future returns falls
- r = real interest rate — the nominal interest rate adjusted for inflation; the actual cost of borrowing in terms of real purchasing power
- G = government spending (defined above)
- The IS curve traces all combinations of Y and r where the goods market is in equilibrium (output = planned expenditure). It slopes downward: higher r → lower I → lower Y.

LM curve (money market equilibrium):
M/P = L(r, Y)

- M = nominal money supply — the total stock of money in the economy, controlled by the central bank
- P = price level — the general level of prices. M/P = real money supply — money supply adjusted for purchasing power.
- L(r, Y) = liquidity preference / money demand function — the real demand for money holdings
- r = interest rate — the opportunity cost of holding money (money held earns no interest; bonds do). Higher r → lower demand for money (people prefer interest-bearing assets).
- Y = income — higher income → more transactions → higher demand for money
- L(r, Y) = kY − hr where k = income sensitivity of money demand, h = interest sensitivity of money demand
- The LM curve traces all combinations of Y and r where money supply equals money demand. It slopes upward: higher Y → higher money demand → higher r required to bring money demand back to fixed supply.
- IS-LM intersection: the combination of Y* and r* where both goods and money markets clear simultaneously. This is the Keynesian short-run equilibrium.

Phillips Curve:
π = πe − α(u − un)

- π = actual inflation rate — the rate at which the general price level is rising
- πe = expected inflation rate — what firms and workers anticipate inflation will be when setting wages and prices. In the short run, πe is given (sticky expectations). In the long run, πe → π (expectations adjust to reality).
- α = sensitivity parameter — how much unemployment above the natural rate reduces inflation. Empirically estimated; varies across economies and time periods.
- u = actual unemployment rate — the fraction of the labour force currently unemployed and seeking work
- un = natural rate of unemployment (NAIRU — Non-Accelerating Inflation Rate of Unemployment) — the unemployment rate consistent with stable inflation. Includes frictional unemployment (job searching) and structural unemployment (skills mismatch). Does not include cyclical unemployment (demand shortfall).
- u − un = unemployment gap — how far actual unemployment is above or below the natural rate
- Interpretation: when u < un (economy is running hot), inflation rises above expectations. When u > un (recession), inflation falls below expectations. Implies a stable tradeoff: policymakers can choose lower unemployment at the cost of higher inflation.
- Why it broke in the 1970s: the oil shock collapsed Rn (oil renewability/availability) and raised Ra (no substitutes at scale), driving up the Reality Tensor for energy-intensive production. This raised costs independently of demand conditions — cost-push inflation — while simultaneously destroying employment. The Phillips Curve has no variable for supply-side physical constraints. It models a demand-only world.

**What it correctly identifies:** That aggregate demand is a real force. That markets can get stuck in equilibria below full employment. That the short run and long run are structurally different problems. That price stickiness is a real phenomenon — transactional mass does not adjust instantaneously to new equilibrium conditions.

**Where it breaks down:**

No invariant physical constraint. Keynesian models can recommend demand stimulus without accounting for whether the underlying physical resource base can support the stimulated demand. If Rn is low and Ra is high, stimulating demand accelerates the approach to the boundary condition rather than resolving it.

No urgency differential model. The mechanism by which monetary policy and fiscal stimulus transmit through the economy depends entirely on who receives the stimulus and what their urgency differential is relative to other market participants. A stimulus delivered to low-urgency actors (already-wealthy institutions) produces asset inflation rather than demand stimulus — the Lorentz factor routes the money to where urgency is lowest.

The 2008 bailout is the empirical demonstration of this failure. Stimulus delivered to creditors with U → 1 (no urgency to lend or invest) produced quantitative easing that inflated asset prices rather than real demand. The Reality Tensor predicts this exactly: low-urgency transactional mass does not produce price movement in real goods — it produces movement in financial assets where Ra is high and Rn is near zero.

**Planes operated on:** Q5 (aggregate mechanism), Q7 (short-run demand effects). Planes absent: Q3 (physical resource constraints), Q1 (urgency differential of specific actors), Q6 (renewability and long-run physical limits).

**Reality Tensor resolution:** Keynesian aggregate demand is the sum of transactional mass across all agents in the economy. Keynesian stimulus is an attempt to adjust P_soc upward across the system. The Reality Tensor shows that this works when Ra and S are within normal parameters and urgency differentials are moderate — and fails when the system is already at or near boundary conditions where the invariant constraint dominates over demand stimulus.

## Marxian Value Theory Extended — Sraffian / Post-Keynesian (Sraffa, Kalecki)

**Core claim:** Prices are determined by the technical conditions of production — the input-output structure of the economy — plus a distributional parameter (profit share or wage share). Distribution between labour and capital is the primary determinant of price level, not marginal utility.

**Equations:**

Sraffian price system:
p = (1 + r) · p · A + w · l

- p = price vector — a row vector [p1, p2, ..., pn] where pi is the price of commodity i. All prices are determined simultaneously; no single price is prior to any other.
- r = uniform rate of profit — the percentage return on capital investment, assumed equal across all industries in long-run equilibrium. This is the distributional parameter: if r rises, wages must fall, and vice versa. Sraffa shows the relationship between r and w is not linear — it depends on the technical structure of production.
- p · A = the vector of input costs — for each commodity, the cost of the commodities used as inputs to produce it. p·A multiplies the price vector by the technology matrix to get total material input costs per unit of output.
- A = input-output technology matrix — an n×n matrix where entry aij = the quantity of commodity i required as input to produce one unit of commodity j. Captures the entire technical structure of the economy. A is taken as given — it reflects the current state of technology, not prices or preferences.
- (1 + r) · p · A = material input costs marked up by the profit rate — what it costs to buy the inputs plus the profit the capitalist requires on that outlay
- w = wage rate — the price of one unit of labour (e.g. per hour or per day). Along with r, w is a distributional parameter. Given r, w is determined by the system; given w, r is determined.
- l = labour input vector — a row vector [l1, l2, ..., ln] where lj = the quantity of labour directly required to produce one unit of commodity j. Does not include the labour embodied in the inputs (that is captured through the matrix A recursively).
- w · l = direct wage costs per unit of output for each commodity
- Full interpretation: the price of each commodity equals the cost of material inputs (marked up by profit) plus direct wage costs. This must hold simultaneously for all commodities — it is a system of n equations in n unknowns (the prices), with r and w as external parameters.
- Key Sraffian result: the wage-profit frontier w(r) — as r rises from 0 to R (maximum profit rate), w falls from its maximum to 0. The shape of this frontier is not generally linear; it curves in ways determined entirely by the technology matrix A. This refutes the neoclassical claim that factor prices are determined by marginal productivity.

Standard commodity:
p* · q* = (1 + R) · p* · A · q*

- p* = standard price vector — the prices that make the standard commodity's value invariant to changes in distribution
- q* = standard commodity vector — the specific bundle of commodities whose composition mirrors the aggregate input-output structure of the economy. Sraffa constructed this as a composite commodity with the property that its ratio of net output to means of production equals R for all industries simultaneously.
- R = maximum rate of profit — the profit rate achieved when wages are zero (w = 0). Determined entirely by the technology matrix A; independent of prices or distribution.
- p* · A · q* = total input value of the standard commodity at standard prices
- (1 + R) · p* · A · q* = total output value at the maximum profit rate
- Purpose: Sraffa needed an invariant measure of value — a yardstick whose own price does not change when distribution changes, so that changes in relative prices can be measured against it without the measuring rod itself shifting. Ricardo and Marx both failed to find this; Sraffa constructed it mathematically. It is his attempt to do what the Reality Tensor's invariant constraint does from the physical side — find a reference point that does not move when the system's parameters change.

Kalecki's profit equation:
P = Ck + I + B − Sw

- P = total economy-wide profits — the aggregate surplus accruing to the capitalist class across all firms
- Ck = capitalist consumption — the portion of profit income that capitalists spend on consumption goods. When capitalists spend, it generates income and profit for other capitalists (the money circulates back).
- I = private investment — spending by firms on capital goods. Every dollar of investment is simultaneously a cost to the investing firm and income/profit to the firm supplying the capital goods.
- B = government budget deficit — government spending in excess of tax revenues. A deficit injects net spending into the economy that becomes income and profit for the private sector.
- Sw = worker saving — the portion of wage income that workers do not spend on consumption. This is a leakage: wage income saved does not flow back as demand for goods, so it does not generate profit.
- Full interpretation: profits in aggregate are generated by spending, not by production efficiency. Workers as a class cannot generate profits by saving more — their saving reduces demand and therefore reduces profits. Only capitalist spending (Ck), investment (I), and government deficits (B) create the demand that allows profits to be realised. This is Kalecki's "capitalists earn what they spend, workers spend what they earn" — a demand-side theory of profit distribution that directly contradicts the supply-side view that profits reward productive contribution.

**What it correctly identifies:** That the technical structure of production is a real constraint on price. That distribution is a power relationship, not an equilibrium outcome. That profit share and wage share are determined by bargaining power rather than marginal productivity.

**Where it breaks down:**

Still no physical boundary conditions. The Sraffian system is formally elegant but operates entirely within the produced economy — it has no mechanism for resource depletion, renewability constraints, or rarity. Rn and Ra are outside the model entirely.

No urgency differential. The Lorentz mechanism — the divergence in bargaining urgency between parties — is not modelled. Distribution is treated as a macro parameter rather than a field effect produced by the aggregate urgency differential across all labour-capital negotiations.

**Reality Tensor resolution:** The Sraffian profit rate maps onto the aggregate Lorentz factor across the labour market — the structural urgency advantage of capital over labour produces a systematic bias in the negotiated price of labour. The Reality Tensor grounds this observation in the physical mechanism rather than leaving it as a distributional parameter without a causal account.

## Econophysics — Agent-Based Models (Mantegna, Stanley, Bouchaud)

**Core claim:** Markets are complex systems exhibiting emergent behaviour consistent with statistical mechanics — power-law distributions, volatility clustering, phase transitions, and scaling invariances. Standard utility maximisation fails because it cannot model the non-linear dynamics of interacting agents.

**Equations:**

Power-law return distribution (Pareto tail):
P(|r| > x) ~ x^(−α)

- P(|r| > x) = the probability that the absolute value of an asset return r exceeds threshold x — the probability of an extreme event of magnitude x or greater
- |r| = absolute return — the magnitude of the price change regardless of direction (up or down)
- x = the threshold level of return being considered
- ~ = asymptotically proportional to — the relationship holds in the tail of the distribution (for large x)
- x^(−α) = power law — the probability falls as a power of the threshold, not as the exponential decay of a Gaussian
- α = the tail exponent (also called the Pareto exponent) — determines how fat the tail is. Lower α = fatter tail = more extreme events. Empirically measured at α ≈ 3 for stock returns (the cubic law). For a Gaussian distribution, α → ∞ — tails decay exponentially, making extreme events essentially impossible. At α = 3, extreme events are orders of magnitude more probable than Gaussian models predict. The 2008 crash was a ~25-sigma event under Gaussian assumptions — essentially impossible. Under a power law with α = 3, it was within the expected range.

Volatility clustering (GARCH(1,1) model):
σ²t = ω + α · ε²(t−1) + β · σ²(t−1)

- σ²t = conditional variance at time t — the expected variance of returns at time t given all information up to t−1. This is the model's estimate of current volatility.
- ω = long-run average variance (weighted) — the baseline variance the process reverts to. ω > 0 ensures variance is always positive.
- α = ARCH parameter — the weight on the previous period's squared shock. Measures how much a large surprise in the last period increases today's volatility. High α = volatility responds strongly and quickly to shocks.
- ε²(t−1) = squared residual from the previous period — the square of last period's unexpected return (actual return minus predicted return). A large shock (positive or negative) increases ε²(t−1) and therefore increases σ²t.
- β = GARCH parameter — the weight on last period's conditional variance. Measures persistence: how long elevated volatility lasts after a shock. High β = volatility decays slowly after a spike.
- σ²(t−1) = conditional variance from the previous period
- α + β < 1 required for stationarity (variance returns to long-run average rather than exploding)
- Interpretation: today's volatility is a weighted average of the long-run variance, last period's shock, and last period's volatility. Markets have memory — a volatile day predicts a volatile tomorrow. This directly refutes the EMH random walk assumption of constant variance.

Sornette's log-periodic power law (LPPL) crash prediction model:
ln P(t) = A + B·(tc − t)^m · [1 + C·cos(ω·ln(tc − t) + φ)]

- ln P(t) = natural logarithm of the asset price at time t — using log price linearises exponential growth and makes the power law visible
- A = the log price at the critical time tc — the level the price approaches as the crash point is reached
- B = amplitude of the power law acceleration — negative B means price is rising in an accelerating bubble. |B| scales the overall magnitude of the bubble component.
- tc = critical time — the predicted time of the crash or phase transition. The singularity point where the power law term diverges. Sornette estimates this from the pattern of oscillations in the data.
- tc − t = time remaining until the critical point — shrinks to zero as t approaches tc, causing the power law term to accelerate
- m = power law exponent — controls the rate of acceleration toward tc. 0 < m < 1 for a genuine bubble (accelerating growth). m determines the shape of the power law approach.
- (tc − t)^m = the power law envelope — the overall accelerating trend of the bubble
- C = amplitude of the log-periodic oscillations — the size of the oscillations around the power law trend
- cos(ω·ln(tc − t) + φ) = the log-periodic oscillation term — produces oscillations that become faster and faster as tc approaches (because ln(tc − t) diverges as tc − t → 0)
- ω = log-periodic angular frequency — the frequency of oscillations in log-time. Empirically found to cluster around ω ≈ 6-8 across different markets and crashes.
- φ = phase parameter — sets the starting point of the oscillations
- Full interpretation: a bubble exhibits accelerating price growth (power law) with superimposed oscillations that increase in frequency as the crash approaches. This pattern is generated by positive feedback mechanisms (herding, leverage) interacting with negative feedback mechanisms (profit-taking, risk limits). The crash occurs when the system can no longer sustain the acceleration — a deterministic phase transition, not a random event.

Bouchaud's market impact model:
ΔP = Y · sgn(ε) · (Q/V)^δ

- ΔP = price impact — the change in mid-price caused by a trade
- Y = market impact constant — a market-specific scaling factor capturing overall liquidity conditions. Higher Y = less liquid market = larger price impact per unit of order.
- sgn(ε) = sign of the order — +1 for a buy order, −1 for a sell order. Buys push prices up; sells push prices down.
- ε = signed order flow — the trade itself: positive for buyer-initiated, negative for seller-initiated
- Q = order size — the number of shares (or contracts) in the trade
- V = average daily volume — the typical total trading volume in this asset per day. Normalises order size relative to the market's liquidity.
- Q/V = participation rate — what fraction of typical daily volume this order represents. A 1% participation rate has modest impact; a 20% participation rate has large impact.
- δ = impact exponent — empirically measured at approximately 0.5 (square root law). The price impact scales as the square root of order size, not linearly. Doubling order size increases impact by √2, not 2. This means large orders are relatively cheaper per unit to execute than linear models predict, but still significantly more impactful than EMH assumes.
- (Q/V)^δ = the nonlinear impact function — the core finding: price impact is a concave power law of order size. This directly contradicts EMH which assumes prices reflect information instantaneously and trading has no systematic price impact.

Partition function for n-body market:
Z = Σ_states exp(−β · H)

- Z = partition function — borrowed from statistical mechanics. Encodes the statistical properties of all possible states of the system. Once Z is known, all thermodynamic quantities (average energy, entropy, phase boundaries) can be derived from it.
- Σ_states = sum over all possible configurations / states of the market — all possible combinations of agent positions, beliefs, and holdings
- exp(−β · H) = Boltzmann weight — the statistical weight of each state. States with lower energy H are exponentially more probable at low temperature; at high temperature, all states become approximately equally probable.
- β = inverse market temperature = 1 / T_neg — the inverse of the system's thermodynamic temperature. High β (low temperature) = agents are rational, coordinated, low variance. Low β (high temperature) = agents are irrational, uncoordinated, high variance, system near phase transition.
- T_neg = market temperature — aggregate variance, behavioural irrationality, and informational friction. Not a metaphor — it is a measurable quantity derived from return variance and correlation structure.
- H = Hamiltonian — the energy function of the system. In market context, H encodes the interaction energies between agents: their positions, beliefs about others' positions, and the costs of changing positions. At the market level, H is constructed from the interaction structure of agent orders.
- Phase transitions occur when Z develops singularities — points where the partition function becomes non-analytic (derivatives diverge). In physical systems these are melting points, boiling points.
