Consequentialism and Utilitarianism

The initial reading of the framework\'s moral calculus presented a significant risk of aligning with Consequentialism or Utilitarianism. The framework initially described morality as a balance sheet of benefit versus cost, defining \"Good\" as creating the most value for the most people and \"Evil\" as hoarding value while hurting the group.

From a strict Catholic moral theological perspective, this initial framing is highly problematic. Catholic doctrine asserts that morality is determined by the object, the intention, and the circumstances. Crucially, the Church teaches the doctrine of intrinsic evil, meaning that certain acts are fundamentally disordered and morally impermissible regardless of any potential collective benefit or positive outcome they might generate. An outcome based ethics system inherently contradicts this because it allows for the justification of harmful means if the net structural benefit is calculated to be positive.

The resolution to this potential heresy was provided through a strict structural constraint regarding the definition of cost. The clarification established that:

- Harmful means intended for greater collective benefit automatically fail the cost requirement of the system.

- A negative moral act is treated as an infinite structural cost, preventing any mathematical justification of an intrinsically evil act.

- Historical anomalies, such as the biblical account of Jericho, are not justified through a human benefit arithmetic but fit within the structure because they operate under direct divine authorization.

- Human beings possess the potential to do anything causally possible within an arbitrary timespan, meaning their actions have open ended causal propagation. Because humans are beings of infinite potential with eternal destinies, any bad act carries the potential for infinite bad.

By hard coding the prohibition of intrinsic evil as a non negotiable boundary and infinite cost within the structure, the framework ceases to be a utilitarian calculus. It effectively operates as a systems based overlay of natural law, where the moral constraints are structurally absolute rather than relative to human calculation.

Pelagianism and Semi-Pelagianism

The second major theological fault line identified was the potential for Pelagianism. Pelagianism is the heretical belief that human nature was uncorrupted by original sin and that mortal will is capable of choosing good, or achieving moral perfection, without the necessity of special divine grace.

The initial documentation of the system described redemption structurally, focusing on the expansion of core understanding and human structural self correction as the primary mechanisms for moral restoration. The absence of explicit references to grace, original sin, or a necessary divine mediator suggested that the human intellect, if structurally aware, could simply map its way out of the egoic vortex. Catholic soteriology strictly maintains that human reason and structure alone cannot fully redeem themselves, and that supernatural grace is an absolute necessity.

This heresy was entirely avoided through the explicit clarification regarding the nature of the structure and the mechanism of movement within it. The framework was aligned with orthodox Augustinian and Thomistic anthropology through the following parameters:

- The structural geometry itself is not a human invention but is explicitly given by God.

- The cross functions as the revealed pattern and the specific escape vector necessary to break free from the vicious circle of the ego.

- Movement toward the core cannot be achieved by mere cognitive recognition. It explicitly requires grace to conquer over base cognition.

- Because sin creates noetic effects that darken the intellect and distort the will, grace is the required corrective force to heal the cognition, allowing the individual to realign with the divine axis.

By defining grace as the necessary engine for structural movement, the system perfectly mirrors the Thomistic principle that grace perfects nature, rather than replacing it or allowing nature to perfect itself.

The framework\'s heavy reliance on a self referential epistemology centered on core understanding presented a risk of Rationalism or Immanentism. If the system implied that truth emerges purely from internal structural coherence, or that moral truth is generated immanently rather than revealed by a transcendent God, it would conflict with Catholic teaching. Furthermore, the concept of \"Relative Morality\" raised concerns about whether moral norms themselves could shift based on systemic pressure.

Impersonal Monism and Process Theology

A subtle but severe theological risk emerged regarding the nature of God within the system. If the framework treated God not as a living, personal, transcendent Creator, but merely as the geometric parameter space itself, the system would collapse into impersonal metaphysical monism or Process Theology. Catholic doctrine requires that God is Subsistent Being Itself (Ipsum Esse Subsistens). While all structure flows from the divine intellect, God cannot be reduced to the structure or the law.
