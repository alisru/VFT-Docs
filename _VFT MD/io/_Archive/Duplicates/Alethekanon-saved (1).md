## Summary
Australia's "foundational mythos of egalitarianism" and the "Lucky Country" mythos are often critically examined in light of the nation's "system defined by extractive economics," leading to calls for "historical revisionism" and "deep structural change."

The "foundational mythos of egalitarianism" in Australia posits a society where everyone has a "fair go" and a sense of "mateship," regardless of background. This idea emerged during the early colonial period, shaped by the demands of the outback and bush life, fostering a culture believed to be inherently egalitarian. However, this ideal is increasingly being challenged as a "dreamland" or a myth, with significant evidence pointing to persistent and entrenched disadvantage and growing wealth inequality. Critics highlight that Australia's constitution does not explicitly state that all Australians are equal, nor does it mention egalitarianism, and there is no Australian Bill of Rights to guarantee equal freedoms. Income and wealth distribution figures show a considerable disparity, with the top 20% of Australians controlling over 40% of the wealth, while the bottom 20% hold only about 5%.

This disconnect between myth and reality is often linked to a "system defined by extractive economics." Australia's prosperity has historically been, and continues to be, heavily reliant on its abundant natural resources, particularly mining. The original intent of Donald Horne's 1964 book, *The Lucky Country*, was to portray Australia's wealth as a result of sheer luck due to its resources, rather than the strength or ingenuity of its "second-rate" political or economic systems. Horne argued that Australia "lived on other people's ideas" and showed "less enterprise than almost any other prosperous industrial society". However, the phrase "The Lucky Country" has largely been misappropriated and used favorably, losing its original critical context and becoming part of a national mythology.

The persistence of the egalitarian and "Lucky Country" myths, despite evidence to the contrary, points to a form of "historical revisionism." There's a reluctance to confront the nation's past and the structural inequalities embedded within its systems. Policy regimes have been criticized for deliberately undermining the chances of disadvantaged Australians while transferring public resources to the wealthy. Since the mid-1970s, with the rise of neo-liberalism, Australia has seen full employment abandoned, labor market regulations dismantled, and income support systems retrenched, leading to a wider gap between real wages and productivity. The government has increasingly acted as an agent for corporate interests rather than for all Australians.

Consequently, there is a recognized need for "deep structural change" to address these issues. Reports, even from pro-business organizations like the Committee for Economic Development in Australia (CEDA), have highlighted the "disgrace" of widespread poverty and entrenched disadvantage, urging a re-evaluation of policy. Such change would involve bridging inequality gaps, spending more and smarter, and actively listening to marginalized voices, including Indigenous Australians. A central irony is that the natural egalitarianism that emerged in the colonies ultimately led to a kind of populism that has since been undermined by a system that now serves a narrow set of established interests, threatening Australia's traditional egalitarianism and highlighting the need to consciously refocus on equality as a shared goal.
