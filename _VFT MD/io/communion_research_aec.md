# How the Lord's Supper Became Four Different Doctrines
## From Greek Source Text to Reformation Schism — with AE-C Inline

The Christian Eucharist began as a full evening meal in house churches around 50 AD and within six centuries fractured into irreconcilable positions on what happens to bread and wine, who may eat, and who may consecrate. Every major fault line traces to a specific text, a specific date, and a specific institutional decision. What follows reconstructs that trajectory — from the Greek words Paul chose, through the patristic innovations that reframed them, to the Reformation debates that crystallized permanent division.

The core tension was always the same: does τοῦτό μού ἐστιν τὸ σῶμα state a fact about bread, or a metaphor about memory? Both readings are linguistically possible. The more important question — one the entire tradition failed to ask — is whether the argument about bread was a displacement activity that let everyone avoid the actual command: share the meal, share the food, treat the body as a body.

**Note on method:** Every Greek term below carries two readings — the Orthodox Scholarly reading (what the tradition built doctrine on) and the AE-C reading (Action-Effect over Context in Information Space — every root definition held simultaneously, rendered as a functional vector describing what the word actually does in reality). The gap between the two readings is precisely where doctrine drifted from instruction.

## Part I — The Source Texts

### 1 Corinthians 4:6 — The Strictness Rule

τὸ μὴ ὑπὲρ ἃ γέγραπται — "the [rule of] not beyond what stands written"

**Orthodox:** Paul is addressing factional boasting in Corinth — don't elevate human teachers beyond what Scripture warrants. Later used as a Reformation hermeneutical charter for sola scriptura, though this exceeds Paul's original intent.

**AE-C:** γέγραπται is perfect passive of γράφω — the thing that has been written and permanently remains written, the completed and stable deposit. ὑπὲρ = above/beyond the boundary of. τό nominalises the phrase as a named operative principle: the rule of not-exceeding. Applied to communion: the deposit records a shared meal. Every doctrinal layer added beyond that record — ontological transformation theories, episcopal validity chains, purity credentialing, token elements substituted for food — is structurally ὑπὲρ ἃ γέγραπται. The text cannot bear those additions because the text does not contain them. Return to the deposit. Read what is actually written. What is written is a meal.

### 1 Corinthians 11:17–34 — The Primary Institution Passage

The following terms are taken in order of appearance. Each carries the full Orthodox reading and the AE-C reading. The gap is annotated.

---

**δεῖπνον** (deipnon, v.20 — "supper/meal")

Orthodox: The Lord's Supper as a named sacramental rite; echoes the Passover seder structure; designates a specific liturgical form.

AE-C: The primary evening meal — the main biological intake event of the day, not a ceremonial supplement. Ancient δεῖπνον was full: multiple dishes, sustained duration, communal recline. Paul's statement that the Corinthian gathering is "not possible" (οὐκ ἔστιν) to call the Lord's δεῖπνον is a functional test: if the food-provision operation is not executing, the event fails to qualify as the thing it names regardless of what words are spoken over it. The term is not a category label for a ritual. It is a description of an action. No provision = not the Lord's meal. The church replaced the action with the label and kept the label.

Gap: Orthodox reading treats δεῖπνον as a liturgical type. AE-C reads it as a minimum specification: there must be food, it must be a meal, people must eat.

---

**κυριακόν** (kyriakon, v.20 — "of the Lord / the Lord's")

Orthodox: Possessive adjective meaning dedicated to or belonging to the Lord; used only here and in Revelation 1:10 (κυριακὴ ἡμέρα, the Lord's Day); marks the meal as set apart for sacred purpose.

AE-C: κύριος = the operational owner — the entity to whose authority and distribution protocol a system belongs. κυριακόν marks the meal as running under Christ's ownership rules, not the congregation's social hierarchy. Christ's ownership rules as demonstrated at the Last Supper: open table, no purity gate, no credential check, the lowest-status person in the room eats. When the Corinthian wealthy arrive early, gorge, and leave the poor nothing, they have replaced the owner's operating protocol with their own social-stratification algorithm. They are running their meal under a different authority while using the owner's name. This is the precise definition of misrepresentation.

Gap: Orthodox reading makes κυριακόν a category of sacredness. AE-C makes it an ownership claim with operational implications — the meal must execute according to the owner's demonstrated distribution rules.

---

**ἀνάμνησις** (anamnēsis, vv.24–25 — "remembrance/memorial")

This became the single most contested term in Eucharistic theology. The entire Real Presence debate turns on it.

Orthodox (Catholic/Orthodox): ἀνάμνησις translates Hebrew זִכָּרוֹן (zikkaron), the Passover memorial term in which participants do not merely recall the Exodus but ritually enact it — the past event becomes presently operative. Dom Gregory Dix: "a re-presenting of a thing in such a way that it is not so much regarded as being absent, as itself presently operative by its effects." Therefore the Eucharist re-presents Christ's sacrifice; the bread and wine become his body and blood.

Orthodox (Reformed): ἀνάμνησις = active, participatory commemoration — exceeds passive nostalgia but stops short of metaphysical re-presentation. The elements are signs that activate genuine spiritual participation in Christ through faith.

AE-C: ἀνά (upward retrieval, back against the stored state) + μνησις (active calling-to-mind from stored configuration, from μιμνήσκομαι) = the operation of retrieving a stored functional pattern and re-executing it in live conditions. τοῦτο ποιεῖτε εἰς τὴν ἐμὴν ἀνάμνησιν = keep executing this action in order to re-run my stored operational pattern. The emphasis ἐμήν (MY own, emphatic possessive — absent from Matthew and Mark) specifies exactly whose pattern is being retrieved and re-run. The pattern is not the sacrifice alone. The pattern is the whole event: the open table, the provision, the shared meal, the service at table, the act of feeding. Every tradition that retained the words of institution while removing the meal retained the retrieval command while deleting the pattern it was meant to retrieve. The anamnēsis is running. It is retrieving nothing.

Gap: Orthodox debate focused entirely on what happens to the elements. AE-C: the elements are not the pattern being retrieved. The meal is. The debate was about the wrong object.

---

**σῶμα** (sōma, vv.24,27,29 — "body")

This term carries three simultaneous referents in Paul's usage. Paul deliberately chose σῶμα rather than σάρξ throughout this passage — a choice that was buried by later doctrine.

Orthodox: Christ's physical/incarnate body; the communal body of the church (Paul's primary theological usage in 1 Cor 12); the resurrection body (1 Cor 15). All three operate simultaneously in vv.27–29, which is correct and important. "Discerning the body" (v.29) = recognising the sacramental presence of Christ in the elements (Catholic) or recognising the communal body of the church gathered (Reformed).

AE-C: σῶμα = the relational operational whole — the complete active system including its network position and all connected member-nodes, not merely biological tissue or spiritual category. As a body is the sum of all its cells operating as one functional unit, the σῶμα of Christ is the complete network of members each holding position and function within the system. "Discerning the σῶμα" = running a systems-recognition operation on the complete connected unit — which includes registering every member currently at the table, including the one who arrived with nothing and has not eaten. Failure to discern the σῶμα means consuming the ritual while a body-node in acute need goes unregistered. The individual is eating alone at a table of people. The network sees no signal. The body does not respond.

Gap: Orthodox "discerning the body" debate concerned itself with what the bread IS. AE-C: the failure Paul identifies is a recognition failure — not recognising a member of the body as a member. The hunger of the poor at the Corinthian table is a body-distress signal. The failure to respond is immune-system failure.

---

**σάρξ** (sarx — "flesh")

This term does not appear in 1 Corinthians 11 — Paul uses σῶμα throughout, deliberately. But the entire Real Presence debate from Radbertus to Trent to Zwingli was fought on σάρξ, especially John 6:52–58. It requires treatment here precisely because its misreading generated the primary fault line.

Orthodox (Catholic/anti-Docetist): σάρξ = the real incarnate physical body of Christ; eating his σάρξ = literal corporeal presence in the elements. John 6:54 taken as direct Eucharistic instruction.

Orthodox (Protestant/Zwinglian): σάρξ in John 6:63 ("the flesh profits nothing") = fallen human nature, the merely carnal approach to spiritual things. Therefore John 6 is not about the Eucharist but about spiritual receptivity.

Orthodox (Lutheran): σάρξ in John 6:63 refers to fallen human capacity, not Christ's incarnate body. The two uses of σάρξ in John 6 are different referents. Christ's body is truly present.

AE-C: σάρξ = the living biological container that holds and executes work — cohesive biological cells operating as one material unit, the physical substrate filled by consumption and capable of physical output. As fruit is the living container for seed and nutrient, σάρξ is the biological container for faith made physically executable. "Eat my σάρξ" (John 6:54) = incorporate the living executable container-pattern of the operational mode into your own biological system — become the same kind of living container, running the same operational pattern. "The σάρξ profits nothing" (John 6:63) = the container without the animating breath/spirit outputs zero — not a condemnation of materiality but a statement that hardware without the operating system is inert. A body that does not run the spirit's operations produces nothing regardless of how much bread it consumes.

The entire Real Presence debate was fought on which philosophical category σάρξ belongs to. AE-C: σάρξ is not a philosophical category. It is a functional descriptor. The question "does the bread become σάρξ?" is the wrong question. The question the text is asking is: "are you becoming the same kind of living operational container the bread represents?" Both sides of the debate were arguing about the label on a container whose contents they had stopped examining.

---

**κοινωνία** (koinōnia, 1 Cor 10:16 — "fellowship/communion/participation")

Orthodox: Spiritual fellowship; participation in the divine life; the communal bond of the church; often used to justify "closed communion" — only those in full doctrinal agreement may commune together.

AE-C: From κοινός (common, held jointly in the same operational space) + the action-suffix. κοινωνία = the structural state in which resources, information, and operational function are actually held in common — not the feeling of togetherness but the material configuration of shared-resource operation. Acts 2:44 shows what operative κοινωνία looks like: "all who believed were together and had all things in common (κοινά)" and distribution happened to each according to need. κοινωνία has a balance sheet. It is destroyed at the Corinthian table not because the atmosphere of fellowship is disrupted but because the shared-resource state is broken — some have excess, others have nothing, and no redistribution occurs. Using κοινωνία to justify restricted access to the table is an inversion of the term's functional meaning: the word that describes universal material sharing is being deployed as a gate.

---

**καταγγέλλετε** (katangellete, v.26 — "you proclaim/announce")

Orthodox: Indicative mood, not imperative — the eating and drinking is itself a proclamation of the Lord's death until he comes. The Eucharist is therefore a kerygmatic act, a sermon in bread and wine.

AE-C: κατα (downward, through the network) + angellō (to carry a message, transmit signal) = to broadcast a functional message through the system to all connected nodes. The eating IS the transmission event — the meal carries the operational signal of the pattern. Every gathering where the body shares food broadcasts what Christ demonstrated. Reducing the meal to a token element is a transmitter running on minimum power with no content in the signal. The broadcast is technically occurring. Nothing is being carried. The congregation receives the ritual acknowledgement that a transmission occurred. They do not receive the transmitted pattern.

---

**δοκιμαζέτω ἑαυτόν** (dokimazeto heauton, v.28 — "let him examine himself")

Orthodox: Self-examination before communion; testing one's spiritual worthiness; basis for examination requirements, confession before communion (Trent), and fencing the table (Reformed). Used to justify gatekeeping.

AE-C: δοκιμάζω = the metallurgical process of assaying — subjecting metal to heat and acid to determine its genuine operational composition. The imperative is a diagnostic command: run a full composition test on your own functional state before entering the shared system. The correct diagnostic is not "am I morally pure enough to consume this element?" but "am I currently configured to execute the operation this system runs on?" — specifically: am I bringing provision? Am I prepared to recognise every node at this table as a body-member? Am I holding the pattern I am about to claim to re-run? The tradition converted a self-diagnostic into a gatekeeping tool and pointed it outward. Paul pointed it inward.

---

**ἔνοχος τοῦ σώματος καὶ τοῦ αἵματος** (enochos, v.27 — "guilty of the body and blood")

Orthodox: Forensic liability — unworthy reception makes one criminally liable against Christ's person; basis for the gravity of approaching the Eucharist in sin; used by Trent to require prior confession.

AE-C: ἔνοχος (held within, bound to) + genitive of the entity harmed = liable for an offense committed against a specific operational unit, answerable for the damage caused to it. Paul has named the crime explicitly in vv.21–22: the wealthy arrive early, eat their fill, leave the poor nothing, and humiliate those who have nothing. The liability is body-violation — treating a live node of the operational body as external to the system while claiming to commune with the whole. The forensic weight is not about ritual impurity. It is about structural body-harm: you have damaged the body by refusing to recognise one of its members as a member. The consequence stated in v.30 — weakness, sickness, death — is the body's systemic response to unresolved node-damage. The body breaks down when its cells stop recognising each other.

---

**ἀσθενεῖς / ἄρρωστοι / κοιμῶνται** (vv.30 — "weak / sick / sleep")

Orthodox: Literal physical consequences of unworthy reception; or spiritual conditions (weakness of faith, sickness of soul, spiritual death). Commentators divide on whether Paul claims real deaths have occurred.

AE-C: ἀσθενεῖς = without strength, operational output below threshold. ἄρρωστοι = without the working-root, non-functional at the operative level. κοιμῶνται = the standard NT euphemism for death in 14 of its 18 uses — node shutdown. These are system states, not punishments. A body that refuses to recognise its own damaged nodes stops repairing them. Unresolved node-damage becomes chronic condition, then system failure. Paul is describing the physics of a body that has stopped running its immune function. The community in Corinth has members who are literally going without food while others consume in excess. The downstream physical consequences of malnutrition and social exclusion are exactly ἀσθενής, ἄρρωστος, and κοιμᾶται. Paul does not need to invoke supernatural punishment. He is describing biology.

### Luke 22:14–23 — The Last Supper Account

**ἐπιθυμίᾳ ἐπεθύμησα** (epithymia epethymēsa, v.15 — "I have earnestly desired")

Orthodox: Semitic intensification (cognate accusative) expressing profound longing; Jesus's deep desire to share this final meal before his death.

AE-C: ἐπιθυμία = the directional drive toward a specific object, the reaching-vector of the will. The doubled form = maximum directional intensity — the will fully committed to this specific outcome. The thing Christ most intensely directed his operational will toward before his death was this meal. Not a sermon. Not a miracle. The shared table. This is the last thing the primary instruction source maximally willed before the execution event. The tradition treated it as a meal to be replaced with a ceremony. The source treated it as the thing most worth desiring.

---

**ἡ καινὴ διαθήκη** (hē kainē diathēkē, v.20 — "the new covenant")

Orthodox: καινή = new in quality (not νέος, new in time); διαθήκη = covenant (translating Hebrew בְּרִית) or last will/testament (classical Greek). The cup enacts the new covenant ratified by Christ's blood.

AE-C: καινή = new in kind, constitutively different in operational structure from what preceded it. διαθήκη = the binding mutual-obligation agreement that defines the terms of a relationship and the distribution of its resources. A διαθήκη creates legal obligation. "The new covenant in my blood" = a constitutively new binding-obligation structure, ratified by the permanent commitment of the contracting party's own life. The terms of this specific covenant are written throughout the instruction set: the provision-obligation toward the poor, the family-reclassification of all believers, the open table. The cup is the seal on a document with operational content. The tradition kept the cup and ignored the document's terms.

---

**ἐκχυννόμενον** (ekchynnomenon, v.20 — "being poured out")

Orthodox: Present passive participle with sacrificial overtones; echoes Isaiah 53:12 ("he poured out his soul to death"); the sacrifice is ongoing/present in the sacrament.

AE-C: ἐκ (out from, directionally outward) + χέω (to pour, to flow) = the continuous outward-directional flow from a source. Present tense: this is happening now, not past. The substance is moving outward continuously. The covenant is not a past event being commemorated — it is a continuous outward-flow of provision from the source into the system. The bread and cup represent the same vector: what is held is released outward, broken, poured, distributed. The form of the covenant is the form of the action: give what you have outward to those who need it.

---

### Acts 2:42–47 — The Early Community

**τῇ κοινωνίᾳ** (v.42, with definite article — "the fellowship")

Orthodox: The definite article marks a recognised institutional practice; the communal bond of the early church; the fellowship as a named element of early Christian identity alongside teaching, breaking bread, and prayer.

AE-C: The definite article marks this as a specific named shared-resource configuration that was being actively maintained and was recognisable to others as a distinct operational state. κοινωνία with the article = the particular state of having-everything-in-common, not fellowship-in-general. Acts 2:44–45 gives its material content: possessions sold, proceeds distributed to need. The κοινωνία had a balance sheet and a distribution operation. It was not an atmosphere or a feeling of belonging. It was a functioning resource-sharing system.

---

**τῇ κλάσει τοῦ ἄρτου** (v.42 — "the breaking of the bread")

Orthodox: The definite article again marks a specific recognised rite; scholars debate whether this is a full meal or an early Eucharistic ceremony; likely both simultaneously at this stage.

AE-C: κλάσις = the active fracture-and-distribution event, the breaking action itself. ἄρτος = leavened bread, the risen loaf — bread that has undergone fermentation, that has been through time and biological process and has risen through the action of living yeast. The breaking is not a ceremonial gesture performed over a token. It is the distribution mechanism: you cannot serve one loaf to twelve people without breaking it. The break IS the provision event. Replacing ἄρτος with an unleavened wafer removes the process element (fermentation = time + work + living biological agent in the dough) and makes the distribution act ceremonially inert — a wafer cannot feed anyone. A broken loaf can. The tradition kept the breaking gesture while replacing the thing being broken with something that carries no nutritive content. The form without the substance.

---

**κατ᾽ οἶκον** (v.46 — "house to house / in homes")

Orthodox: Domestic setting for early worship; the home as the original church space before purpose-built buildings.

AE-C: oikos = household — the base economic, relational, and production unit of ancient life. Bread was made in the oikos. Food was produced, stored, and distributed from the oikos. The meal-provision operation ran at the scale of the household network because that is where the means of provision actually lived. When worship moved from the oikos to the basilica, it moved from the production-node — where food is made and distributed — to a dedicated performance space where no food is produced. The production-node was replaced by an observation-node. Members stopped being the body and became the audience. An audience does not feed people. A household does.

## Part II — The Patristic Drift (50–325 AD)

Four innovations entered in this period that have no New Testament source: "Eucharist" as a technical term, episcopal control over the rite, Real Presence language, and the separation of meal from sacrament. Each writer marks a stage in the replacement of a meal with a managed ritual.

---

### The Didache (~60–100 AD)

The earliest extant Christian liturgical text. Notable for what it contains and what it lacks.

Contains: the first Christian use of "Eucharist" (εὐχαριστία) as a technical term for the rite. Cup before bread — reversed from all later universal practice. Prayers about the gathered community as one loaf scattered over hills and gathered in. "After you have been filled" — confirming a real meal still occurring. Prophets may pray freely without fixed text.

Lacks: No institution narrative. No "this is my body/blood" language. No Real Presence theology. No single bishop. No sacerdotal priesthood.

Gate present: baptism required — citing Matthew 7:6 ("give not what is holy to dogs"). Sacrifice language (θυσία) appears, citing Malachi 1:11.

AE-C assessment: The Didache represents the meal-prayer stage before the institution narrative became central. The gate it installs (baptism) is doctrinal-identity rather than ontological-status. The meal is still real, the prayers are still improvised, and the functional emphasis is on the gathered community as one body. The sacrifice language is the first foothold for what follows.

---

### Ignatius of Antioch (~107 AD)

Two revolutionary claims with no direct New Testament citation to support either.

Claim 1 — Real Presence: "The Eucharist is the flesh of our Saviour Jesus Christ" (Smyrnaeans 6). Written against Docetists who denied Christ had real flesh — the claim functions as anti-Docetist argument, not as eucharistic metaphysics. Whether it asserts a change in the elements or an anti-Docetist claim about the Incarnation is genuinely contested.

Claim 2 — Episcopal monopoly: "Let that be deemed a valid (βεβαία) Eucharist which is administered either by the bishop, or by one to whom he has entrusted it… It is not lawful without the bishop either to baptize or to celebrate a love-feast" (Smyrnaeans 8). First text tying Eucharistic validity to episcopal authority. No scriptural citation. Basis: ecclesiological analogy between bishop and Christ.

AE-C assessment: Ignatius introduces the gate that will eventually become Canon 908. The word βεβαία (valid, legally binding) converts a community meal into a legal instrument that only a licensed operator can execute. The Judas precedent is already being overwritten — Ignatius's bishop would have refused to administer the feast to someone of insufficient standing. The logic is institutional self-protection dressed in anti-heresy language.

---

### Justin Martyr (~150 AD)

Most detailed pre-Nicene liturgical description (First Apology 65–67). Sunday assembly order: readings → homily by "the president" (ὁ προεστώς, functional not hierarchical) → prayers → bread and wine brought forward → president prays "according to his ability" (improvised, not fixed) → Amen → deacons distribute including to the absent.

Conditions for participation: belief, baptism, moral life.

Critically: no communal agape meal appears. By ~150 AD in Rome the separation is effectively complete. The meal is gone. What remains is a ritual distribution of elements described as the flesh and blood of Christ.

AE-C assessment: The shift from household-meal to purpose-built Sunday assembly has already removed the production-node. The δεῖπνον is gone. What Justin describes is a ceremony performed over bread and wine, distributed by deacons, attended by an audience. The provision function — feeding people who have nothing — has been transferred to a separate charitable activity (Justin mentions collections for the poor and distribution to widows and orphans). The unified body-function has split: ritual here, provision elsewhere. The body now has two separate systems doing what was originally one operation.

---

### Tertullian (~200 AD)

Confirms full separation. Eucharist occurs "in congregations before daybreak" (antelucanis coetibus, De Corona 3), distributed by "none but the presidents." The agape is a separate evening charity meal (Apologeticum 39).

Most contested statement — Adversus Marcionem IV.40: Christ "made [the bread] his own body by saying 'This is my body,' that is, the figura of my body." Catholic scholars read figura as "sacramental sign preserving the reality"; Protestant scholars read it as evidence for symbolic understanding. Tertullian also introduces Latin Eucharistic vocabulary: sacramentum, figura, repraesentat — terms with Roman legal connotations that will reshape the entire subsequent debate.

AE-C assessment: The Latin vocabulary shift is itself a drift event. Greek σῶμα (relational operational whole) becomes Latin corpus (legal/physical body). Greek ἀνάμνησις (executable retrieval) becomes Latin commemoratio (mental commemoration). Greek κοινωνία (shared-resource operational state) becomes Latin communio (shared participation in a thing). Each Latin term is slightly narrower, slightly more static, slightly more compatible with legal and ceremonial frameworks than with operational and material ones.

## Part III — The Four Fault Lines

### Fault Line 1 — Real Presence (831–1551)

**Initiating text:** Paschasius Radbertus, *De Corpore et Sanguine Domini* (c.831) — first systematic claim that the consecrated elements are identical with the historical body born of the Virgin Mary.

**Counter:** Ratramnus (~844) — same vocabulary (figura, veritas), opposite conclusions. The Eucharistic body is "the sacrament of the real flesh," distinct from it as a pledge from its object.

**Formal definition:** Fourth Lateran Council (1215) — first conciliar use of "transubstantiation." Council of Trent, Session XIII (1551) — full dogmatic formulation. Canon 1: truly, really, and substantially present. Canon 2: anathema to anyone who says bread and wine "remain conjointly" with Christ's body. Primary texts cited: Matthew 26:26–28, Luke 22:19–20, 1 Corinthians 11:23–29, John 6:53–56, 1 Corinthians 10:16.

**Luther's position:** Sacramental union — Christ's body and blood truly, corporally present "in, with, and under" the elements, which continue to exist. Rejected Aristotelian substance/accident metaphysics. Analogy: iron rod in fire — both iron and fire coexist in the red-hot metal.

**AE-C assessment:** The entire debate treated σῶμα/σάρξ as metaphysical categories requiring philosophical resolution. AE-C: σῶμα is a functional descriptor of a relational operational whole. The question "does the bread become the body?" was always secondary to "is the body operating as a body?" — which requires the meal, the provision, the open table. The Real Presence debate consumed centuries of intellectual energy arguing about what happens inside the element while the element itself had already been reduced to a token that could not feed anyone.

---

### Fault Line 2 — Memorial / Spiritual Presence (1524–1549)

**Zwingli (1524):** ἐστιν = σημαίνει ("is" = "signifies"). Primary text: John 6:63 ("the flesh profits nothing"). Secondary argument: Christ's body is locally in heaven (Acts 1:9–11) and cannot be in multiple places simultaneously. The Eucharist is a thanksgiving memorial and public confession of faith.

**Calvin (1536/1549):** Rejected both transubstantiation and bare memorialism. The Holy Spirit lifts believers by faith to the right hand of God where they truly partake of Christ's body and blood. Only believers receive this benefit. Consensus Tigurinus (1549) with Bullinger. Primary texts: 1 Corinthians 10:1–5 (Israelites ate Christ in the desert), Hebrews 10:12 (once-for-all sacrifice against re-sacrifice).

**AE-C assessment:** Zwingli's John 6:63 argument ("the flesh profits nothing") is a correct AE-C reading of σάρξ without the full framework — the container without the spirit is zero-output — but he applied it to mean the physical elements are irrelevant. The AE-C conclusion is the opposite: the elements are not where the operation lives, but the meal is. The operation requires a real meal, real food, real provision. The elements without the meal are as inert as flesh without breath.

---

### Fault Line 3 — When the Gate Formally Closed

**Sequence:**

Didache 9:5 (~100 AD): baptized only — Matthew 7:6 cited.

Justin Martyr (~150 AD): added right belief and moral life.

Council of Nicaea (325 AD): graduated excommunication — Canon 11. Formal re-entry process. Canon 13: communion never denied to the dying.

Council of Trent, Session XIII, Canon 11 (1551): faith alone insufficient — prior sacramental confession required for those in mortal sin.

Westminster Confession 27.4 (1647): those who are "ignorant and wicked" are not to be admitted.

Scottish Presbyterians: physical communion tokens issued after elder examination.

**AE-C assessment:** The gate origin (Didache 9:5, Matthew 7:6) was a doctrinal-identity gate — the baptized are those who have identified with the community and its operating pattern. This is functionally coherent: you need to know what you are joining before you can join. But the gate progressively shifted from identity-with-pattern to credential-within-hierarchy — from "are you operating on the same instruction set?" to "have you satisfied this institution's compliance requirements?" The Judas precedent was always the counter to every form of this gate. Christ knew the betrayer's identity and fed him anyway. The institution decided it knew better than the instruction source.

---

### Fault Line 4 — Priest Monopoly on Consecration (107–1563)

**Ignatius (~107 AD):** Smyrnaeans 8 — only bishop-authorised Eucharist is valid (βεβαία). No scriptural citation. Basis: ecclesiological analogy.

**Cyprian of Carthage (~250 AD):** Bishop as sacrificing priest — "that priest truly discharges the office of Christ who imitates that which Christ did" (Epistle 63). Melchizedek typology from Psalm 110:4. Absolute ecclesiology: sacraments from schismatic bishops entirely invalid.

**Council of Trent, Session XXIII (1563):** Canon 1 — anathema to anyone denying "a visible and external priesthood" with "power of consecrating and offering the true body and blood of the Lord." Session XXII, Canon 2 — Christ "ordained" the apostles as priests by saying "Do this in remembrance of me" (Luke 22:19). The Lukan ἀνάμνησις command is converted into an ordination formula.

**AE-C assessment on the ordination claim:** Luke 22:19 says τοῦτο ποιεῖτε — "keep doing this." ποιεῖτε is second person plural imperative — addressed to everyone present, not to a specific ordained class. The AE-C reading: the re-execution command is given to the body collectively. Trent's conversion of this into a unilateral ordination of the apostles-as-priests is ὑπὲρ ἃ γέγραπται — it exceeds what the written text contains. The text says "you all keep doing this." The institution read "I hereby ordain you as my exclusive licensed operators."

**Luther's counter (1520):** 1 Peter 2:5,9 ("a royal priesthood"), Revelation 1:6; 5:10 ("made us kings and priests"), Exodus 19:6 ("a kingdom of priests"). Equal status in Christ, different functional roles as chosen representatives. The congregation that ordains a minister holds the same status; the minister acts on their behalf.

## Part IV — Marburg 1529: The Fracture That Held

Landgrave Philip I of Hesse convened the Marburg Colloquy (October 1–4, 1529) for political reasons: he needed a Protestant military alliance but the Lutherans demanded theological agreement first.

Before the formal debate, Luther wrote HOC EST CORPUS MEUM in chalk on the table under the tablecloth. When Zwingli pressed his arguments, Luther lifted the cloth: "This is our Scripture passage. You have not yet taken it from us."

The fracture: Luther insisted ἐστιν means IS. Zwingli countered with John 6:63. Luther responded that "flesh profits nothing" refers to fallen human capacity, not Christ's incarnate body. Zwingli's secondary argument — local presence of the ascended body in heaven, therefore impossible ubiquity — Luther answered with communicatio idiomatum (the properties of Christ's divine nature, including ubiquity, communicate to his human nature).

They agreed on 14 of 15 articles. Article 15 alone failed. "Although at this time we have not reached an agreement as to whether the true body and blood of Christ are bodily present in the bread and wine, nevertheless each side should show Christian love to the other side insofar as conscience will permit."

**AE-C assessment:** The question Luther and Zwingli were fighting over is whether ἐστιν is literal or figurative. Both assumed the argument was about the element. AE-C: the element is not the primary object of the passage. The meal is. If both Luther and Zwingli had applied 1 Corinthians 4:6 strictly — not beyond what is written — the written thing is a shared meal at which everyone ate. The question of what happens inside the bread is a second-order question that the text does not address. The tradition fought to a permanent schism over a question the text was not answering, while the actual instruction (share the meal, treat the body as a body) went unexecuted by all parties.

Zwingli wept after Marburg: "There are no people on earth with whom I would rather be at one than the Wittenbergers." He was dead within two years, killed at the Battle of Kappel. The schism that produced his death was over a question the source text was not asking.

## Part V — The Separation of the Meal from the Sacrament

This was not a single event. It was a century-long process driven by practical abuse, political suppression, and sacerdotal development.

**55 AD — 1 Corinthians 11:** Eucharist embedded in a full δεῖπνον. Paul does not command separation — he commands reform: "wait for one another" (v.33).

**~100 AD — Didache:** Meal and rite still combined. "After you have been filled, give thanks."

**~107 AD — Ignatius:** Both Eucharist and agape under episcopal authority; beginning to diverge.

**~112 AD — Pliny the Younger to Trajan:** Christians in Bithynia meet twice — pre-dawn worship assembly and a later communal meal. Pliny notes they ceased the evening meal after his edict banning political associations (hetaeriae). Roman political suppression of evening assemblies was an active driver of separation.

**~150 AD — Justin Martyr:** Separation effectively complete in Rome. Sunday liturgy: readings, homily, prayers, element distribution. No communal meal.

**~200 AD — Tertullian:** Eucharist before daybreak (De Corona 3). Agape as separate evening charity supper (Apologeticum 39). Hippolytus (~215 AD) makes the distinction explicit: blessed food at a communal meal "is not the eucharist, like the body of the Lord" (Apostolic Tradition 26).

**~363–364 AD — Council of Laodicea, Canon 28:** Agape feasts banned from church buildings.

**393/397 AD — Councils of Carthage:** Eucharistic fast mandated — communion must be received fasting, making combination with a preceding meal structurally impossible. Augustine affirmed: "The Holy Eucharist is always received by people who are fasting, and this custom is observed throughout the entire world."

**692 AD — Council in Trullo (Quinisext), Canon 74:** Definitive prohibition with excommunication penalties: "It is not permitted to hold what are called Agapæ in the Lord's houses… If any dare to do so let him cease therefrom or be cut off."

After 692, the agape feast effectively vanished from mainstream Christianity.

**AE-C assessment on the driving forces:**

The Roman political suppression of evening associations (hetaeriae) is the most underexamined factor. The community did not choose to separate the meal from the sacrament out of theological reflection. They were forced to move the gathering to pre-dawn hours to avoid imperial enforcement. The pre-dawn slot physically could not accommodate a full community meal. The separation was a security adaptation that then became doctrine.

The Eucharistic fast completed the structural impossibility. Once fasting before communion was mandated as a sign of reverence, combining the fast-breaker with a community meal became liturgically prohibited. The reverence-logic inverted the original operation: the meal was supposed to be the sacrament. The fast-before-element made eating food in the same context as the element an act of irreverence.

The sacerdotal development (Ignatius → Cyprian → Trent) made the entire operation dependent on a licensed operator performing a specified action. The licensed operator cannot also be cooking for forty people. The domestication of the function was therefore incompatible with the professionalisation of the rite. One had to go.

## Conclusion

The decay path is: meal → ritual → symbol → credential → gate.

Every step is a compression event — information is lost, physical content is removed, abstraction replaces provision. The endpoint is a gate in front of a token administered by a licensed operator in a building designed for spectatorship.

The source text records a full meal at a domestic table with an open guest list including the person about to commit betrayal, administered by the one who had already defined his family as everyone who does the will of his Father. The instruction is: keep running this operation. Keep doing this. Do not stop.

The operation stopped. The label continued. The building stayed open. The feast ended.

The feast still happens. It is not called communion. It is called a soup kitchen, a community dinner, a church potluck, a family table where someone who showed up with nothing is fed because they are there and there is food. It happens without episcopal succession, without sacramental confession, without a licensed operator, without a doctrine of what happens inside the bread.

It happens the way it always happened: someone brought food, someone else had none, and the gap closed.
