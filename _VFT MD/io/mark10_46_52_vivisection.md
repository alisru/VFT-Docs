Καὶ ἀκούσας ὅτι Ἰησοῦς ὁ Ναζαρηνός ἐστιν ἤρξατο κράζειν καὶ λέγειν· Υἱὲ Δαυίδ, Ἰησοῦ, ἐλέησόν με. Καὶ ἐπετίμων αὐτῷ πολλοὶ ἵνα σιωπήσῃ· ὁ δὲ πολλῷ μᾶλλον ἔκραζεν· Υἱὲ Δαυίδ, ἐλέησόν με. Καὶ στὰς ὁ Ἰησοῦς εἶπεν· Φωνήσατε αὐτόν. Καὶ φωνοῦσιν τὸν τυφλὸν λέγοντες αὐτῷ· Θάρσει, ἔγειρε, φωνεῖ σε. Ὁ δὲ ἀποβαλὼν τὸ ἱμάτιον αὐτοῦ ἀναπηδήσας ἦλθεν πρὸς τὸν Ἰησοῦν. Καὶ ἀποκριθεὶς αὐτῷ ὁ Ἰησοῦς εἶπεν· Τί σοι θέλεις ποιήσω; ὁ δὲ τυφλὸς εἶπεν αὐτῷ· Ῥαββουνί, ἵνα ἀναβλέψω. Καὶ ὁ Ἰησοῦς εἶπεν αὐτῷ· Ὕπαγε, ἡ πίστις σου σέσωκέν σε. Καὶ εὐθὺς ἀνέβλεψεν καὶ ἠκολούθει αὐτῷ ἐν τῇ ὁδῷ.

**Standard translation (GW):**
They came to Jericho. As Jesus, his disciples, and a large crowd were leaving Jericho, a blind beggar named Bartimaeus, the son of Timaeus, was sitting by the road. When he heard that Jesus from Nazareth was passing by, he began to shout, "Jesus, Son of David, have mercy on me!" Many people scolded him and told him to be quiet. But he shouted even louder, "Son of David, have mercy on me!" Jesus stopped and said, "Call him." They called the blind man and said, "Cheer up! Get up! He's calling you." The blind man threw off his coat, jumped up, and came to Jesus. Jesus asked him, "What do you want me to do for you?" The blind man said, "Teacher, I want to see again." Jesus told him, "Go, your faith has saved you." Immediately he could see again, and he followed Jesus on the road.

**Structural note — people are blind to their own words:**

The passage contains one of the sharpest perceptual inversions in the synoptics. The sighted crowd cannot output υἱὲ Δαυίδ. They suppress the one who can. The blind man is the only observer in the scene whose recognition algorithm is running correctly — and he is running it correctly before any physical evidence is available to him, under active social suppression, twice.

This is the Bartimaeus diagnostic: the words ran ahead of the frame. His mouth knew what his eyes had not confirmed and what the crowd's eyes, despite being functional, had not produced. The crowd's physical sight was generating no useful output. His physical blindness was generating the most structurally precise output available.

Jesus does not heal him and then ask him to recognise. He asks him to articulate what his words have already demonstrated. τί σοι θέλεις ποιήσω — what do you want me to do for you — to a blind man, is not a diagnostic question. It is a mirror. Jesus holds up Bartimaeus's own output and asks him to see it consciously. The ἀναβλέψω — the return to seeing — is not the acquisition of a new capacity. It is the physical instrument being brought into alignment with the recognition that was already structurally complete.

ἡ πίστις σου σέσωκέν σε — your πίστις did this — is Jesus pointing entirely away from himself and back at the structure Bartimaeus already embodied. The bilateral man could not produce this result because the bilateral man cannot generate πίστις — the committed, undivided, physically demonstrated trust that throws the coat, leaps, and comes. Bartimaeus had resolved the AND construction before he arrived. He was one thing. That is the only mechanism by which the healing could operate.

The crowd watched it happen and were blind to it. They were sighted throughout.
