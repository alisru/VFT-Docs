The intuition is simpler than the math suggests.
Take the political compass — most people have seen it. Now instead of asking "where do you sit on economics," ask one question instead: 
"who does this idea actually benefit?" That single swap turns an economics map into a moral one. The horizontal axis goes from "everyone" on one side to "only me" on the other.
 The vertical axis goes from "actively doing something about it" at the top to "doing nothing, or actively preventing it" at the bottom.
 
Every idea, policy, system, or argument has a position on that map. Not just a position — a direction it's moving and a speed.
This is where the physics comes in. Lorentz transformation equations are how you calculate velocity relative to a fixed point in 
spacetime — when two objects are moving, you can calculate exactly when and where they'll intersect. The same operation works here.
You pick a fixed point on the moral map — say, maximum extraction: "only me, destroy everything to get it" — and you calculate how fast 
any given system is moving toward it, and when it arrives.
The map has six known fixed points — attractors, like gravity wells. Greatest good, lesser good, neutral-but-selfless, neutral-but-selfish, 
lesser evil, greatest evil. From any position on the map, you can calculate the velocity toward all six simultaneously — 
including the distance and bearing from the neutral origin (0,0) to every attractor at once. That gives you the full gravitational picture: 
not just "is this heading toward evil" but which evil, how fast, and what's pulling it sideways along the way.
The geometry of a healthy system is a circle — action produces effect, effect feeds back into the next action, the loop closes. 
When a system starts moving toward extraction, that circle flattens into an oval. The more it flattens, the closer it is to the attractor. 
At maximum extraction velocity the oval becomes a straight line — no loop, no feedback, just projection. Output becomes undefined. The door closes.
You only actually need two things to use this: a floor (what's objectively real — the actual situation, not an ideal) and at least one known fixed point to measure distance from. 
The floor is what stops the frame from floating. The fixed point is what gives direction meaning.
Without a floor, anyone can say "in an ideal world..." and build an imaginary box with no ground — inside that box, "the best price is whatever they have times infinitely more" 
is internally consistent. The formula just makes visible what's actually happening: the floor is gone, the frame is floating, the output is already undefined. 
The oval broke before you even started.
It's modelling how people implicitly value things when they're trying to be fair — and showing exactly where and how fast they stop being fair.
